# Building a Succession Pipeline for a Scaling Organization

## Context

You are an HR Manager at a 220-person fintech company that has grown from 90 to 220 employees in two years. The CEO just asked, in a board prep meeting, "What happens if our VP of Engineering gets poached tomorrow?" — and nobody in the room had an answer. The Head of Sales separately flagged that two of her strongest regional managers seem disengaged and may be flight risks. You have performance review data in your HRIS and an engagement survey from last quarter, but no succession plan, no defined high-potential pool, and no career framework beyond a basic title ladder.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "We have no succession plan at all and the board is starting to ask hard questions. Where do I start and what should I prioritize first?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a direct board-level trigger, an identified single point of failure, and early flight-risk signals on your sales team — start with two priority areas:

**Critical role coverage (board's concern):**

- Identify the 8–12 roles whose sudden departure would materially disrupt the business
- For each, assess whether a "ready now" internal successor exists
- Flag any role with zero viable internal successors as a critical gap

The VP of Engineering question is not really about one person — it is a proxy for whether the organization has any succession discipline at all. You need an answer for that role specifically, and a credible plan for the rest of the critical list.

**Flight-risk triage (Sales' concern):**

- Pull engagement scores, tenure, and recent performance ratings for the two regional managers
- Run brief stay interviews before assuming the cause
- Determine whether this is a compensation issue, a growth-path issue, or a manager relationship issue

> **Avoid:** Do not try to build a full 9-box talent review across the entire 220-person company in month one. Start with the critical-role list and the flagged flight-risk cases, prove the process works, then expand to a full talent review next quarter.

## Step 2 — Mapping Critical Roles and Readiness

**Sample prompt:**

> "I have a list of 10 critical roles. For each, help me figure out how to classify successor readiness."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Readiness tiers to apply to each critical role:**

```text
Ready Now      → Could step into the role within 0–3 months with minimal support
Ready 1–2 Yrs  → Has the trajectory but needs targeted development first
Ready 3+ Yrs   → Long-term potential identified, early-stage development
No Successor   → No internal candidate identified — critical gap
```

**Applying this to the VP of Engineering role:**

```text
Candidate: Senior Engineering Manager (3 years tenure, owns largest platform team)
Performance: Consistently exceeds expectations, last 2 cycles
Gaps: No experience managing managers across multiple teams,
      limited exposure to board-level reporting
Classification: Ready in 1–2 years, with a structured stretch assignment
```

**Coverage ratio across the 10 critical roles:**

```text
3 roles — Ready Now
4 roles — Ready in 1–2 Years
2 roles — Ready in 3+ Years
1 role  — No Successor Identified (critical gap)

Coverage ratio: 70% have at least a developing successor; 1 role requires
immediate action (external bench-building or accelerated internal development)
```

**Benchmark context:**

Korn Ferry research recommends at least two viable successors per critical leadership role to maintain resilience. At present, your 10 critical roles average fewer than one developing successor each, and one role has none. This is a reasonable starting baseline for a fast-scaling company, but the board will expect to see the "no successor" gap closed within two quarters.

> **Business impact estimate:** An unplanned VP-level departure with no internal successor typically results in 4–6 months of external search time, a 6–12 month ramp period for an external hire, and a measurable dip in team retention during the vacancy. For a function the size of Engineering at your stage, this represents meaningful execution risk during the gap, not just a hiring-cost line item.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Flight-Risk Signals

**Sample prompt:**

> "Two of our top regional sales managers seem disengaged. I need to understand why before this becomes a resignation. What should I be looking at?"

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming the cause, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Engagement and pulse data:**

- Have their individual engagement scores dropped over the last 2–3 survey cycles?
- Are specific themes showing up — career growth, manager relationship, recognition, workload?
- How does their score compare to their peer group and team average?

Common root causes for high performers at growth-stage companies: unclear path to the next level, feeling under-compensated relative to market or internal peers, a sense that growth has stalled while the company scaled around them, and burnout from carrying disproportionate account load.

**2. Tenure and trajectory pattern:**

- How long have they been in their current role without a title or scope change?
- Is their performance rating trajectory flat, improving, or declining?
- Have they applied for or expressed interest in other internal roles that did not materialize?

**3. Manager and team correlation:**

- Has their direct manager changed recently, or has reporting structure shifted?
- Are other strong performers on the same team also showing early disengagement signals?
- What is the manager's own span of control and bandwidth to coach top performers?

**4. Compensation and market positioning:**

- When was their compensation last benchmarked against market data for their role and level?
- Do they have visibility into what advancement would mean for their pay and scope?
- Is there an equity or incentive structure that rewards continued tenure at this stage?

> **What to bring to the conversation:** Do not open with "are you thinking of leaving." Run a structured stay interview focused on what is working, what is frustrating, and what would make the next two years here compelling. Pair that qualitative input with the engagement trend data before deciding on intervention.

## Step 4 — Running a 9-Box Talent Review

**Sample prompt:**

> "I want to run a 9-box talent review for our top 30 people in Sales and Engineering. I have never facilitated one. Walk me through how to set it up."

**Skill response — 9-box facilitation structure:**

Design the session around two axes — performance (horizontal) and potential (vertical) — each rated low, moderate, or high, producing nine placement boxes.

**Pre-work before the session:**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Each manager submits a draft performance rating per person, based on the last 2 review cycles |
| 2 | Each manager submits a draft potential rating, based on learning agility, scope readiness, and leadership behaviors — explicitly separate from performance |
| 3 | HR compiles draft placements into a single grid before the session, anonymized by department |
| 4 | A facilitation guide is shared in advance defining what "potential" means, to prevent it collapsing into "is currently a top performer" |

**During the calibration session:**

- Walk through one quadrant at a time, starting with the top-right (high performance, high potential) box
- For each name, the sponsoring manager gives one specific example supporting the placement
- Other managers can challenge a placement, but only with specific behavioral evidence, not general impressions
- Flag any name where managers strongly disagree for a follow-up 1-on-1 review outside the group session

**Resulting grid for this cohort (30 people):**

```text
High Potential / High Performance   →  4 people  (core succession pool)
High Potential / Moderate Performance → 5 people (development priority)
Moderate Potential / High Performance → 9 people (strong specialists, retain)
Low Potential / High Performance     →  6 people (valued contributors, not pipeline)
All other combinations               →  6 people (monitor or address)
```

> **Common facilitation mistake:** Letting "high performance" silently become a proxy for "high potential." A strong individual contributor who consistently exceeds targets may have no interest in or aptitude for managing people — that does not make them low value, it makes them a different kind of talent investment.

## Step 5 — Presenting Succession Coverage to the Board

**Sample prompt:**

> "The board wants a succession update at the next meeting. I have the data but I don't know how to frame it without sounding alarmist or sounding like everything is fine."

**Skill response — board communication framework:**

Boards think in risk exposure and trend direction, not individual employee stories. Structure your update in three parts:

**Part 1 — State the coverage position with data:**

"Across our 10 board-critical roles, 3 have a ready-now internal successor, 6 have a successor in active development on a 1–3 year timeline, and 1 — VP of Engineering — currently has no internal successor, though we have identified and begun developing a candidate on an accelerated 12–18 month track."

**Part 2 — Contextualize against company stage:**

"For a company at our growth stage, having gaps in succession coverage is expected — we have scaled headcount faster than we have built leadership depth. Our coverage today is a reasonable starting point, not a finished state. The plan is to close the highest-risk gap within two quarters and reach two viable successors for every board-critical role within 12 months."

**Part 3 — Flag the real risk and the mitigation in progress:**

"The most time-sensitive risk is the VP of Engineering role. We are mitigating this two ways: accelerating internal development for our identified successor through a structured stretch assignment, and maintaining light external bench awareness in case the timeline needs to compress. I will report progress on this specific gap at every board meeting until it is closed."

> **Key principle:** Boards respond to defined risk, a credible timeline, and visible ownership. Avoid presenting succession as either a solved problem or an open-ended worry — frame it as a managed risk with a closing date.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Analyze Talent and Engagement Data

**Sample prompt:**

> "I have performance ratings, tenure, and engagement scores for 220 employees in a spreadsheet. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to surface flight risk and succession candidates without being a data analyst?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted analysis workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your data:**

Export to CSV or paste directly into the chat. Include columns for: employee ID (anonymized), department, role level, tenure, last two performance ratings, most recent engagement score and trend, and manager ID.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```text
I am an HR Manager at a 220-person fintech company.
Here is my current workforce data:
[paste your data here]

Please:
1. Identify employees with high performance ratings combined with
   a declining engagement trend over the last two survey cycles
2. Flag any concentration of flight-risk signals under a specific
   manager or department
3. Identify employees who may be under-leveled relative to their
   tenure and performance trajectory — possible internal mobility
   or promotion candidates
4. Compare engagement trends to these benchmarks:
   - Global employee engagement: 20% (Gallup 2026)
   - High performer voluntary turnover benchmark: under 10% annually (SHRM)
5. Summarize the top 2–3 talent risks in plain language suitable
   for a leadership briefing
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with action planning:**

```text
Based on this analysis, draft a 90-day retention and development plan
for the highest-risk high performers identified. Focus on interventions
that do not require a compensation cycle to implement.
Format as: Action / Owner / Timeline / Success Metric.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Cross-referencing performance, tenure, and engagement signals quickly
- Surfacing patterns across a large employee population
- Translating data into narrative summaries for leadership
- Generating draft retention and development action plans

**What AI cannot replace:**
- The judgment of a manager who knows the person's actual circumstances
- Reading the tone and trust level in a stay interview conversation
- Final calibration decisions that carry political and team-structure implications

## Step 7 — Designing a Career Path to Support Retention

**Sample prompt:**

> "Part of our flight-risk problem seems to be that people don't see a clear path forward. We only have basic titles. How do I build something better?"

**Skill response — career architecture build:**

Design career paths around three components: levels, expectations, and transition criteria — for each major role family.

**Levels and expectations:**

| Level | Scope | Example Expectation Shift |
|-------|-------|---------------------------|
| Individual Contributor (IC1–IC3) | Own tasks and projects with increasing independence | IC1: executes defined work → IC3: owns ambiguous problems end-to-end |
| Senior / Staff IC (IC4–IC5) | Influence beyond own team without formal authority | Sets technical or functional direction across multiple teams |
| Manager (M1–M2) | Owns people outcomes for a single team | Manages performance, hiring, and team execution |
| Senior Manager / Director | Owns outcomes across multiple teams or a function | Translates strategy into multi-team execution plans |

**Transition criteria (example — Sales role family, Account Executive to Senior AE):**

- Sustained quota attainment above 100% for 3 consecutive quarters
- Demonstrated ability to manage complex, multi-stakeholder deal cycles
- At least one example of mentoring a newer team member to a successful close
- Manager and one cross-functional peer both support the transition

**Implementation notes:**

- Publish the framework so employees and managers can self-assess against it, rather than keeping it as an internal HR document
- Pair the framework with a quarterly career conversation guide for managers, separate from the performance review
- Track internal fill rate and lateral movement rate as the success metrics for whether the framework is actually being used

> **Common finding at fast-growing companies:** The retention problem is often not compensation — it is the absence of a visible, credible next step. A well-communicated career framework, even without immediate promotions, materially improves engagement scores tied to "growth opportunity" within one to two survey cycles.

## Full Talent Management Workflow Summary

```text
Identify critical roles and business-driven trigger (board, leadership gap, flight risk)
                    ↓
Classify successor readiness for each critical role
                    ↓
Run stay interviews and diagnostics on flagged flight-risk employees
                    ↓
Facilitate a 9-box talent review to calibrate performance vs potential
                    ↓
Build or refine career architecture to support visible growth paths
                    ↓
Use AI to analyze performance and engagement data for hidden risk
                    ↓
Present to leadership and the board with risk framing and a closing timeline
                    ↓
Review and refresh the succession pipeline every quarter as roles and people change
```

### Common Talent Management Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Updating the succession plan once a year and forgetting it | Build a quarterly refresh cadence tied to the talent review calendar |
| Treating "high performer" and "high potential" as the same thing | Calibrate the two dimensions separately using a 9-box or equivalent framework |
| Assigning HiPo status without any development action | Pair every HiPo and successor designation with a specific stretch assignment or owner |
| Presenting succession data to the board as either solved or hopeless | Frame every gap as a managed risk with a defined closing timeline |
| Running engagement surveys but never communicating action back to employees | Close the loop publicly after every survey cycle, even with partial actions |
| Building a career framework only HR can interpret | Publish it in plain language so employees and managers can self-assess without HR mediation |
| Assuming flight risk is always a compensation problem | Run a structured stay interview before assuming the root cause; growth-path and manager issues are equally common |
