# Building Talent Market Maps

## Overview

A talent market map is a structured view of where a specific type of talent sits — which companies employ it, roughly how many people hold the target profile, and how talent tends to move between employers. It's the research foundation that makes sourcing and executive search efficient, done once at the start of a search rather than rediscovered ad hoc with every individual outreach attempt.

## Defining the Search Scope

A market map needs a precisely defined target profile before research begins — an under-specified scope produces a map too broad to be actionable:

- Define the target role, seniority level, and required capability specifically enough that a researcher could recognize a matching profile without ambiguity
- Define the relevant geography and any constraints (visa status, remote eligibility, language requirements) that affect which candidates are genuinely addressable
- Clarify whether the map should include only directly comparable roles at target companies, or also adjacent roles where transferable capability exists — this materially changes the map's scope and size

## Identifying Target Companies

- Start from companies with a genuine talent overlap with the hiring organization — competitors, adjacent-industry companies with similar functional needs, and companies known for strong capability in the target domain
- Segment target companies by tier — companies where the target talent is most concentrated and highest-fit warrant deeper research than a long tail of lower-probability sources
- Revisit the target company list periodically for searches that run over an extended period, since company landscapes shift (acquisitions, layoffs, strategic pivots) in ways that open or close talent pools

## Researching Org Structure at Target Companies

- Use public sources (company websites, professional network profiles, press coverage) to build an approximate picture of relevant team structure and reporting lines at target companies, without overstating confidence in inferred details that aren't confirmed
- Focus research effort on the specific function or team most likely to hold the target profile, rather than mapping an entire target company's org structure indiscriminately
- Keep research notes organized by company and role for reuse across future searches with similar profiles, rather than starting from scratch each time

## Sizing the Addressable Market

- Estimate market size using available public signal (professional network member counts for a given title and geography, job posting volume, industry reports) while being explicit about the estimate's uncertainty
- Distinguish total market size from realistically addressable market size — the latter accounts for factors like compensation competitiveness, visa constraints, or relocation willingness that meaningfully shrink the practical talent pool
- Use market sizing to calibrate search strategy expectations early — a genuinely small addressable market signals the need for a different approach (broader geography, adjacent profile, build-instead-of-buy) before significant sourcing effort is invested

## Tracking Talent Movement Patterns

- Note where target-profile talent typically moves from and to across career transitions — this often reveals under-considered source companies or reveals that a target company is a common exit point rather than a strong retention environment
- Use movement pattern data to inform timing and messaging for outreach — understanding common transition triggers for a given profile helps calibrate both when and how to approach passive candidates
- Update movement pattern observations as new data comes in through ongoing search work, treating the map as a living resource rather than a one-time static document

## From Map to Search Execution

A market map that isn't translated into a prioritized outreach plan doesn't deliver its value:

1. Prioritize target companies and named contacts by fit and addressability, not just company prestige
2. Hand off the map to sourcing or search execution with clear context on why each source was prioritized, so outreach can be tailored appropriately
3. Update the map with what's learned during actual outreach — response rates, willingness to move, unexpected sources of strong candidates — so it improves for future related searches

## Common Pitfalls

- **Researching too broadly without a precisely defined target profile**, producing a map too diffuse to prioritize effectively.
- **Treating the map as a one-time deliverable** rather than a living resource updated as the search progresses.
- **Overstating confidence in inferred org structure** drawn from limited public sources, which can misdirect outreach effort.
