# Conducting a market mapping exercise

## Context

A talent acquisition lead is opening a VP of Engineering search for a Series C climate-tech company. The hiring manager wants to restrict sourcing to direct competitors, but the market is thin. The recruiter needs to build a defensible market map that expands the target universe beyond obvious competitors and presents findings in a way that earns the hiring manager's buy-in.

## Step 1: Build the target company list

Sample prompt: "Build a talent market map for VP of Engineering candidates across climate-tech, clean energy, and adjacent deep-tech industries in North America — identify 20 target companies and explain why each is a good source."

Expected response: A prioritized list of companies organized by tier:

- Tier 1 (direct): companies building similar products with comparable engineering complexity and team size
- Tier 2 (adjacent): climate-adjacent companies (energy storage, grid software, industrial IoT) where engineering leadership skills transfer directly
- Tier 3 (feeder): larger tech companies (FAANG and similar) with sustainability engineering divisions where candidates may be motivated to move to mission-driven work

Each entry should include: company name, estimated engineering org size, why it's a good source, and any known reasons to exclude.

## Step 2: Size the addressable market

Sample prompt: "Estimate the addressable market size for VP of Engineering candidates with hardware/software systems experience in climate-tech or adjacent industries in North America."

Expected response: An estimate with methodology — not an exact number, but a structured approach using LinkedIn population data, title variations considered, geography scope, and a realism check on what % of that population is actually approachable (not locked by equity, non-competes, or other constraints).

## Step 3: Add competitive intelligence

Sample prompt: "Compare the talent value propositions of [Competitor A] and [Competitor B] for a VP of Engineering — compensation positioning, brand strength, growth trajectory, and attrition signals."

Expected response: A side-by-side comparison noting publicly known compensation positioning (from job posting data and compensation databases), employee review sentiment, recent growth or contraction signals from news and hiring activity, and any recent departures visible on LinkedIn.

## Step 4: Present to the hiring manager

Sample prompt: "Summarize this market map into a one-page briefing for the hiring manager, explaining what it means for timeline, comp positioning, and outreach strategy."

Expected response: A structured one-pager covering:

- Market summary: addressable pool, scarcity or availability signals
- Compensation: market range and where the company's offer sits
- Source mix recommendation: why expanding beyond direct competitors increases chances of success without lowering the bar
- Timeline implication: realistic time-to-close given pool size and expected response rates

## Workflow summary

Use `hr-market-mapping` to build a structured, evidence-based map that expands the sourcing universe beyond obvious targets, prices the market accurately, and arms the hiring manager with data needed to make good decisions about scope, compensation, and timeline before sourcing begins.
