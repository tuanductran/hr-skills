# HR as Internal Consultant

## Overview

Effective HR business partners operate as internal consultants — diagnosing organizational problems, developing evidence-based recommendations, and influencing stakeholders who have no obligation to follow their advice. This is a distinct skill set from HR expertise, and it is the capability most commonly underdeveloped in HR professionals who are technically strong but struggle to create impact at the senior level.

## The Consulting Mindset

The shift from HR specialist to internal consultant requires a change in how problems are framed:

**Specialist mindset:** "The manager asked me to run a team-building workshop. I'll design the best workshop I can."

**Consultant mindset:** "The manager is asking for a team-building workshop. What problem are they actually trying to solve? Is a workshop the right intervention? What would I need to understand before recommending an approach?"

Internal consulting is about solving the right problem well, not delivering what was requested if the request is not the right solution.

### Diagnostic before prescription

The most common consulting failure is prescribing a solution before diagnosing the problem. This happens because:
- Clients (managers and leaders) often come with a solution already in mind ("I need a training program")
- It feels helpful to move immediately to solutions
- Diagnosis takes time that both parties feel is being wasted

A good diagnostic question to use at the start of any significant request: "Before I think about what to recommend, help me understand the problem. What are you seeing that made you think something needs to change?"

## Problem Diagnosis

### Types of organizational problems

Not all organizational problems have the same root cause, and therefore not the same solution.

**Skill gaps:** People lack the knowledge or capability to perform the required task. Solution: training, coaching, structured development.

**Motivation or engagement problems:** People have the capability but are not applying it consistently. Solution: understand what is driving low motivation — clarity, recognition, relationship with manager, misalignment with role, burnout.

**Process or system problems:** The way work is organized, the tools available, or the process flow is preventing performance. Training or development will not fix a process problem.

**Structural problems:** Reporting relationships, decision rights, or organizational design is creating friction or misalignment. Cannot be solved by changing individual behavior.

**Strategy or clarity problems:** People are not aligned on what they are trying to achieve, or the goals are unclear, conflicting, or changing faster than the organization can adapt.

Diagnostic questions to distinguish these:
- "Has this person or team ever performed well at this? What was different then?"
- "Does this problem occur in some parts of the organization but not others? What is different about the contexts where it works?"
- "If we gave this person twice as much support, would the problem be solved?"
- "Is this an individual issue or does it affect everyone in similar roles?"

### Data for diagnosis

A diagnosis based on one stakeholder's perception is not a diagnosis — it is one data point. A robust diagnosis draws from multiple sources:

- Quantitative data (performance metrics, attrition data, engagement survey results)
- Multiple perspectives (the manager's view, the team's view, sometimes a skip-level or cross-functional view)
- Historical context (when did the problem start? what changed around that time?)
- Organizational comparison (is this unique to this team, or a pattern across the organization?)

## Building the Business Case

When an HR intervention requires significant investment, a business case is required. A business case answers: what is the problem costing, what is the proposed solution, and is the expected return worth the investment?

### Business case structure

**Problem quantification:** What is the current state costing in measurable terms? Attrition cost, productivity gap, time lost to process inefficiency, compliance risk.

**Root cause summary:** What diagnosis supports this being the right problem to solve? What evidence supports the diagnosis?

**Proposed solution:** What specifically will be done? What does it include and not include?

**Investment required:** Time, money, and opportunity cost.

**Expected outcomes and ROI:** What will change as a result? How will you measure it? What is the expected financial or operational return?

**Risks and mitigations:** What could cause this not to work, and how will you manage those risks?

**Decision requested:** What do you need the stakeholder to decide or approve?

Keep the business case to one page where possible. Supporting data and analysis can be in an appendix. A business case that requires 20 minutes to read will not be read.

## Stakeholder Advisory

### Influencing without authority

HR has no direct authority over the business decisions it is most trying to influence. Influencing without authority is therefore a core HR consulting skill.

**Principles that make influence work:**

- **Credibility first:** Stakeholders are influenced by people they trust. Build credibility through consistent, accurate, well-researched advice before you need to influence a significant decision.
- **Understand their goals:** Influence is much easier when your recommendation serves the stakeholder's interests, not just HR's agenda. Frame recommendations in terms of what the stakeholder is trying to achieve.
- **Evidence over assertion:** "Studies show that..." is less convincing than "your own attrition data shows that managers who have monthly 1:1s with direct reports have 40% lower attrition than those who do not."
- **Acknowledge the trade-offs:** Recommendations that present only the upside are less credible than ones that honestly name the costs, risks, and what you are not recommending and why.
- **Know when to push and when to concede:** Some issues are worth sustained advocacy; most are not. Choosing which hills to hold is a judgment call that experienced HR leaders make deliberately.

### Managing difficult stakeholders

The most common difficult stakeholder situation: a senior leader who wants HR to help them do something HR believes is wrong (a specific termination, a biased hiring decision, an approach to a sensitive conversation that carries legal risk).

The effective approach:
1. Understand their goal fully before responding
2. Name the concern directly and specifically (not vaguely) — "I have a concern about this approach that I want to raise"
3. Provide the evidence or reasoning for the concern
4. Offer an alternative that achieves their goal with less risk
5. Be clear about what HR can and cannot support, and why

Avoiding difficult conversations with senior leaders is the most common way HR loses credibility and influence.
