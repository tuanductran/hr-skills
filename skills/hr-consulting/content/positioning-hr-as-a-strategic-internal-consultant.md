# Positioning HR as a Strategic Internal Consultant

## Overview

HR business partners are frequently pulled into a transactional advisory mode — answering policy questions, processing requests, mediating disputes — that leaves little room for the higher-value consulting work of diagnosing root causes and building genuine business cases. Operating as a strategic internal consultant is a deliberate shift in how HRBPs engage with business stakeholders: from responding to stated requests to diagnosing underlying problems, and from delivering opinions to delivering evidence-based recommendations that hold up under scrutiny.

## Diagnosing Before Prescribing

The most common internal consulting failure is accepting a stakeholder's framing of a problem at face value and jumping straight to a solution:

- When a business leader requests a specific intervention ("we need a training program for the sales team"), treat the request as a hypothesis to test, not a brief to execute — the underlying issue may be something training won't fix (unclear targets, a compensation structure misaligned with desired behavior, a management gap)
- Gather independent data before accepting the stated diagnosis — a short set of structured conversations or a quick review of relevant metrics often reveals whether the requested solution actually addresses the root cause
- Reframe the problem back to the stakeholder when the data suggests a different root cause, rather than silently substituting a different intervention — stakeholders are generally receptive to a well-evidenced reframe, but resistant to a solution they didn't ask for and don't understand the rationale behind

## Building a Business Case

An HR recommendation lands with more credibility when it's framed the way the business evaluates other investments, not just in HR-specific terms:

- **Quantify the cost of the problem** in terms the stakeholder already tracks — turnover cost, productivity loss, time-to-fill delay — rather than assuming the problem's severity is self-evident
- **Present the recommended intervention's expected impact** with realistic, evidence-grounded estimates, not aspirational figures that won't hold up if challenged
- **Address the counterfactual explicitly** — what happens if the organization does nothing — since this is often the most persuasive part of a well-built case and is frequently omitted
- **Acknowledge uncertainty honestly** rather than presenting estimates with false precision; a stakeholder who catches an overconfident estimate later discounts the rest of the case

## Running an HR Diagnostic

A structured diagnostic gives a consulting engagement rigor beyond an HRBP's individual intuition about the problem:

1. Define the specific question the diagnostic will answer, scoped narrowly enough to be tractable within the engagement's time and resource constraints
2. Collect data from multiple sources — structured interviews, relevant existing metrics, document review — rather than relying on a single source that risks reflecting one perspective disproportionately
3. Synthesize findings into a small number of prioritized, evidence-backed conclusions, resisting the urge to report every observation with equal weight
4. Present findings with the underlying evidence visible, so stakeholders can evaluate the reasoning, not just accept a conclusion on authority

## Managing the Advisory Relationship

- Build credibility incrementally through smaller, well-executed engagements before attempting to influence larger strategic decisions — trust in internal consulting capability is earned through track record, not asserted through title
- Maintain independence in the diagnosis even while building a collaborative relationship with the stakeholder — an HRBP who only ever confirms what the business leader already believes provides limited actual value as a consultant
- Know when to escalate or bring in additional expertise (legal, specialized HR functions, external resources) rather than attempting to advise beyond genuine competence, which risks both a poor outcome and damaged credibility for future engagements

## Delivering Recommendations Stakeholders Will Act On

A technically correct recommendation that isn't framed for the stakeholder's actual decision-making context often goes unimplemented:

- Present a small number of clearly differentiated options with trade-offs made explicit, rather than a single prescribed answer with no visible alternative considered — this format both respects the stakeholder's decision authority and surfaces where genuine judgment calls remain
- Anticipate and address the most likely objections proactively within the recommendation itself, rather than waiting for pushback to surface them
- Define a clear next step and owner for implementation, since a recommendation without an implementation path frequently stalls regardless of its quality

## Common Pitfalls

- **Accepting a stakeholder's stated solution as the actual problem definition**, skipping the diagnostic work that internal consulting is meant to provide.
- **Building a business case with unsubstantiated or overconfident estimates**, which undermines credibility when scrutinized.
- **Delivering findings without a clear recommended path forward**, leaving stakeholders with analysis but no actionable next step.
