# Talent Acquisition Strategy and Operations

Strategic talent acquisition (TA) is the discipline of building an efficient recruiting function aligned to business demand. It spans designing the TA operating model, setting up processes and technology, measuring pipeline health, and ensuring the organization can hire at the pace and quality required to execute strategy.

## The TA function and business alignment

A mature TA function starts with clarity on the business question: "What talent do we need to execute our strategy?" This requires understanding:

- **Business plan:** Revenue targets, geographic expansion, product launches, that drive headcount needs
- **Demand signals:** Which functions are growing? Which roles are bottlenecks? What time-to-fill targets are realistic?
- **Supply constraints:** Which talent pools are tight? Which markets are we weak in? What's the hiring velocity we can realistically achieve?

The TA function translates business demand into hiring plans, then executes the plan with speed and quality. Too much emphasis on speed and you hire poor quality; too much emphasis on quality and you can't scale hiring fast enough to support business growth.

## TA operating model

TA operating models vary by company size and hiring complexity:

**Startup (0-50 people):** Usually 1 generalist recruiter or founder doing recruiting. Manual processes, no formal ATS, hiring driven by founder network.

**Growing (50-200 people):** 2-4 recruiters, some specialization (engineering vs. business), ATS implemented, structured sourcing channels emerging. TA reports to CEO or COO.

**Scaling (200-1000 people):** 10-20 recruiters organized by function (engineering recruiting, sales recruiting, etc.), dedicated TA operations role, talent planning, analytics dashboard, ATS + sourcing tools stack. TA leader reports to CHRO or CEO.

**Enterprise (1000+ people):** 50+ person TA organization, centers of excellence (university recruiting, RPO management, talent pool curation), sophisticated demand forecasting, integrated with workforce planning. TA is a strategic business partner.

## Sourcing channels and velocity

Recruiting velocity depends on your sourcing channel mix:

**Inbound applications:** Come to you. Low recruiter time but limited volume and quality varies. Best for well-known companies or attractive roles.

**Direct search (LinkedIn Recruiter, Boolean search):** Recruiter actively reaches out to prospects. Higher volume than inbound, better targeting, but recruiter-intensive. Works for specialized skills.

**Referrals:** Employees refer friends. High-quality, fastest conversion, but volume is limited by employee network. Needs referral incentive program.

**Agencies and RPO:** External party does sourcing. Costs 15-20% of first-year salary. Better for high-volume, standard roles. Maintains pipeline when internal TA is overwhelmed.

**University and apprenticeship programs:** Pipeline for early-career talent. Long lead time (recruiting in fall for summer start) but predictable supply of junior talent.

**Community and events:** Sponsor meetups, conferences, bootcamps. High-touch relationship building. Works for mission-driven hiring or specialized skills.

Most organizations use a mix: 40% inbound + referrals, 30% direct search, 20% agencies, 10% universities/events. The mix shifts based on hiring volumes and role type.

## Recruiting SLAs and time-to-fill

Time-to-fill is a core TA metric. Industry benchmark is 45-60 days from requisition open to offer accepted. Drivers:

- **Process design:** Number of interview stages, parallel vs. serial interviews. Each stage adds 1-2 weeks.
- **Sourcing velocity:** How fast can you source qualified candidates? Tight markets slow this down.
- **Decision speed:** How fast do hiring managers decide? Slow decision-makers extend cycle.
- **Offer acceptance:** Some roles have high rejection rates (counteroffer from current employer). Affects time-to-start.

Realistic SLAs by hiring scenario:
- Standard roles (abundant supply): 35-45 days target
- Competitive roles (moderate supply): 50-70 days target
- Scarce roles (very tight market): 80-120 days target, sometimes never fill

SLAs should be negotiated with business stakeholders upfront, not set after hiring fails.

## ATS and technology

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is the operating system of modern recruiting. It captures:

- **Requisition lifecycle:** Open → sourcing → candidates → interviews → offer → hired
- **Candidate pipeline:** All people in various stages of the hiring funnel
- **Recruiting metrics:** Time-to-fill, source quality, hiring velocity, cost-per-hire
- **Compliance:** EEO reporting, background checks, offer management

Modern ATS tools include integrations with email (track recruiter-candidate communication), calendar (schedule interviews), LinkedIn (import candidates), and reporting.

Common ATS choices: Greenhouse, Lever, Workable, iCIMS. Smaller companies use Google Forms or Airtable as lightweight alternatives.

## Measuring TA effectiveness

Core metrics to track:

**Velocity:** Time-to-fill, time-to-start, requisitions filled per quarter. Trending up or down?

**Quality:** Offer acceptance rate (should be 70%+), new hire retention at 90 days, hire quality per hiring manager feedback.

**Efficiency:** Cost-per-hire (recruiting spend ÷ hires), cost-per-application, recruiter efficiency (hires per recruiter).

**Pipeline:** Candidates by stage (applications, screens, interviews, offers), pipeline velocity (how fast candidates move through stages).

**Sourcing:** Quality and volume by channel (referrals typically highest quality, lowest cost; agencies lowest quality, highest cost).

Most mature organizations track these in a dashboard updated weekly and reviewed with business stakeholders monthly.
