# Talent Acquisition Strategy and Operating Model

## Overview

Talent acquisition is not just recruiting. It is the full system by which an organization builds workforce capability through external hiring — strategy, process, infrastructure, and the team that operates it. Organizations that treat TA as an administrative function get administrative results. Those that treat it as a strategic capability get a durable competitive advantage in the labor market.

## TA Operating Model Design

The operating model determines how TA work is organized, who does what, and how it connects to the business.

### Centralized vs. decentralized vs. hybrid

**Centralized TA:** All recruiters report to a central TA function. Provides consistency in process and candidate experience, enables specialization, and supports enterprise-wide talent strategy. Can feel slow to business units that want dedicated resources.

**Decentralized TA:** Recruiters are embedded in business units and report to HR business partners or line leaders. Strong business alignment and context, but inconsistent process, competing priorities, and difficulty maintaining recruiter expertise and career development.

**Hybrid (most common at scale):** A central TA team owns enterprise process, technology, employer brand, and sourcing infrastructure. Embedded or aligned recruiters handle business-unit relationships and execution. Works well when roles and accountabilities are clearly defined.

### Specialist vs. generalist recruiters

Specialist recruiters (engineering, finance, sales, executive) develop deeper domain knowledge, stronger talent networks, and better assessor judgment within their domain. They are more efficient for high-volume or highly competitive segments.

Generalist recruiters are more flexible for lower-volume functions and provide coverage when demand shifts between business areas.

Most TA functions should specialize recruiters for their two or three highest-volume or most competitive hiring segments (typically engineering and sales in tech companies), and use generalists or hybrid models for the rest.

## Sourcing Strategy

Sourcing is where TA creates the most value relative to passive job posting. A mature sourcing strategy combines inbound and outbound channels calibrated to each role's market conditions.

### Inbound channels

- Career site (owned, highest-quality traffic when SEO and conversion are optimized)
- Job boards (LinkedIn, VietnamWorks, TopCV in Vietnam context; Indeed, Glassdoor globally)
- Employee referral program
- University and campus pipelines
- Recruiter relationship pipeline from previous processes

### Outbound sourcing

Outbound is required when the target candidate pool is not actively looking. Key outbound approaches:

**Direct search:** LinkedIn Recruiter, GitHub, Behance, Stack Overflow, and domain-specific communities. Effective when the target profile is well-defined and the tools surface the right population.

**Network mapping:** Identifying target organizations with concentrations of the talent profile you need, mapping their org charts, and building targeted outreach.

**Community sourcing:** Developer communities, HR professional associations, alumni networks, Slack/Discord communities. Higher trust, lower volume than direct search.

**Talent pools from prior processes:** Candidates who interviewed but were not hired, or who applied in the past, are warm contacts. A well-maintained CRM makes this systematic.

### Channel prioritization by role type

Not all roles require the same sourcing investment. A framework:

- **High volume, active job market:** Optimize inbound (career site, job boards, referrals). Outbound is expensive relative to yield.
- **Low volume, passive candidate pool:** Outbound is essential. Budget time and tools accordingly.
- **Niche technical skills, global competition:** Specialized community sourcing, conference networks, and targeted direct search. Standard channels have low yield.

## ATS and Process Design

The ATS is not just a database — it is the operating system for TA. A well-configured ATS reduces recruiter administrative load, maintains process consistency, and generates the data needed for performance management.

### Minimum ATS configuration requirements

- Stages reflect your actual process, not the default template
- SLA targets are built into stage definitions so delays are visible
- Structured interview scorecards are attached to each stage requiring evaluation
- Offer and disposition workflows are documented and followed consistently
- Reporting is configured before the ATS goes live, not as an afterthought

### Common ATS configuration failures

- Too many stages that no one uses (creates noise in reporting)
- Dispositions that are not used consistently (destroys pipeline reporting accuracy)
- Interview feedback entered as free text rather than structured scorecards (makes calibration impossible)
- Requisition approval workflows that add weeks of delay before recruiting begins

## Recruiting SLAs and Metrics

SLAs create accountability and visibility. Without them, TA performance is managed by anecdote.

### Core TA metrics

**Time to fill:** Date requisition opened to date offer accepted. The primary measure of TA speed.

**Time to hire:** Date candidate entered pipeline to date offer accepted. Measures recruiting process efficiency, isolated from requisition approval delays.

**Offer acceptance rate:** Offers accepted ÷ offers extended. Below 85% signals compensation, process, or candidate experience issues.

**Pipeline conversion rates by stage:** Identifies where candidates are being lost and whether volume at top of funnel is adequate.

**Source effectiveness:** Hires by source as a percentage of total hires. Calibrates sourcing investment.

**Quality of hire:** Correlation between hiring assessment and post-hire performance. Requires 90-180 day lag to measure. The hardest metric to get right but the most strategically valuable.

### SLA design principles

SLAs should be set based on actual business need and calibrated against what the team can reliably achieve. SLAs that are consistently missed because they are unrealistic are worse than no SLAs — they train the team to ignore them.

Start with time-to-fill SLAs by role category (leadership, specialist, high-volume). Add stage-level SLAs (time from application to screen, screen to interview, interview to decision) as the team matures.
