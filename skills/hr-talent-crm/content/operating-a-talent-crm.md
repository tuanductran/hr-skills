# Operating a Talent CRM

## Overview

A talent CRM exists to solve a specific problem that transactional recruiting doesn't: strong candidates who aren't right for a role today are often right for a different role in six or twelve months, but without a deliberate system, that relationship goes cold and has to be rebuilt from scratch. A talent CRM is the infrastructure for keeping those relationships warm — segmenting talent communities, running nurture campaigns, and re-engaging candidates ahead of actual hiring need rather than only when a requisition opens.

## Segmenting the Talent Pool

Effective segmentation goes beyond a single "candidates" bucket and organizes talent by what determines the right engagement approach:

- **By role family and seniority** — the content and cadence that resonates with early-career candidates differs substantially from what resonates with senior specialists
- **By pipeline stage or origin** — silver medalists (strong candidates who lost out narrowly), inbound applicants for roles not currently open, and passively sourced but not-yet-engaged candidates each warrant different nurture strategies
- **By engagement recency and responsiveness** — segments that haven't engaged with outreach in an extended period need either a different approach or should be sunset from active nurture rather than continuing generic, ignored outreach indefinitely

Segmentation should be revisited periodically, not set once at CRM launch — role priorities and hiring needs shift, and a segmentation scheme built for last year's hiring plan may not reflect current priorities.

## Silver Medalist Re-engagement

Silver medalists — candidates who reached late stages but weren't selected — are typically the highest-value CRM segment because they've already been vetted through much of the process:

- Re-engage promptly when a similar or related role opens, rather than starting a fresh search when a known strong candidate exists in the pipeline
- Communicate the original outcome honestly when re-engaging, briefly acknowledging the prior process rather than reaching out as if the relationship didn't exist — candidates notice and appreciate this distinction
- Track silver medalist conversion rate as a specific CRM performance metric, since it's often one of the strongest signals that the CRM is generating real hiring value rather than just maintaining a large, unused database

## Designing Nurture Campaigns

Nurture campaigns keep candidates engaged with the organization over time without requiring an open role to justify contact:

- Vary content type across the nurture sequence — company news, role-relevant industry content, employee stories — rather than repeatedly sending generic "we're hiring" messages, which fatigue recipients quickly
- Set a deliberate cadence that respects candidate attention without going silent for long stretches — cadence that's too frequent drives opt-outs; cadence that's too sparse lets the relationship go cold
- Personalize based on the segment and the individual's known interests or role area, rather than sending identical content across the entire database

## CRM Data Hygiene

A CRM's value degrades quickly without deliberate maintenance:

- Set a defined process for capturing and updating candidate data at each touchpoint (application, interview, informal networking contact) so the database doesn't rely solely on point-in-time imports
- Periodically sunset or archive contacts who have gone unresponsive across multiple engagement attempts, rather than letting the active database grow indefinitely with stale, unengaged records that dilute campaign performance metrics
- Maintain consent and communication preference records carefully — outreach to candidates who've opted out or requested no contact is both a trust issue and, in many jurisdictions, a compliance issue

## Measuring CRM Performance

- **Engagement rate by segment** — open, click, and response rates, tracked over time and compared across segments to identify what content and cadence actually work for this candidate population
- **Pipeline contribution** — the share of new interview pipeline sourced from existing CRM contacts versus fresh outbound sourcing, which is often the clearest indicator of whether the CRM is paying for itself
- **Time-to-fill for CRM-sourced hires** compared to other sourcing channels — a well-maintained CRM should meaningfully outperform cold outreach on this metric, since the relationship groundwork is already done

## Common Pitfalls

- **Building a large database with no active nurture strategy**, which produces a stale contact list rather than a functioning pipeline.
- **Treating every segment identically** with the same generic content and cadence, missing the relevance that makes nurture campaigns actually effective.
- **Never measuring CRM-sourced hire outcomes**, which makes it impossible to demonstrate the CRM's actual contribution to hiring versus its cost to maintain.
