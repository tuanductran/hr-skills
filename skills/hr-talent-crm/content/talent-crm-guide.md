# Talent CRM: Building and Managing Candidate Pipelines

## Overview

A talent CRM (Candidate Relationship Management system) is the infrastructure for building relationships with candidates before a role is open — so that when hiring need emerges, you have a warm pipeline rather than starting cold every time.

Most TA teams treat every search as a new problem. A talent CRM makes prior work compound: sourced candidates, nurture relationships, talent community members, and silver medalists from past processes all become assets for future searches.

## Why a Talent CRM Is Worth Building

The case for a talent CRM is most compelling in three situations:

**High-volume, recurring roles:** If you hire 20 engineers a year, the sourcing work done for this quarter's hire should feed next quarter's pipeline. Without a CRM, that work resets every cycle.

**Competitive talent segments:** For roles where top candidates are rarely active job seekers, the relationship you built 6 months ago is the reason they take your call today. Cold outreach to passive candidates at the moment of need has low response rates; warm outreach to someone you have been in contact with has much higher conversion.

**Long-lead-time searches:** Senior leadership and specialized technical roles often have 3-6 month time-to-fill even with a warm pipeline. Without pre-built relationships, these searches stretch longer.

## CRM Segmentation

A CRM that is just a database is not useful. Useful CRMs are organized so that the right candidate pool can be identified and activated quickly.

### Recommended segmentation dimensions

**Role category:** Engineering, Product, Sales, Operations, Leadership. The most fundamental cut — a recruiter activating a pipeline needs to start within their domain.

**Pipeline stage:**
- *Identified* — name and profile captured, not yet contacted
- *Contacted* — outreach sent, no response yet
- *Engaged* — responded positively, not in an active process
- *Nurture* — ongoing relationship, not currently open to roles
- *Active* — currently interested and being considered for an open role
- *Silver medalist* — interviewed and not selected, strong candidate for future roles

**Talent tier:** Calibrate candidate quality relative to your hiring bar. Tier 1 candidates are immediate-close fits; Tier 2 are strong with some gaps; Tier 3 are pipeline for future development.

**Last activity date:** Stale records with no recent contact are not an active pipeline. Surface contacts by recency to prioritize re-engagement.

## Nurture Campaigns

Nurture is the ongoing communication that keeps engaged candidates warm until a relevant role opens. Done well, it is low-volume, personalized, and genuinely useful to the candidate. Done poorly, it is spam that destroys the relationship it is meant to maintain.

### Principles of effective nurture

**Relevant, not generic:** A software engineer interested in distributed systems does not want updates about your company's office coffee bar. They want content about your technical architecture, engineering culture, and the problems your team is solving.

**Low frequency:** Monthly or quarterly touchpoints are sufficient for most passive candidates. More frequent contact without a specific reason to reach out erodes the relationship.

**Value to the candidate:** Content that is useful to the candidate — industry insights, career resources, relevant events — converts better than promotional content about the company.

**Personal when it matters:** For Tier 1 and Silver Medalist candidates, a personal note from the recruiter who built the relationship outperforms a mass email campaign.

### Nurture content types by segment

**Technical candidates:** Engineering blog posts, technical talks, open source contributions, architecture decisions.

**Leadership candidates:** Thought leadership from company executives, strategic milestones, business momentum signals.

**Passive candidates who declined a previous process:** Acknowledge the time that has passed. "It's been a year since we last spoke — I wanted to reach out because X has changed and I thought it might be relevant to you" is more credible than a generic update.

## CRM Hygiene

A CRM degrades quickly without active maintenance. Poor hygiene produces false confidence — a database with 2,000 records that has not been updated in 18 months is not a pipeline.

### Minimum hygiene standards

- **Disposition every contact** after each outreach attempt — respond, no response, bounced, opted out
- **Date-stamp all activity** — last contact date is the primary signal of pipeline health
- **Remove opted-out contacts immediately** — legal requirement in most jurisdictions with data privacy law
- **Audit stale records quarterly** — contacts with no activity in 12+ months should be reviewed and either re-engaged or archived

### Data privacy compliance

In Vietnam, the Personal Data Protection Decree (Nghị định 13/2023/NĐ-CP) and broader data privacy requirements govern how personal data in a talent CRM must be handled:

- Candidates should know their data is being retained and for what purpose
- Retention periods should be defined and enforced (common practice: 2 years from last contact for candidates who did not join)
- Opt-out requests must be honored promptly
- Data should not be shared with third parties without appropriate consent

## Measuring CRM Effectiveness

A talent CRM is an investment. Measure its return.

**Pipeline activation rate:** When a role opens, what percentage of hires came from the existing CRM versus cold-sourced? Rising activation rate signals CRM maturity.

**Time-to-engagement:** How long from opening a search to first qualified candidate in process, for roles sourced from CRM versus cold? CRM should reduce this materially.

**Nurture open and response rates:** Directional signal on whether nurture content is resonating.

**Silver medalist conversion:** What percentage of silver medalists from past processes are hired within 18 months? This is the clearest metric on whether the CRM is capturing and retaining high-quality relationships.
