# Managing a Union Organizing Campaign and Building a Labor Relations Strategy for a Scaling Company

## Context

You are the HR Business Partner at a 260-person logistics and fulfillment company. Three weeks ago, you learned through a supervisor report that a union organizer from a regional Teamsters local has been distributing authorization cards to warehouse employees during break periods. Since then, two supervisors have made comments to employees that may constitute NLRA violations, the operations director has asked HR to "find out who is involved and stop it," and a petition for a representation election has been filed with the NLRB. You have been asked to build the company's legal response, the labor relations strategy, and a longer-term employee relations stabilization plan — starting essentially from a reactive, panicked posture with no existing labor relations infrastructure.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "A union petition was just filed with the NLRB, two supervisors may have said things that violated the NLRA, and the operations director wants us to find out who signed cards and stop the organizing. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a filed NLRB petition, potential supervisor NLRA violations already on the record, and pressure from operations leadership to take actions that could compound legal exposure — start with two priority areas before anything else:

**Legal triage and supervisor conduct containment (the immediate legal risk):**

- Contact employment counsel immediately — a filed NLRB petition triggers a defined legal timeline and requires specific response steps that HR cannot manage unilaterally
- Identify exactly what the two supervisors said, to whom, and in what context, before any further supervisor communication about the organizing campaign occurs
- Brief the operations director on what the NLRA prohibits — specifically that interrogating employees about union activity, threatening job loss, promising benefits to stop organizing, and surveilling union activity are illegal under the TIPS framework, and that the "find out who is involved" instruction is a textbook NLRA violation if acted on

**Employee relations climate diagnosis (the underlying cause):**

- The filing is a symptom. Before designing any response strategy, understand what the actual drivers of organizing interest are — compensation, scheduling, management treatment, safety concerns, or some combination
- Do not assume the organizing campaign created the problem; it surfaced a problem that already existed
- Employees who file with the NLRB have reached the point where they do not trust internal channels — the response strategy needs to address that trust gap, not only the legal process

> **Avoid:** Do not let operations leadership's pressure to "stop it" result in any supervisor actions toward identified or suspected union supporters before counsel has been briefed and a legal boundary has been clearly communicated to all people managers. A single retaliatory action against a union supporter after a petition is filed creates an unfair labor practice charge that can delay the election, trigger bargaining obligation without a vote, and generate significant legal liability.

## Step 2 — Modeling Strategic Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few strategic options for responding to the organizing campaign so I can compare trade-offs before recommending anything to leadership."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Legal defense only (contest the election, focus on procedural response):**

```text
Approach: Retain labor counsel to contest the bargaining unit definition,
          challenge the election timeline, and prepare a legal campaign
          focused on the election process and employee communication
          about the costs and risks of union representation
Timeline: Petition response within 7 days; election typically 25 days
          after consent or directed election order
Trade-off: Legally aggressive posture may win or delay the election,
           but does not address the underlying employee relations
           conditions that generated organizing interest; a union loss
           under this approach often produces a second organizing
           campaign within 12-18 months if root causes are unaddressed
```

**Option B — Positive employee relations campaign (address underlying conditions while running legal process):**

```text
Approach: Run the legal process with counsel while simultaneously
          launching a visible, credible effort to address the
          substantive employee concerns driving organizing interest
          (compensation review, scheduling, management practices)
Timeline: Legal process runs in parallel; employee relations
          improvements communicated and implemented within 30 days
Trade-off: More expensive and operationally complex, but addresses
           the root cause alongside the legal process; legally
           requires that any improvements were already planned
           before the petition — implementing new benefits solely
           in response to organizing can itself be an NLRA violation
```

**Option C — Neutrality and card check agreement (recognize without an election):**

```text
Approach: Negotiate a neutrality agreement with the union allowing
          card-check recognition rather than contesting the election,
          in exchange for specific organizing conduct commitments
Timeline: Negotiated directly with the union; bypasses the NLRB
          election timeline
Trade-off: Avoids the legal and reputational cost of an election
           campaign; positions the company for a more constructive
           first-contract negotiation, but requires leadership to
           accept union representation as an outcome and forgo
           the election process
```

**Benchmark context:**

NLRB election data consistently shows that employer campaigns focused on legal delay and anti-union messaging without addressing underlying workplace conditions have a lower long-term success rate than strategies that combine legal compliance with genuine employee relations improvement. A company that loses an election having run a purely defensive campaign also typically faces a more adversarial first-contract negotiation than one that entered the process with a more constructive posture.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the supervisor conduct already on record and the operations director's stated instinct toward aggressive containment, Option A's purely defensive posture carries elevated unfair labor practice risk in this specific situation. A modified Option B — run the legal process correctly and carefully, while launching a credible, pre-existing-plan-based employee relations improvement effort — is the strongest path that balances legal defense with the underlying trust problem.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Underlying Employee Relations Gap

**Sample prompt:**

> "Before I design any response strategy, I need to understand what actually drove employees to file. How do I diagnose this without interrogating people or creating more legal exposure?"

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming the cause, gather signals across four dimensions — without directly asking employees about the union campaign, which is prohibited:

**1. Compensation and scheduling fairness:**

- Are warehouse wages competitive with the regional market and peer employers? When was the last compensation review?
- Are scheduling practices — shift assignments, overtime distribution, weekend rotation — applied consistently and transparently, or at supervisor discretion?

**2. Management practice and supervisor behavior:**

- What does the day-to-day experience of warehouse employees look like under their direct supervisors?
- Have there been patterns of favoritism, inconsistent discipline, or disrespectful treatment that HR has seen in prior complaint data or exit interviews?

**3. Safety and working conditions:**

- Are there unresolved safety concerns, equipment complaints, or physical working condition issues in the warehouse that have been reported and not addressed?
- Safety and working conditions are among the most consistent drivers of warehouse and logistics organizing campaigns

**4. Voice and trust in internal channels:**

- Have employees had meaningful access to HR or senior leadership, or is the supervisory layer their only point of contact with the company?
- Exit interview data and any existing climate or engagement survey results are the most useful source here — employees who reached the NLRB did so because they did not believe internal channels would work

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present the organizing campaign as evidence that employees are being manipulated by outside agitators. Bring a clear, honest assessment of which conditions are driving organizing interest and which are addressable within the legal constraints of the active campaign period — and which require post-election or post-contract work regardless of the outcome.

## Step 4 — Building the Labor Relations Response Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build a labor relations response plan that covers the legal process, the employee relations improvement work, and the supervisor training — since we have all three running in parallel right now."

**Skill response — phased response plan:**

| Phase | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Legal triage | Not done | Retain labor counsel within 24 hours; brief all supervisors on TIPS framework before any further manager communication |
| Supervisor conduct remediation | Violation risk active | Document what was said; counsel to assess ULP exposure; supervisor retraining before any further employee communication |
| Employee relations diagnosis | Not done | Review compensation data, exit interviews, safety logs, and prior complaint data for root cause signals |
| Employee communications | Not yet structured | Develop lawful, factual company communications about the election process — no threats, no promises, no interrogation |
| Employee relations improvement | Dependent on what was already planned | Identify which improvements were already in plan or budget before the petition; implement only those under counsel guidance |
| Election preparation | Timeline running | Consent or directed election order expected within 25 days of petition; counsel to manage NLRB process |

**Stakeholder-specific communication sequence:**

1. Employment counsel — retained and briefed before any other internal action is taken
2. Operations director and senior leadership — NLRA legal boundaries briefed immediately, before any further supervisor instructions are issued
3. All people managers — TIPS framework training completed before managers have any further conversation with employees about the union
4. Employees — lawful, factual election-related communications drafted by counsel and reviewed before distribution

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequencing is legally critical: managers who have not been briefed on the TIPS framework should have zero contact with employees about the union campaign until the training is complete
- Any employee relations improvement that is implemented during the campaign window must be traceable to a pre-petition decision; implementing improvements specifically in response to organizing is an NLRA violation regardless of intent

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the election as the primary problem to solve. If the underlying employee relations conditions are not addressed, a union loss in the election produces a second campaign; if the company wins, it produces the same workforce conditions that generated the first campaign. The election outcome is one event in a longer labor relations arc.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this response plan to the CEO and operations director before they do anything that makes the legal situation worse. How do I frame it given the pressure to 'stop' the organizing?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership in a labor relations crisis responds to a plan that names the legal risk clearly and gives them defensible actions they can take — rather than a list of things they cannot do. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Name the legal reality directly and early:**

"Two things have already happened that create legal exposure we need to manage immediately: supervisor comments that may constitute unfair labor practices under the NLRA, and a filed petition that triggers a defined legal timeline. I've retained counsel and I'm bringing you a response plan. What I need from you first is a commitment that no manager will communicate with employees about the union campaign until counsel has briefed the supervisor group — because one more misstep in the next 48 hours doubles our legal exposure."

**Part 2 — Reframe the goal from "stop it" to "respond correctly":**

"I understand the instinct to stop this, and I want to be direct with you: some of what you've asked supervisors to do — finding out who signed cards, telling employees what will happen if the union gets in — are NLRA violations that expose the company to unfair labor practice charges, bargaining orders without an election, and significant litigation cost. The goal I'm recommending is responding correctly and addressing what actually drove employees to file, which gives us the best chance at a good outcome — in the election and in the long-term employee relationship."

**Part 3 — Present the plan and ask for what you need:**

"I'm recommending counsel-guided election response, an immediate supervisor TIPS training, and a parallel review of the compensation and scheduling conditions that likely drove organizing interest. I need your public commitment to the supervisor training this week, and your agreement that no operations leader takes any action toward identified union supporters before I've cleared it with counsel."

> **Key principle:** Leadership in a labor crisis responds better to a plan that gives them something they can do — rather than only a list of what they cannot do. Frame legal compliance as the path to the best available outcome, not as constraint alone.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Labor Relations Communications and Policy

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft the supervisor TIPS training content, the lawful employee election communication, and a longer-term employee relations policy framework. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without creating legal risk?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context and confirm counsel review:**

AI tools can draft labor relations content efficiently, but any employee-facing election communication, disciplinary policy, or grievance process must be reviewed by employment counsel before use — particularly during an active NLRB election period.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure for supervisor training:**

```text
I am an HR Business Partner preparing supervisor training for an
NLRB election period at a 260-person logistics company.

Context:
- A Teamsters petition has been filed covering warehouse employees
- Two supervisors have already made comments that may violate the NLRA
- I need a practical TIPS framework training for all people managers

Please:
1. Draft a TIPS framework training outline covering what managers
   legally cannot do during an election period (Threaten, Interrogate,
   Promise, Spy) with one concrete example of each type of violation
2. Draft a list of lawful responses managers can give if employees
   ask them about the union campaign
3. Draft a short manager FAQ covering the most common missteps
   and the correct alternative action for each
```

**Step 3 — Use this prompt structure for employee communications:**

```text
Based on this situation, draft a factual, lawful employee communication
that: explains the NLRB election process in neutral terms; states the
company's position that employees have the right to vote; avoids
threats, promises, interrogation, or surveillance language; and
directs employees to HR for factual questions about the process.
Note: This draft must be reviewed by employment counsel before use.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting structured TIPS training content and manager FAQs quickly
- Generating policy language drafts for grievance processes and disciplinary frameworks
- Producing election communication drafts for counsel review

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Legal review of any employee-facing election or labor relations communication
- Judgment on which specific facts in your situation create legal risk
- The employment counsel relationship that is non-negotiable in an active NLRB matter

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Labor Relations Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Whether we win or lose the election, I know the underlying employee relations problems will still be there. How do I build a stabilization plan that addresses the root causes?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Have supervisors applied the TIPS framework consistently since training, or have there been additional NLRA-risk communications?
- Have the pre-existing employee relations improvements (compensation review, scheduling transparency, safety response) been implemented as committed?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- If an election occurred: is the post-election employee climate stabilizing, or is there residual resentment — from either outcome — that is compounding into new grievances or attrition?
- If a contract is being negotiated: are first-contract negotiations proceeding constructively, or is the bargaining relationship already adversarial?
- Are the management practice changes (scheduling consistency, supervisor accountability) holding under operational pressure?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would warehouse employees describe the company's employee relations practices as meaningfully different than they were before the campaign, or as unchanged?
- Have grievance and complaint rates shifted since the stabilization work began?
- What structural changes — if any — are still needed based on what has been learned in the first quarter?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Supervisor NLRA compliance check-in | HRBP + employment counsel | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Employee relations climate pulse check | HRBP | Monthly |
| Grievance and complaint volume review | Employee Relations | Monthly |
| Compensation and scheduling equity audit | HR + Operations | 60 days |

> **Common finding at companies following an organizing campaign:** Whether the union wins or loses, the employee relations climate in the first 90 days after the election outcome is the critical window. Companies that treat a union loss as a signal to return to the pre-campaign status quo typically face a second organizing campaign within 18 months. Companies that treat a union win as adversarial from day one of first-contract bargaining typically spend years in grievance arbitration. The stabilization plan is where the long-term labor relations relationship is actually determined.

## Full Labor Relations Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (organizing campaign, grievance escalation, compliance gap, leadership request)
                    ↓
Retain employment counsel and triage immediate legal risk before any other action
                    ↓
Diagnose the underlying employee relations conditions driving the trigger
                    ↓
Model multiple strategic options and compare legal, operational, and trust trade-offs
                    ↓
Build a phased response plan covering legal compliance, supervisor training, and employee relations
                    ↓
Present the recommendation to leadership with legal risk framing and defensible action options
                    ↓
Use AI to draft training content and policy language, with counsel review before use
                    ↓
Design a 90-day stabilization plan with defined climate and compliance checkpoints
                    ↓
Report on legal status, employee relations climate, and improvement actions at each checkpoint
```

### Common Labor Relations Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Taking action against suspected union supporters before briefing counsel | Retain and brief employment counsel before any supervisor action is taken toward any employee involved in organizing |
| Interrogating employees about union activity or card signing | Train all supervisors on the TIPS framework before any manager communication about the campaign occurs |
| Implementing new benefits or promises during an election campaign | Only implement improvements that were already planned before the petition; document the pre-petition decision trail with counsel |
| Treating the election outcome as the end of the labor relations work | Build a 90-day stabilization plan regardless of election outcome; the conditions that generated organizing interest remain after the vote |
| Presenting organizing as employee manipulation rather than a symptom | Diagnose the underlying employee relations conditions honestly before designing any response strategy |
| Running the legal process without addressing the root cause | Model the legal response and the employee relations improvement work as parallel tracks, not sequential ones |
| Skipping a grievance and dispute resolution infrastructure post-campaign | Build a formal grievance process as part of the stabilization plan; its absence was likely a contributing factor to the organizing campaign in the first place |
