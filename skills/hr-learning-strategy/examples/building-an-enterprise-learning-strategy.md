# Building an enterprise learning strategy

## Context

You are the L&D Director at a 2,500-person financial services company. The CHRO has asked you to present the company's first formal enterprise learning strategy to the executive team next quarter. The current L&D function runs a catalog of courses with no coherent strategy, no connection to business priorities, and a budget that leaders question every year because they can't see the impact. You need to build a credible strategy before the presentation.

## Step 1 — Ground the strategy in business priorities

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to build a learning strategy but don't know where to start. How do I connect it to what the business actually cares about?"

**Expected skill response:**

Start with business context, not learning content. Before designing anything, answer:

- What are the 3-4 strategic priorities the business is focused on this year and next?
- What capability gaps are currently preventing those priorities from being achieved?
- What does leadership expect L&D to produce — behavior change, compliance coverage, retention improvement, productivity acceleration?

Run a learning needs analysis that is grounded in business data, not just manager course requests:

**Data sources to review:**

- Business strategy documents and OKRs
- Performance review data — what skills are rated below expectations most frequently?
- Attrition data — are people leaving because of lack of development opportunities?
- Engagement survey — what does the "opportunities to learn and grow" score tell you?
- Exit interview themes — are development gaps cited as a reason for leaving?

**Stakeholder conversations to conduct:**

Interview 6-8 senior business leaders with 3 questions:
1. What business outcomes are you most at risk of missing, and is capability a factor?
2. What would your team need to be able to do in 12 months that they can't do today?
3. Where do you feel the current L&D program is most and least useful to your team?

## Step 2 — Design the strategy architecture

**Sample prompt:**

> "I've done the needs analysis. The business priorities are AI adoption, client relationship depth, and manager effectiveness. Help me design the strategy architecture."

**Generated learning strategy architecture:**

**Three strategic pillars:**

| Pillar | Business priority | Capability target | L&D investment |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Future skills | AI adoption | AI literacy and applied AI workflows across all roles | Mandatory AI fluency pathway + role-specific applied modules |
| 2. Client excellence | Client relationship depth | Relationship management, consultative communication, client insight skills | Cohort-based client excellence program for client-facing roles |
| 3. Manager capability | Manager effectiveness | Coaching, feedback, performance conversations, team development | Manager development cohort + ongoing peer learning community |

**Supporting infrastructure:**

| Area | Current state | Target state |
|---|---|---|
| Learning technology | Multiple disconnected tools | Integrated LMS with skills tracking and pathway management |
| Content | Vendor catalog with no curation | Curated pathways aligned to pillars + internal content for proprietary skills |
| Measurement | Completion rates only | Business impact metrics: performance improvement, retention, productivity signals |
| Governance | Ad hoc requests | Quarterly learning portfolio review against strategic pillars |

## Step 3 — Build the governance model

**Sample prompt:**

> "Every business unit sends us requests for custom programs. We say yes to everything and then nothing is strategic. How do I build a governance model that changes this?"

**Generated governance framework:**

**Learning investment tiers:**

| Tier | Description | Decision owner | Process |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — Enterprise-wide | Strategic pillar programs affecting all employees | CHRO + L&D Director | Annual strategy cycle; funded in base L&D budget |
| Tier 2 — Function-specific | Business unit capability programs linked to strategic pillars | HR Business Partner + business leader | Quarterly portfolio review; business unit co-funds |
| Tier 3 — Discretionary | Role or team learning not linked to strategic pillars | Manager | Self-directed through learning catalog; no custom build |

**Prioritization filter (apply to every new request before accepting):**

1. Does this address a skill gap linked to a strategic business priority?
2. Is the target population large enough to justify a custom program versus catalog solution?
3. Can we measure a business outcome from this investment within 12 months?
4. Is there an existing solution (internal or vendor) that could meet this need?

If a request fails 3 of 4 filters, redirect to the self-directed catalog rather than building a custom program.

## Step 4 — Design the measurement model

**Sample prompt:**

> "The CFO wants to see ROI on learning. What does a credible learning measurement model look like beyond completion rates?"

**Generated four-level measurement approach:**

| Level | What it measures | Example metrics | Data source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Reaction | Did participants find the learning valuable? | Net Promoter Score, post-program rating | Post-program survey |
| 2. Learning | Did participants gain the intended knowledge or skill? | Pre/post assessment score, skill demonstration | Assessment, manager observation |
| 3. Behavior | Are participants applying new skills on the job? | Manager-rated behavior change at 60 and 90 days | 90-day follow-up survey with managers |
| 4. Business results | Did the learning contribute to a business outcome? | Retention improvement in target cohort, performance score change, deal win rate | HR analytics, business performance data |

**Presenting ROI to the CFO:**

> "For our Manager Development cohort, we measured behavior change at 90 days post-program: 74% of participants' direct reports rated their manager's feedback quality as improved. In the same cohort, voluntary attrition in participants' teams ran 8% lower than the company average over the following 6 months. At our average replacement cost per employee, this represents approximately $X in avoided attrition cost. The program cost $Y per participant."

## Step 5 — Structure the executive presentation

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me structure the executive strategy presentation so leaders approve the investment and understand what L&D is accountable for."

**Generated presentation structure:**

```text
ENTERPRISE LEARNING STRATEGY — EXECUTIVE PRESENTATION

Section 1 — The business case (5 minutes)
  - Three strategic business priorities and the capability gaps blocking them
  - Cost of inaction: what happens to AI adoption, client retention, and manager effectiveness
    if capability gaps are not addressed?
  - Current L&D investment: where it is going and what it is producing

Section 2 — The strategy (10 minutes)
  - Three strategic pillars and how each connects to a business priority
  - What we will build, what we will buy, and what we will stop doing
  - The governance model: how we will prioritize and say no to low-value requests

Section 3 — The investment (5 minutes)
  - Proposed budget by pillar
  - Expected ROI metrics for each pillar at 12 months
  - What success looks like at 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months

Section 4 — What we need from leadership (5 minutes)
  - Executive sponsorship for each pillar (named sponsor per priority)
  - Manager time: how much we need from managers to support learning application
  - Budget approval and governance commitment

Section 5 — Questions
```

## Summary

Use `hr-learning-strategy` to ground enterprise learning in business priorities rather than course catalogs, design a three-pillar strategy that allocates investment to the highest-impact capability gaps, build a governance model that enables HR to say no to low-value requests, and measure learning in business terms that finance and operations leaders find credible.
