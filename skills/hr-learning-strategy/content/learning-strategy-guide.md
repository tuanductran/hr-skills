# Learning Strategy

## Overview

A learning strategy is the organization's plan for building the capabilities it needs to execute its business strategy — through what learning approaches, delivered how, to whom, and measured against what outcomes. It is the answer to "how does this organization systematically build the capability it needs?" rather than a catalog of training programs.

Most organizations have training programs but not a learning strategy. A training catalog is a supply-side document. A learning strategy connects supply to demand — what capabilities the business needs and how learning investment will close the gaps.

## Strategy Before Programs

The most common mistake in L&D is building the learning curriculum before defining the strategy. The result is programs that are well-produced but not connected to business priorities — and therefore not prioritized by employees or managers when competing with urgent work.

### Learning strategy inputs

**Business strategy:** What is the organization trying to achieve in the next 1-3 years? What capabilities are required that the organization does not currently have at the needed scale?

**Skills gap analysis:** What are the gaps between current workforce capability and required capability? Which gaps are addressable through learning versus hiring versus process change?

**Learning demand signals:** What are employees and managers requesting? What performance issues are related to skill gaps versus other factors (process problems, resource constraints, unclear goals)?

**Market intelligence:** What capabilities are competitors building? What skills are emerging in the external market that will become important?

**Learning effectiveness data:** For organizations with existing learning programs, what is working and what is not? Where is investment producing demonstrable capability improvement, and where is it producing completions without behavior change?

### Learning priorities vs. learning catalog

From these inputs, define learning priorities — the 3-5 capability domains where learning investment will have the most strategic impact in the next year. These priorities drive program investment, facilitator time, and budget allocation.

Everything else — compliance training, onboarding basics, functional skills maintenance — is necessary but not strategically prioritized. It should be delivered as efficiently as possible (typically through self-paced digital learning) so that time and investment can focus on strategic priorities.

## Learning Ecosystem Design

A learning ecosystem is the full range of mechanisms through which employees develop capability — not just formal training.

### The 70-20-10 framework

Development research consistently shows that capability develops primarily through:
- **70% on-the-job experience:** Stretch assignments, new responsibilities, cross-functional projects, handling unfamiliar problems
- **20% social learning:** Feedback from managers and peers, coaching, mentoring, communities of practice
- **10% formal learning:** Courses, workshops, certifications, structured programs

Most organizational learning investment focuses on the 10% (formal programs) while underfunding the 70% (experience design) and 20% (coaching and feedback capability). A learning strategy that deliberately invests across all three is more effective than one that treats formal training as the primary development mechanism.

### Formal learning modalities

**Instructor-led training (ILT):** Live classroom or virtual facilitation. Most effective for complex skills requiring practice, feedback, and peer interaction. High cost per learner; justified for high-value capability development.

**Self-paced digital learning:** Asynchronous modules, videos, and assessments. Efficient for knowledge transfer, compliance, and skills with clear right-wrong answers. Less effective for complex judgment and behavior change.

**Blended learning:** Combines self-paced pre-work with live facilitation for practice, discussion, and application. Increases efficiency of live time while providing the interaction that asynchronous learning cannot replicate.

**Cohort-based programs:** Groups of learners move through a curriculum together, with peer learning and discussion as a core element. Highly effective for leadership development and complex capability building. Builds relationships across the organization as a secondary benefit.

**On-the-job learning programs:** Structured stretch assignments, job rotations, and project placements designed as development experiences rather than purely operational moves. Requires manager and HR coordination to design and debrief.

### Learning technology

Learning technology serves three functions: content delivery, progress tracking, and data for measurement. Common platforms:

**LMS (Learning Management System):** The library and compliance tracking layer. Houses self-paced content, tracks completions, manages certifications. Table stakes for most organizations.

**LXP (Learning Experience Platform):** A more consumer-grade interface for discovery, recommendation, and curation of learning content from multiple sources. Better for voluntary, exploratory learning than mandatory compliance.

**Skills platforms:** Tools that connect skills data, learning content, and career pathways. Growing rapidly in capability. Most useful for organizations with a mature skills taxonomy and strong HR data infrastructure.

## Learning Measurement

Learning measurement is one of the most commonly discussed and least well-executed aspects of L&D. The Kirkpatrick model provides a useful framework:

**Level 1 — Reaction:** Did learners find the program useful and engaging? (Post-training survey). Necessary but not sufficient. High satisfaction scores do not predict behavior change.

**Level 2 — Learning:** Did learners acquire the knowledge or skill the program targeted? (Assessment, quiz, skills demonstration). Tests whether learning occurred, not whether it transfers.

**Level 3 — Behavior:** Are learners applying what they learned on the job? (Manager observation, 30/60/90 day follow-up survey, performance data). This is where most measurement stops. Difficult because it requires follow-up beyond the training event.

**Level 4 — Results:** Did the learning investment produce business outcomes? (Performance metrics, error reduction, productivity, revenue, retention). The most strategically important level; the hardest to measure with attribution to learning alone.

The practical approach: measure Level 1 for all programs as a minimum. Measure Level 2 for programs targeting specific skills. Invest in Level 3 and Level 4 measurement for strategic priority programs where the investment is significant enough to justify the measurement effort.
