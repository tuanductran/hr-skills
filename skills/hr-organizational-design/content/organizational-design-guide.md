# Organizational Design

## Overview

Organizational design is the deliberate shaping of an organization's structure, roles, processes, and governance to execute a strategy. It is one of the highest-leverage interventions available to senior HR leaders — and one of the most commonly mishandled, because structure is often changed reactively (in response to a leader departure, a growth problem, or a political negotiation) rather than designed proactively to serve strategic intent.

Good organizational design starts with strategy and works backward to structure. Bad organizational design starts with a blank org chart and fills in boxes.

## The Four Organizational Design Decisions

Every organizational design comes down to four fundamental decisions:

### 1. Grouping: how work is divided and clustered

Work can be grouped by function (all marketing together, all engineering together), by product (all people working on Product A together regardless of discipline), by geography, by customer segment, or by some combination. Each grouping logic optimizes for different coordination needs and creates different trade-offs.

**Functional grouping** optimizes for deep expertise and resource efficiency. Shared services, specialized knowledge, and career development in a discipline are easier in a functional structure. Coordination across functions to deliver a product or customer outcome is harder.

**Product or business unit grouping** optimizes for accountability to outcomes and speed of coordination within a team. Teams that build, sell, and support a product together move faster and have clearer P&L accountability. The cost is duplication of capability across units and weaker functional depth.

**Matrix** attempts to get both — typically functional reporting for expertise and career development, and product or project assignment for delivery accountability. Works when the matrix is simple, role clarity is maintained, and decision rights are clear. Frequently fails when spans widen and the dual-reporting load becomes unmanageable.

### 2. Linking: how coordination happens across groups

Structure alone does not produce coordination. Linking mechanisms are the processes and roles that connect groups that need to work together:

- **Hierarchy:** Management coordination through shared management chains
- **Lateral processes:** Cross-functional teams, working groups, integration roles
- **Standards and processes:** Shared processes, governance frameworks, and tooling that create consistency without requiring constant negotiation
- **Information systems:** Common data, reporting, and transparency mechanisms

### 3. Sizing: spans of control and layers

**Span of control** (number of direct reports per manager) affects both cost and management quality. Wider spans reduce management layers and cost, but exceed managers' capacity to develop and support their teams when too wide. Typical effective spans: 6-8 for knowledge workers in complex roles; 10-15 for more structured operational roles.

**Management layers** affect organizational speed and information fidelity. Each layer adds latency to decisions and attenuation to signals. Most knowledge-work organizations benefit from fewer than six layers from CEO to front-line contributor. More than six typically indicates organizational complexity that is hurting speed.

### 4. Decision rights: who decides what

An organization with clear structure but ambiguous decision rights will be slow and political. Decision rights design specifies: who has authority to decide, who must be consulted, who must be informed, and who can veto. RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) is the common framework.

## Design Process

### Start with strategy

Before drawing the org chart, articulate what the organization must be good at to execute its strategy. What capabilities are core? What coordination needs are most critical? What does the organization need to do faster or better than it currently does?

Strategy inputs shape the design criteria. Without design criteria, structural choices are arbitrary.

### Design criteria

Design criteria are the specific requirements that the new organization must meet. Examples:

- "Product teams must have end-to-end accountability for a product's performance without cross-functional approval chains for routine decisions"
- "Engineering talent must have a career path that does not require moving into management"
- "Customer success and product must be able to coordinate on roadmap without a formal committee"

Design criteria are used to evaluate design options, not as outputs of the design process.

### Generate and evaluate options

For any significant design question, generate at least two genuine alternatives before selecting one. Evaluate each against the design criteria. Where a design option fails a criterion, identify whether the failure is fatal or mitigable.

### Avoid common structural errors

**Designing around individuals:** Structure designed to accommodate current incumbents rather than future work requirements creates rigidity and perpetuates the wrong role designs as leaders change.

**Over-designing at the macro level while under-designing at the team level:** Enterprise-level restructuring is visible and politically significant, but execution happens at the team and role level. Macro structure change without attention to role clarity, team-level decision rights, and individual level job architecture rarely produces the intended outcomes.

**Confusing restructuring with organizational change:** Changing the boxes on the org chart is not the same as changing how work gets done. Structural change without process change, culture change, and capability investment typically fails to produce the strategic outcome it was designed for.

## Job Architecture

Job architecture is the systematic design of roles across the organization — defining levels, titles, scope, and compensation ranges in a way that creates consistency, transparency, and a coherent career framework.

### Why job architecture matters

Without a consistent job architecture:
- Compensation decisions are made individually, creating pay equity problems
- Titles are inconsistent and meaningless as signals of scope or seniority
- Career paths are opaque and perceived as political
- Span and layer analysis is impossible

### Job architecture components

**Career tracks:** Distinct paths within a function — typically a management track (leading people and teams) and an individual contributor track (deepening expertise without managing people). Not all functions need both tracks; most professional functions do.

**Levels:** A small number of defined levels with clear scope criteria. Typically 4-6 levels is sufficient for most functions. More than 6 creates grade inflation pressure.

**Level criteria:** For each level, what does the work scope look like, what is the expected impact, and what is the decision authority? Criteria should be specific enough to distinguish adjacent levels.

**Compensation ranges:** A defined range (typically a band spanning 30-50% from minimum to maximum) for each level, calibrated to market data for that role category.
