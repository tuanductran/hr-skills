# Designing Spans, Layers, and Operating Models

## Overview

Organizational design decisions — how many layers exist between the CEO and frontline employees, how wide each manager's span of control is, how decision rights are distributed — shape organizational speed and cost more than almost any other structural lever, and yet they're frequently made informally, one hire and one promotion at a time, rather than through deliberate design. By the time the resulting structure is visibly dysfunctional (decision bottlenecks, excessive management layers, unclear accountability), unwinding it is far more disruptive than designing it well from the start.

## Spans of Control

Span of control — the number of direct reports a manager has — should be set deliberately based on the nature of the work being managed, not a single company-wide default:

| Work type | Typical sustainable span |
|---|---|
| Highly standardized, low-complexity work | 10–15+ |
| Moderately complex, some judgment required | 6–9 |
| Highly complex, coaching-intensive, or novel work | 3–6 |

Spans that are too narrow create unnecessary management layers and slow decision-making; spans that are too wide leave managers unable to provide adequate coaching and oversight, particularly for complex or judgment-intensive work. Set spans based on actual role complexity and required coaching intensity, not a uniform ratio applied across dissimilar functions.

## Layers and Decision Velocity

Every additional organizational layer adds a step to decision paths and typically slows execution, so layers should be added deliberately, only when justified by genuine coordination or coaching need — not as a default reward for growth or a way to create promotion opportunities.

- Count actual layers between the CEO and frontline individual contributors as a diagnostic — organizations frequently discover more layers than leadership assumed once mapped explicitly
- Evaluate each layer against a specific question: what decision or coordination function does this layer perform that couldn't be handled by the layer above or below it?
- Be especially skeptical of layers created primarily to provide a promotion path rather than to serve a genuine coordination need — this is one of the most common sources of unnecessary layering

## Choosing an Operating Model

| Model | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Functional | Deep expertise, clear career paths within function | Cross-functional coordination requires more deliberate mechanisms |
| Divisional / business unit | Strong accountability for business outcomes | Potential duplication of functional capability across units |
| Matrix | Balances functional expertise with business-unit focus | Dual reporting lines create accountability ambiguity if not carefully designed |
| Network / flat | High speed and autonomy at small scale | Coordination becomes difficult as headcount grows without added structure |

The right model depends on the balance between functional expertise depth and business-unit accountability the organization actually needs — there is no universally superior model, only one that fits the current stage and strategy better or worse.

## Building RACI Clarity

Restructuring often changes reporting lines without clarifying decision rights, which leaves the new structure formally in place but functionally ambiguous. A RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) exercise for key decisions and processes should accompany any structural change:

- Limit "Accountable" to exactly one role per decision — shared accountability in a RACI is a common design flaw that reproduces the same ambiguity the exercise is meant to resolve
- Focus RACI mapping on decisions that actually caused friction under the prior structure, rather than exhaustively mapping every process, which produces an unused document
- Revisit RACI assignments after the new structure has been operating for a few months — some decision-rights assumptions made during design won't hold up against real operating conditions

## Managing the Restructuring Process

- Sequence the design work before the announcement — a structure change communicated before the details (roles, reporting lines, transition plan) are settled creates prolonged uncertainty that damages morale and retention more than the change itself
- Identify and address role redundancies and gaps explicitly as part of the design, rather than assuming the new structure will resolve them organically after go-live
- Build a transition plan with a defined timeline for reaching the target state — restructurings that linger in a partial, ambiguous state for months compound the disruption without delivering the intended benefit

## Common Pitfalls

- **Redesigning structure without redesigning decision rights**, which leaves the org chart different but the actual way decisions get made unchanged.
- **Applying a uniform span-of-control target across functions with very different work complexity.**
- **Underestimating the transition cost** of a restructuring relative to its long-term benefit, particularly for changes driven more by cost-cutting than genuine structural misalignment.
