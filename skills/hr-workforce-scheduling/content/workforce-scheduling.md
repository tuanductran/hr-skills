# Workforce Scheduling

## Overview

Workforce scheduling matches labor supply (available employees) to labor demand (forecasted workload) while respecting legal rest requirements, cost targets, and employee preferences.

## Main topics

### Demand forecasting

Good scheduling starts with a demand forecast: historical transaction/volume data, seasonality, and known events (promotions, holidays) are used to predict how many staff are needed in each time block.

### Shift patterns

Common patterns include fixed shifts, rotating shifts (e.g., DuPont, Pitman, 2-2-3), and 4-on-3-off patterns for 24/7 operations. The right pattern balances coverage continuity with employee fatigue and preference.

### Predictive scheduling and rest-period law

Many jurisdictions require advance notice of schedules (e.g., 14 days) and predictability pay for last-minute changes, plus minimum rest periods between shifts ("clopening" restrictions).

### Shift swaps and open shifts

A clear swap policy defines who can initiate a swap, approval requirements, and deadlines, while an open-shift process lets qualified employees claim unfilled shifts, often through a scheduling system.

### On-call and standby

On-call policies define response-time expectations, compensation for standby time, and escalation if the on-call person doesn't respond.

## Practical guidance

- Use historical volume data, not gut feel, to size shifts.
- Build buffer capacity for expected absenteeism rather than scheduling to the exact headcount.
- Give employees visibility into schedules and swap status through a shared system.
- Review schedules regularly against actual demand to refine the forecast model.

## Conclusion

Effective workforce scheduling reduces both under- and over-staffing costs, keeps organizations compliant with scheduling law, and improves employee satisfaction through fair, predictable shift assignment.
