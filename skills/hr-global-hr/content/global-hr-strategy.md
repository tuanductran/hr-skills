# Global HR Strategy and Operations

## Overview

Global HR (also called International HR or IHRM) is the practice of managing human resources across multiple countries and cultures — navigating legal differences, compensation structures, tax implications, employment laws, and cultural norms. It is one of the most complex HR domains because there is no single "right way" — every country has different rules, and organizations often have different strategies for different regions.

Global HR is not just HR translated to different geographies. It requires understanding how to build consistency across organizations while respecting and adapting to local context.

## Global HR Strategy

A global HR strategy defines how an organization will approach talent, culture, and people operations across its footprint. Key decisions:

**Centralized vs. decentralized:** How much HR is managed from headquarters versus delegated to local entities? Centralization provides consistency and cost efficiency; decentralization enables local responsiveness but risks inconsistency.

**Standardization vs. localization:** How much should policies, compensation, and benefits be identical globally versus adapted to each market? Example: some organizations have uniform salary bands globally; others have local bands pegged to local markets. Both can work; the choice depends on strategy.

**Talent philosophy:** Is the organization hiring and developing globally (building a global talent pool that moves across geographies) or locally (hiring in each country for local roles)? This affects everything from relocation policies to executive development to succession planning.

**Culture:** How do you maintain organizational culture across different national cultures? Some approaches: shared values with local expression, explicit culture training for new hires, deliberate role modeling from leaders, off-sites that bring people together.

## Country-Specific Compliance

Every country has labor law, employment law, tax law, and benefits law that constrains HR decisions. The complexity of global HR is largely about understanding these constraints.

### Key compliance domains by country

**Employment law:** How long is the notice period for termination? Can you terminate for performance reasons, or only for cause? What is the definition of "cause"? Does the country have a maximum working hours? Are there mandatory breaks?

**Compensation and payroll:** What is the income tax structure? Are there employer contributions to pensions, social insurance, health insurance? What is the minimum wage? Are there mandatory bonuses or profit-sharing schemes?

**Benefits:** What benefits are legally mandatory (health insurance, pension, unemployment insurance)? What is the standard for voluntary benefits? What is expected for maternity/paternity leave?

**Work authorization:** What visas or work permits are required for foreign nationals? How long does it take to obtain them? What are the salary requirements for work visa eligibility?

Examples of real complexity:
- Germany has strong co-determination laws requiring works councils on major HR decisions
- France has very generous severance requirements and making redundancies is extremely expensive
- Vietnam's labor law requires contracts to be in Vietnamese; bonuses follow specific formulas
- The US has at-will employment; most of Europe requires cause
- Singapore has mandatory central provident fund contributions; the US has optional 401(k)

Organizations operating globally typically:
- Hire local employment law firms to advise on country-specific requirements
- Maintain a compliance playbook or matrix documenting requirements by country
- Centralize policy-setting but delegate compliance interpretation to regional leaders

## Building a Global Compensation and Benefits Strategy

Compensation and benefits are often where global HR complexity surfaces most acutely.

### Approaches to global compensation

**Global grades with local market adjustments:** Define job levels and salary bands globally, but adjust for local market rates. A Level 4 Software Engineer in San Francisco earns more than a Level 4 in Mexico City, but both are Level 4.

Advantage: consistency and portability (moving between geographies is straightforward). Challenge: perceived unfairness when colleagues in cheaper markets earn less for the same work.

**Local market rates:** Set compensation based on what the local market pays for each role. Maximum local competitiveness; maximum complexity in managing cross-border equity.

**Third country national approaches:** For expats and international hires, use a separate compensation structure — sometimes an "expat package" with allowances on top of base salary. Used for mobility during assignments.

### Benefits localization

Few benefits are truly global because tax and regulatory structures vary so much. Common approaches:

**Mandatory benefits:** Comply with local requirements (pensions, social insurance, health insurance). These are non-negotiable and typically the biggest benefit cost.

**Voluntary benefits:** Offer additional benefits that employees can choose. Examples: supplemental health insurance, life insurance, wellness programs. These are more portable across geographies.

**Core vs. periphery:** Define a set of core benefits (health, retirement, time off) that you commit to providing globally; then customize periphery benefits locally. Example: everyone gets health insurance; locally what that looks like varies. Everyone gets 20 days of leave; whether that includes holidays varies by country.

## Managing Global Talent Movement

Global organizations benefit from moving talent across geographies — for development, succession planning, or to meet business needs. But international assignment is expensive and complex.

### International assignment costs and components

**Direct costs:** Relocation, housing, education (for children), travel home. These are large one-time costs.

**Ongoing assignment costs:** Cost of living differences, education allowances, home country leave, assignment premiums. These compound over time.

**Indirect costs:** Time away from family, career disruption, administrative overhead. These are real but harder to quantify.

A 2-3 year assignment abroad typically costs $300K-$500K+ for a senior professional in developed countries.

### When to assign vs. hire locally

Assignment is justified when:
- Building a global talent pool is strategic (want future leaders to have international experience)
- Transferring critical knowledge or organizational culture
- Filling a gap that cannot be filled with local hiring

Assignment should not be used just because it's easier than building local capability, or as a development opportunity if local hiring would be more cost-effective.

### Assignment management

For organizations that do international assignments regularly:
- Tax equalization (the organization bears the tax burden, not the employee)
- Policy clarity on assignment length, roles, and return expectations
- Family support (counseling, school search, spouse career support)
- Clear repatriation plan — what happens when the assignment ends?

Repatriation is often mismanaged — assignment ends, person returns to headquarters, no clear role, person leaves. This wastes the investment made in the assignment and damages retention.

## Global HR Governance and Culture

Operating across multiple countries requires structures to make consistent decisions while respecting local context.

**Global HR leadership:** CHRO or Global Head of HR at corporate office, with regional HR leaders (APAC, EMEA, Americas) reporting up.

**Governance mechanisms:** Quarterly global HR forums to discuss policy, compliance, and talent issues. Clear escalation paths for decisions that require global coordination.

**Culture and values:** Organizations often invest significant energy in transmitting culture globally — explicit onboarding on company values, regular communication from senior leadership, all-hands meetings (increasingly virtual), team rituals, off-sites.

The goal is belonging and alignment despite geographic distance.
