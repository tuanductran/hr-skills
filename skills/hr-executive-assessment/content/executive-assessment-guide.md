# Executive Assessment

## Overview

Executive assessment is the structured evaluation of senior leader candidates — typically director level and above — for hiring, promotion, or development. It goes beyond standard competency interviews to assess leadership capability, risk profile, and organizational fit at a level that routine interview processes cannot reliably surface.

The stakes justify rigor. A failed executive hire costs 200%+ of annual compensation plus organizational disruption and opportunity cost.

## Multi-Method Assessment Design

No single assessment method reliably predicts executive performance. Effective executive assessment combines multiple methods to triangulate:

**Structured behavioral interviews:** Probe decision-making in high-stakes, ambiguous situations. Executives are skilled at speaking in principles — "I believe in empowering teams" — rather than evidence. Press for specific examples: "Tell me about a specific situation where a team member you were responsible for was significantly underperforming. What did you do, and what happened?"

**Strategic exercise or business case:** A prepared presentation or case that assesses strategic thinking, communication to senior audiences, and how the candidate structures complex problems. Calibrate to actual role requirements — a CHRO candidate's exercise should involve people strategy, not financial modeling.

**Enhanced reference checks:** Go beyond the candidate's provided references. Use your network to reach people not on the reference list, particularly former direct reports. How a leader treated the people below them is the most revealing dimension and the most commonly concealed in provided references.

**Psychometric assessment:** Personality instruments (Hogan, Predictive Index) and cognitive assessments add structured data when interpreted by a trained assessor with role context. Most useful as one data point among several, not as a primary decision driver.

## Derailment Risk Assessment

Executive assessment must explicitly evaluate derailment risk — the factors that most commonly cause capable executives to fail: excessive ego, low empathy for direct reports, resistance to feedback, poor political judgment, and ethical corner-cutting under pressure.

Derailment risk is rarely visible at an executive's best. It surfaces under stress, in conflict, and when authority is challenged. Reference calls specifically designed to probe these situations are the most reliable source.

Questions designed for derailment risk:
- "Tell me about a time when this executive received feedback they disagreed with. How did they respond?"
- "Describe the executive's behavior when a significant project went wrong."
- "How did they handle a direct report who was not performing?"
- "Would you work for them again? Without hesitation, or with reservations?"

## Executive Onboarding Risk

Assessment findings should directly inform onboarding. If assessment identifies a development need (e.g., a strong strategic thinker who has not previously built a team from scratch), the onboarding plan should address it explicitly — not wait for it to surface as a performance problem six months in.

High-risk executive onboarding periods: the first 90 days (cultural adjustment), the 6-month mark (honeymoon period ends, organizational resistance surfaces), and the 12-month mark (strategy is set and early performance is visible). Design check-in touchpoints at each.
