# Assessing Executive Candidates for Fit and Derailment Risk

## Overview

Executive assessment differs from standard candidate evaluation in a specific way: at senior levels, the cost of a bad hire is dramatically higher, the skills being evaluated are harder to observe directly in a standard interview process, and derailment risk — traits that predict failure under pressure, not just capability under normal conditions — becomes a central concern in a way it rarely is for individual-contributor hiring.

## Assessing Capability Beyond Track Record

A strong track record at a prior company doesn't automatically transfer to a different organizational context, and assessment should probe this transferability explicitly rather than treating past results as sufficient evidence:

- Distinguish results the candidate directly drove from results attributable to favorable market conditions, a strong existing team, or organizational tailwinds they inherited rather than built — this distinction is often more revealing than the headline result itself
- Probe for how the candidate operated during difficult periods, not just successful ones — resilience and judgment under genuine pressure are harder to fake in a structured conversation than a polished success narrative
- Assess fit between the candidate's demonstrated leadership style and what this specific organizational context and stage actually require, since a style that succeeded in one context (e.g., a mature, process-heavy organization) may not translate to a different one (e.g., an early-stage, ambiguous environment)

## Derailment Risk Assessment

Derailment risk refers to leadership traits or tendencies that predict failure specifically under stress or at higher levels of authority, often traits that were invisible or even advantageous at earlier career stages:

- Common derailment patterns include an inability to adapt leadership style as scope grows, difficulty building genuine trust and delegation as span of control increases, and a track record of strong individual execution without a corresponding pattern of developing others
- Assess derailment risk through structured behavioral evidence — specific examples of how the candidate handled setbacks, conflict, or a team failure — rather than relying on general impression from a polished interview presence, which correlates weakly with actual derailment risk
- Weight reference checks heavily for derailment signal specifically, since peers and former direct reports often observe derailment patterns that don't surface in a standard interview process and that the candidate themselves may be unaware of

## Structuring the Assessment Process

- Use multiple assessors across different interaction contexts (board-level conversation, working session with peers, structured behavioral interview) rather than relying on a single assessor's impression, since executive presence can create a strong but potentially misleading first impression
- Build a structured rubric specific to the leadership competencies this role and organizational context require, rather than a generic executive competency framework applied without adaptation
- Where the stakes justify it, incorporate a validated leadership assessment instrument alongside structured interviews and reference checks — a single method, however well-executed, is less reliable than triangulated methods for senior-level assessment

## Board and Stakeholder Involvement

For C-suite and board-level roles, assessment typically involves stakeholders beyond the standard hiring team:

- Clarify each stakeholder's role in the assessment process upfront — who provides input, who makes the final decision, and how disagreement between stakeholders will be resolved — to avoid a fragmented or contradictory process
- Prepare board members or senior stakeholders participating in assessment with structured guidance on what to probe for, since without this, senior assessors often default to informal, unstructured conversation that provides weaker signal than a standard structured interview
- Maintain confidentiality discipline appropriate to executive search throughout the assessment process, consistent with retained search practices

## Communicating Assessment Findings

- Present findings with specific behavioral evidence supporting each conclusion, not just an overall favorable or unfavorable impression — this is especially important at the executive level, where the decision typically involves multiple stakeholders who need to evaluate the reasoning independently
- Be explicit about areas of genuine uncertainty or unresolved risk in the assessment, rather than presenting a falsely confident recommendation — executive hiring decisions benefit from honest calibration of confidence, given the stakes involved
- Distinguish capability findings from cultural or organizational fit findings clearly, since conflating the two can obscure which specific concern, if any, should actually drive a hesitation about the candidate

## Common Pitfalls

- **Relying primarily on interview presence and polish**, which correlates weakly with actual leadership effectiveness and derailment risk.
- **Skipping structured derailment-specific assessment**, treating executive evaluation as simply a higher-stakes version of standard candidate assessment.
- **Under-preparing board members or senior stakeholders** participating in the assessment, resulting in unstructured impressions rather than genuine structured signal.
