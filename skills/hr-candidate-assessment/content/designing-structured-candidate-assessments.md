# Designing Structured Candidate Assessments

## Overview

Unstructured interviews are consistently among the weakest predictors of on-the-job performance in the selection research literature, despite being the most widely used method. Structured assessments — work samples, skills tests, simulations, and assessment centers — predict performance substantially better because they directly measure the capability the role requires, under conditions closer to the actual job, rather than relying on a candidate's ability to talk about their capability.

The design challenge is building assessments that are predictive, fair, and proportionate to the role — an assessment process that's too heavy discourages strong candidates from completing it, while one that's too light adds process burden without improving decision quality.

## Choosing an Assessment Method

| Method | Best predicts | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| Work sample / job simulation | Direct task performance | Time-intensive to design and score; must closely mirror actual job tasks |
| Skills test (technical or cognitive) | Domain-specific capability | Risk of testing knowledge that's easily looked up rather than applied skill |
| Assessment center (multiple exercises, often with observers) | Complex, multi-dimensional roles (leadership, cross-functional coordination) | High cost and candidate time investment; only justified for senior or high-stakes roles |
| Structured scenario / case interview | Judgment and problem-solving approach | Scoring consistency depends heavily on well-designed rubrics and trained assessors |

Match assessment weight to role seniority and hiring volume — a lightweight skills test suits high-volume early-career hiring, while an assessment center is justified only for senior or highly consequential roles where the cost is proportionate to the decision's stakes.

## Building Predictive Work Samples

An effective work sample closely mirrors a real, representative task from the actual job — not a generic industry exercise. Design principles:

- **Source the task from job analysis**, not from a template — the closer the exercise matches actual job content, the stronger its predictive validity
- **Time-box realistically.** An exercise that takes far longer than a comparable real task either signals poor design or unfairly disadvantages candidates with less time availability, which can itself introduce bias against candidates balancing other responsibilities
- **Provide the same context a real employee would have**, including relevant constraints — an artificially unconstrained exercise doesn't reflect how the role actually operates

## Building Scoring Rubrics

A rubric is what makes an assessment structured rather than subjectively evaluated in disguise. Effective rubrics:

- Define 3–5 specific, observable criteria tied directly to what the exercise is meant to measure, not generic traits like "communication skills" applied without specifics
- Use anchored scale descriptions for each score level ("a 4 looks like..." rather than a bare numeric scale), which is what most improves inter-rater consistency
- Are calibrated across assessors before live use — have multiple raters independently score the same sample responses and reconcile disagreements before using the rubric on real candidates

## Assessment Centers for Senior Roles

Assessment centers combine multiple exercises (simulations, structured interviews, group exercises) observed by multiple trained assessors, and are justified primarily for senior or highly consequential hires where the cost is proportionate:

- Use multiple assessors across exercises and average or discuss ratings rather than relying on a single observer's overall impression, which reintroduces the same bias structured methods are meant to reduce
- Train assessors specifically on the rubrics being used — untrained observers in an assessment center setting revert to unstructured, intuition-based judgment despite the structured format
- Separate assessor roles from advocacy roles — an assessor who is also the hiring manager championing a candidate has a harder time rating objectively

## Fairness and Adverse Impact Review

Any assessment used at scale should be periodically reviewed for adverse impact across demographic groups where legally permissible to analyze:

- Track pass rates by group at each assessment stage, not just overall hire outcomes
- Where a disparity appears, investigate whether the assessment content itself (not just candidate pool composition) may be contributing, and revise accordingly
- Validate that assessment content doesn't inadvertently test for skills unrelated to actual job performance — a common and often unintentional source of adverse impact

## Common Pitfalls

- **Using an off-the-shelf assessment without validating it against this organization's actual roles.** Generic assessments predict generic job performance; validated, role-specific assessments predict better.
- **Skipping rater calibration**, which lets a nominally "structured" assessment drift back into inconsistent, intuition-driven scoring.
- **Over-testing for entry-level or high-volume roles**, where a lengthy assessment center is disproportionate and drives strong candidates to withdraw before completing the process.
