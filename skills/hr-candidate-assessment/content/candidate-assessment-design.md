# Candidate Assessment Design

## Overview

Most interview processes are theater. They feel rigorous but produce little predictive validity — meaning the candidates who interview well are not reliably the same as the candidates who perform well. The research on unstructured interviews is clear: they have poor predictive validity and are highly susceptible to interviewer bias.

Effective candidate assessment replaces opinion with structure: defined competencies, consistent questions, calibrated scoring, and evidence-based decisions. This guide covers how to build an assessment framework that actually predicts job performance.

## What Actually Predicts Job Performance

Decades of personnel psychology research identifies the assessment methods with the highest predictive validity for job performance (measured by correlation with actual performance ratings):

| Method | Validity (approximate) |
|---|---|
| Work sample tests | High |
| Structured interviews (behavioral + situational) | Moderate-high |
| General cognitive ability tests | Moderate-high |
| Structured peer/panel assessments | Moderate |
| Unstructured interviews | Low |
| Resume screening alone | Low |
| Personality tests (depends heavily on test quality) | Variable |

The implication: if your process is primarily unstructured interviews and resume screening, it is a costly exercise in confirming interviewer instincts rather than a genuine prediction of performance.

## Defining Assessment Dimensions

Before designing any assessment activity, define what you are assessing. Assessment dimensions should be:

- **Job-relevant:** Directly connected to the work the person will do and the competencies the role requires
- **Observable:** Assessable through evidence, not inference about personality or fit
- **Differentiating:** Dimensions on which strong and weak candidates actually differ

A common mistake is including dimensions that apply to everyone ("communication skills," "teamwork") without specifying what excellent versus poor looks like for this specific role at this seniority level.

### Typical assessment dimension categories

**Technical competency:** Domain knowledge and skill directly required for the role (engineering, data analysis, financial modeling, etc.)

**Execution and problem-solving:** How the candidate approaches complex, ambiguous problems and drives to outcomes

**Leadership and influence:** For leadership roles — how they build teams, make decisions, and operate across organizational levels

**Communication and collaboration:** Role-specific communication demands (executive communication, cross-functional stakeholder management, frontline team management)

**Values and culture alignment:** Specific, observable behaviors that reflect organizational values — not "are they a culture fit" (which is code for demographic similarity)

## Structured Interview Design

A structured interview uses the same questions for all candidates for a role, in the same sequence, with a defined scoring rubric. This enables comparison across candidates and reduces the influence of interview style and chemistry.

### Behavioral questions

Based on the premise that past behavior predicts future behavior. Ask candidates to describe a specific situation they faced, the action they took, and the outcome.

Format: "Tell me about a time when [competency-relevant situation]. What was the situation, what did you do, and what was the result?"

Good behavioral questions:
- Have a specific competency they are designed to assess
- Require concrete examples, not hypothetical answers
- Have a follow-up sequence that probes depth ("What was the most difficult part?" / "What would you do differently?")

### Situational questions

Present a hypothetical scenario relevant to the role and ask how the candidate would respond.

Format: "Imagine you are [scenario relevant to the role]. How would you approach this?"

Situational questions are useful when candidates do not have directly relevant prior experience, or when you want to assess judgment in a context specific to your organization.

### Scoring rubrics

Every structured interview question should have a defined scoring rubric — what a strong, acceptable, and weak answer looks like for this question at this role level. Without rubrics, scoring defaults to gut feel.

Build rubrics collaboratively with hiring managers before the first interview, not after candidates have been seen.

## Assessment Design by Stage

Assessment should be staged to eliminate candidates efficiently rather than putting all assessment effort into the final round.

### Screen (phone/video, 30 minutes)

Goal: confirm minimum qualifications, assess basic communication, identify obvious mismatches.

Assess: career narrative coherence, minimum technical requirements, compensation alignment, availability.

Do not: conduct a deep technical assessment or full competency evaluation in the screen.

### Technical / domain assessment (60-90 minutes, or async)

Goal: evaluate technical or domain competency with a work sample or structured exercise.

Best practice: use a task representative of real work, not a trivia quiz. A take-home assignment or live coding exercise on a realistic problem is more predictive than whiteboard algorithm questions for most engineering roles.

Time limit and scope: respect candidates' time. A take-home assessment requiring 3+ hours is unreasonable for candidates with competing processes and full-time jobs.

### Competency interview (45-60 minutes per interviewer)

Goal: assess behavioral competencies defined in the assessment framework.

Structure: assign each interviewer one or two competency dimensions. No two interviewers should assess the same dimension (this wastes panels and creates conflicting evidence rather than additional evidence).

### Final / panel (for leadership roles)

Goal: senior stakeholder assessment of strategic thinking, executive presence, and cultural alignment.

Do not use the final round to repeat competency assessment already completed. Use it for dimensions only observable at the leadership interaction level.

## Calibration and Decision-Making

The debrief is where assessment data becomes a hiring decision. Without structure, debriefs devolve into the most senior person in the room's opinion winning.

### Structured debrief format

1. Each interviewer shares their evidence (specific examples from the interview) before sharing their recommendation. This prevents anchoring on the first person's opinion.
2. Review ratings by dimension, not overall impression.
3. Identify dimensions with divergent ratings and discuss the underlying evidence.
4. Make the hire/no-hire decision based on assessment against the defined criteria.

### Decision documentation

Document the reasoning for the decision — what evidence supported the hire recommendation, and if a candidate was not selected, what specific gaps drove the decision. This creates accountability and data for quality-of-hire analysis.
