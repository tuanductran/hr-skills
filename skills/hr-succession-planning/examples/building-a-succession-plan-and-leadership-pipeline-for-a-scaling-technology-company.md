# Building a Succession Plan and Leadership Pipeline for a Scaling Technology Company

## Context

You are the HR Business Partner for a 280-person technology company that has just
completed a Series C funding round. The new investors have asked the board to see a
formal succession plan for CEO and C-suite roles within 90 days as part of their
governance requirements. Simultaneously, the Head of Engineering — one of the company's
most critical technical leaders — privately told the CPO last week that she is considering
leaving within the next 12 months. Two potential internal successors for the Head of
Engineering role exist but neither has a development plan, and neither knows they are
being considered. You have been asked to build the succession plan for investors, manage
the Head of Engineering retention risk, and establish a pipeline development process
across the company — starting essentially from no formal succession planning structure.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "We have 90 days to deliver a board-level succession plan for investors, a potential
> departure in one of our most critical roles, and no succession infrastructure at all.
> Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a hard governance deadline, an active flight risk in a critical
role, and no existing process — start with two priority areas in parallel:

**Retention risk containment (the Head of Engineering concern):**

- Have a direct retention conversation with the Head of Engineering before any succession
  work begins — losing her while building the succession plan would create exactly the
  crisis the plan is meant to prevent
- Identify whether her departure consideration is role-specific, compensation-related, or
  a signal about the company's direction after the funding round
- Move quickly — the longer a named flight risk goes without a retention response, the
  more the intention solidifies

**Succession scope and critical role mapping (the investor requirement):**

- Translate the investor's governance requirement into a concrete scope before building
  anything — a full company succession plan in 90 days is not realistic; a C-suite and
  critical role plan is
- Identify the 6–8 most critical roles by business continuity risk, not just by
  seniority, to anchor the plan in something defensible
- Map current successor availability for each role before the 9-box session — walking
  into a calibration without pre-work data is the most common reason talent reviews
  produce incomplete pipeline outputs

The investor timeline and the Head of Engineering risk are not separate problems — they
are both symptoms of a company that has scaled without succession infrastructure. You need
a concrete sequencing answer before either deadline compounds the other.

> **Avoid:** Do not begin with a company-wide talent review before defining the critical
> role scope. A 280-person company attempting to calibrate every employee in a single
> talent review before a governance deadline will produce rushed, low-quality pipeline
> data that will not hold up to board scrutiny.

## Step 2 — Mapping Critical Roles and Coverage Gaps

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me map the most critical roles and assess our current succession coverage before
> we build anything else, since we are starting from zero."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Seniority-based scope (C-suite only):**

```txt
Scope: CEO, CPO, CTO, CFO, CMO — 5 roles
Coverage today: Likely 0 named successors with documented readiness
Trade-off: Meets the minimum governance requirement quickly, but
           misses critical operational roles like Head of Engineering
           whose departure would create immediate delivery risk
```

**Option B — Business continuity risk-based scope (C-suite plus critical operational roles):**

```txt
Scope: C-suite (5) + critical operational roles by impact
       (Head of Engineering, VP Sales, Head of Data — 3 additional)
Coverage today: Likely mixed — some organic internal candidates exist
               without documentation; others have genuine gaps
Trade-off: More representative of actual organizational risk, stronger
           for investor and board credibility, requires more pre-work
           but produces a defensible plan
```

**Option C — Full company calibration (all roles above manager level):**

```txt
Scope: All director-level and above roles, estimated 35–40 roles
Coverage today: Unknown — no calibration data exists yet
Trade-off: Comprehensive in theory, but produces low-quality data
           if rushed to meet a 90-day deadline with no prior
           succession infrastructure
```

**Benchmark context:**

A healthy succession coverage ratio targets at least two to three named successors per
critical role, each with a distinct time-to-readiness rating. Starting with Option B
allows the company to present a credible investor report on the eight highest-risk roles
while building the infrastructure needed to extend the scope in future cycles.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the investor deadline and the Head of Engineering
> flight risk, Option B best balances governance credibility with realistic scope for a
> 90-day build. Begin with the C-suite plus the three highest-continuity-risk operational
> roles, and include the Head of Engineering succession scenario explicitly as evidence
> that the plan addresses real current risk, not just theoretical future scenarios.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Head of Engineering Retention Risk

**Sample prompt:**

> "The Head of Engineering told the CPO she may leave within 12 months. I need to
> understand what is really driving this before I build a succession plan around her
> departure as an assumption."

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming departure is inevitable, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Role and scope concerns:**

- Is the Head of Engineering's consideration driven by feeling her role is constrained
  or undervalued post-funding round, as the company brings in senior hires above her?
- Is there a specific mandate or scope expansion she was expecting that has not
  materialized since the Series C closed?

**2. Compensation and recognition gaps:**

- Has her compensation been reviewed relative to market since the funding round, given
  that Series C companies typically experience significant talent market competition?
- Is this primarily a financial consideration, or is compensation a proxy for feeling
  undervalued relative to her impact?

**3. Direction and culture concerns:**

- Does she see a clear role for herself in the company's next chapter, or does the
  funding and growth plan feel like a trajectory that moves away from what she values?
- Has the relationship between her and the CPO or CEO changed since the round closed?

**4. Individual conversation specifics:**

- Has anyone had a direct, structured retention conversation with her, or was the
  CPO's conversation an informal mention without a formal follow-up commitment?
- Are there two or three specific changes that, if made, would materially shift her
  intention — or is this a decision that is largely already made?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present her potential departure as an
> inevitable succession trigger. Bring a clear picture of which concerns are addressable
> through concrete action (scope, compensation, clarity of role in the growth plan)
> versus which reflect a decision that is already further along than the single comment
> suggested.

## Step 4 — Running the Talent Review and 9-Box Calibration

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me run a talent review and 9-box calibration for our C-suite and critical
> operational roles, since we have no prior calibration data and need a defensible
> pipeline output in 90 days."

**Skill response — calibration design:**

| Calibration Stage | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Critical role scope defined | Not yet — scope has not been agreed | Align with CEO and CPO on the 8 critical roles before scheduling any calibration |
| Pre-work data collected | Not yet — no prior performance or potential ratings exist | Gather last 12 months of performance review data and manager nominations before the session |
| Calibration facilitation designed | Not yet | Design a structured facilitation with bias-reduction anchors, not an open discussion |
| Successor readiness rated | Not yet | Use a consistent time-to-readiness framework (Ready Now / 1–2 Years / 3+ Years) per candidate |
| Development plans built | Not yet | Assign development plan ownership immediately after calibration, before the session output decays |

**Calibration session structure (recommended for this context):**

1. Pre-work: each participating leader submits performance and potential ratings for their direct reports one week before the session
2. Opening anchor: facilitator presents the 9-box definition with behavioral examples to normalize ratings across leaders
3. Individual calibration: each nominee is discussed for 5–8 minutes; facilitator surfaces discrepancies between manager ratings
4. Succession mapping: identified high-potentials are mapped to critical roles with time-to-readiness ratings
5. Action assignment: each named successor receives a development plan owner and a 30-day development conversation commitment

**Stakeholder-specific sequencing:**

1. CEO and CPO — align on critical role scope and calibration process before involving the broader leadership team
2. Leadership team — calibration session participation with structured facilitation, not open discussion
3. Named successors — development conversations sequenced by sensitivity, with the Head of Engineering's potential successors prioritized given the retention context

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Running a 9-box calibration as an open room discussion
> without a structured facilitation anchor. Without behavioral definitions and a
> facilitator actively managing recency bias and personal favoritism, the output
> reflects who spoke loudest in the room, not a consistent pipeline assessment.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Succession Plan to the Board

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to present the succession plan to the board and investors at the 90-day mark.
> How do I frame it given we are building this from scratch and cannot pretend we have
> years of pipeline data?"

**Skill response — board communication framework:**

Boards respond to a credible, honest plan that demonstrates awareness of risk and a
concrete action in motion, rather than a polished document that overstates readiness.
Structure the presentation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Affirm the governance commitment with a concrete plan:**

"We have completed a critical role mapping and talent calibration covering the eight
highest-continuity-risk roles in the company. We have identified named successors with
time-to-readiness ratings for each, and development plans are now active for five
successor candidates across three roles."

**Part 2 — Name the current risk directly:**

"Two of the eight critical roles have a single named successor at the 2-year readiness
threshold, which represents our most significant pipeline exposure. We are addressing
this through an accelerated external pipeline build for one role and an internal
development acceleration program for the other. We are also managing an active retention
situation for the Head of Engineering role, which is included in the plan."

**Part 3 — Present the forward plan and ask for board support:**

"We are recommending a quarterly pipeline review cadence going forward, with a full
calibration refresh annually. We are asking the board to support a leadership development
budget of [X] to fund the stretch assignment and external development investments the
plan requires. Without that investment, the time-to-readiness ratings for our current
successor candidates will not improve."

> **Key principle:** Boards respond well to a succession plan that shows honest risk
> assessment and a concrete action plan rather than an optimistic coverage grid. Frame
> gaps as known and managed, not hidden.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Succession Documentation

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft development plans for the two Head of Engineering successor candidates
> and the board succession report. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without
> producing something generic?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the critical role requirements, the successor candidate's current strengths and
development gaps, the time-to-readiness rating, and the specific experiences or
exposures most likely to accelerate readiness before drafting anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am an HR Business Partner building succession documentation for
a 280-person Series C technology company.

Context:
- Critical role: Head of Engineering (technical leadership,
  cross-functional collaboration, 40-person team, board-visible)
- Successor candidate: Senior Engineering Manager, 6 years at the
  company, high performance in current role, rated 2-year readiness
- Key readiness gaps identified in calibration: limited experience
  managing across multiple teams simultaneously; no P&L or budget
  ownership; strong technically but developing in executive
  communication and stakeholder influence

Please:
1. Draft a 12-month individual development plan closing the
   identified gaps, with specific experiences, milestones, and
   owners for each
2. Draft a structured development conversation guide for the HRBP
   to use when first informing this candidate they are in the
   succession pipeline
3. Suggest 3-4 likely questions this candidate will ask when they
   learn they are a named successor, and draft direct, honest
   answers to each
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with board documentation:**

```txt
Based on this succession plan, draft a one-page board succession
summary for the Head of Engineering role, covering: current
coverage ratio, named successors with time-to-readiness, active
development actions, and the retention risk situation and how it
is being managed.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting structured development plans from a specific readiness gap input quickly
- Anticipating likely candidate questions when succession status is disclosed
- Generating reusable board summary templates with consistent structure

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Reading the candidate's reaction in the actual development conversation
- Judgment on which development experiences are politically feasible at this company
- The trust that comes from a leader being personally invested in the successor's growth

## Step 7 — Designing the Post-Transition Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "If the Head of Engineering does leave, or when we promote an internal successor into
> any critical role, I do not want the transition to quietly fail after the first 30
> days. How do I build a stabilization plan?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic
question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Is the newly promoted successor exercising the decision-making authority of the new
  role, or are they deferring to peers and former managers out of uncertainty about
  their mandate?
- Are direct reports treating the transition as real and permanent, or are they
  informally routing decisions around the new leader while adjusting to the change?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Is the successor's relationship with cross-functional partners (CPO, CTO, CEO) stable
  and productive, or is friction emerging that was not present during the candidate phase?
- Are the development gaps identified during calibration becoming visible in the new
  role, and if so, what targeted support is being provided?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would the successor, their direct reports, and their peers describe the transition as
  successful and complete, or is it still in progress informally?
- What structural or developmental adjustments, if any, are needed based on what has
  been learned in the first quarter of the new role?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Successor mandate clarity check-in | HRBP | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Direct report experience pulse check | HRBP + Successor | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Cross-functional relationship review | CPO + HRBP | 60 days |
| Development gap reassessment | HRBP | 90 days |

> **Common finding at scaling companies:** Leadership transitions frequently appear
> successful immediately after announcement because the energy of the promotion itself
> creates temporary momentum. The real test is whether the successor is fully exercising
> their mandate and building authority 60–90 days later — without a deliberate
> stabilization cadence, informal deference to the previous role-holder or to peers is
> the most common early failure pattern.

## Full Succession Planning Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (investor requirement, departure signal, growth milestone, board governance)
                    ↓
Map critical roles by business continuity risk and current coverage gap
                    ↓
Diagnose retention risk for any named flight risks in critical roles
                    ↓
Run talent review and 9-box calibration alongside the succession mapping
                    ↓
Present the succession plan to leadership and the board with honest gap framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft tailored development plans and board documentation
                    ↓
Design a post-transition stabilization plan for any upcoming leadership moves
                    ↓
Report on pipeline health and development progress at each review checkpoint
```

### Common Succession Planning Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Scoping succession as a full company exercise before a governance deadline | Define critical role scope first; deliver a credible focused plan before expanding |
| Running a 9-box without structured facilitation and behavioral anchors | Facilitate calibration sessions with defined bias-reduction steps before the session begins |
| Treating a single retention comment as an inevitable departure | Diagnose the root cause before building the succession plan around an assumption of vacancy |
| Building a succession chart without active development plans attached | Assign a development plan owner immediately after every calibration session, not at the next review cycle |
| Assuming pipeline strength is confirmed once names are in boxes | Track development plan progress and flight risk signals through defined quarterly checkpoints |
| Keeping succession entirely confidential with no development investment for named successors | Maintain list confidentiality while still investing visibly in development for high-potential employees |
| Skipping post-transition stabilization after a planned leadership promotion | Build a 90-day stabilization cadence with named owners before declaring a succession complete |
