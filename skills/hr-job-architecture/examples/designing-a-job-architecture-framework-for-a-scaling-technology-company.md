# Designing a Job Architecture Framework for a Scaling Technology Company

## Context

You are the HR Business Partner and sole Total Rewards owner at a 220-person technology
company that has grown from 80 to 220 employees in 18 months following a Series B funding
round. In that time, hiring managers and recruiters created job titles reactively to match
candidate expectations and close offers — resulting in 94 unique job titles across the
company, no consistent leveling criteria, and three separate Engineering title conventions
running in parallel (one from the founding team, one introduced by a VP Engineering hired
12 months ago, and one inherited from a 30-person acqui-hire completed six months ago).

The CFO has flagged that headcount cost planning is impossible without a consistent grade
structure. A senior engineer has filed an informal pay equity concern noting that two
colleagues hired externally six months ago carry higher titles for apparently equivalent
work. And the Head of Product has told you privately that two strong Product Managers
are at risk of leaving because they see no visible path to growth beyond their current
level. You have been asked to design a job architecture from the ground up, manage the
pay equity concern, and deliver a grade structure the finance team can use for headcount
budgeting — without creating a wave of employee anxiety about title changes.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "We have 94 unique job titles, a pay equity concern already filed, two flight risks on
> the Product team, and a CFO who needs a grade structure for headcount planning. Where
> do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — an active pay equity concern, visible flight risk, and a finance
deadline driving urgency — start with two priority areas in parallel:

**Pay equity concern containment (the filed concern):**

- Treat the informal filing as a formal trigger: conduct a leveling review of the
  engineer who filed and the two externally hired colleagues before any broader
  architecture work is visible to the organization
- Identify whether the concern reflects a genuine leveling inconsistency that will need
  to be corrected, or a title mismatch where the level and compensation are actually
  consistent but the visible title creates a misleading impression
- Document your review process from this point forward — a pay equity concern on record
  means any future architecture decision in Engineering needs a clear rationale trail

**Scope and sequencing clarity (the CFO and Product flight risk):**

- Align with the CFO on what the finance team actually needs in the near term: a grade
  structure with defined bands is sufficient for headcount budgeting and does not
  require a fully published employee-facing career framework first
- Identify the two Product Manager flight risks by name and have direct, individual
  career conversations this week — a job architecture build takes months; losing two
  strong PMs while building it would be a problem the architecture cannot fix retroactively
- Define whether you are building for the whole company simultaneously or by function,
  since a phased approach by job family reduces implementation risk substantially

The pay equity concern and the Product flight risk are not separate problems — both
trace back to the same root cause: leveling decisions made without a consistent
framework. You need a scoping decision before the architecture work begins, or the
design will be overtaken by the urgency.

> **Avoid:** Do not announce a company-wide job architecture project to employees
> before the design is complete. A 220-person company told that titles and levels are
> being reviewed will generate anxiety about demotions and pay changes before you have
> any answers to give them.

## Step 2 — Modeling Job Architecture Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few structural options for our job architecture before I commit to
> an approach, since we have three different Engineering conventions and no consistent
> model anywhere in the company."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Function-based architecture (one framework per department):**

```txt
Structure: Separate leveling guides and title conventions per function
           (Engineering, Product, Design, Data, GTM), each maintained
           by the function head with HR oversight
Trade-off: Easier to get function-specific buy-in, but creates
           cross-functional leveling inconsistency that makes internal
           mobility, pay equity comparison, and headcount planning
           across functions harder to sustain
```

**Option B — Unified job family architecture (common level spine across all functions):**

```txt
Structure: A single level spine (L1–L6 or equivalent) applied across
           all job families, with function-specific titles mapped to
           the common level at each grade
           Example: L4 = Senior Software Engineer / Senior PM /
           Senior Designer / Senior Data Analyst depending on family
Trade-off: Stronger for pay equity, internal mobility, and finance
           planning, but requires more upfront calibration and a
           clear communication plan for employees seeing their
           level expressed numerically for the first time
```

**Option C — Phased hybrid (unified spine for Engineering and Product first, extend to other functions in phase two):**

```txt
Structure: Unified level spine implemented for Engineering and Product
           in the first phase given the active pay equity concern and
           the acqui-hire title conflict, with Design, Data, and GTM
           following in a second phase 3–4 months later
Trade-off: Addresses the highest-urgency problem first and allows
           learning from the Engineering/Product rollout before
           extending, but creates a temporary period where only
           some functions have a defined architecture
```

**Benchmark context:**

A unified level spine (Option B or C) is the stronger foundation for pay equity
defensibility, internal mobility, and finance planning. The pay equity concern already
on record in Engineering makes a function-siloed architecture (Option A) a higher legal
and reputational risk — cross-functional leveling inconsistency is harder to defend in
an audit than a documented, calibrated unified framework applied consistently.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the active pay equity concern and the finance
> timeline, Option C best balances urgency with risk reduction. Start with a unified
> spine for Engineering and Product, resolve the pay equity concern within that
> structure, deliver the grade framework to finance, and extend to remaining functions
> in a planned second phase. This avoids both the legal risk of Option A and the
> timeline pressure of attempting a full company rollout in a single phase.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Pay Equity Concern

**Sample prompt:**

> "A senior engineer filed a concern that two external hires are at higher titles for
> equivalent work. I need to understand what is actually happening before I design the
> architecture around an assumption."

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before concluding whether the concern reflects a genuine pay equity issue, gather signals
across four dimensions:

**1. Leveling and scope comparison:**

- Are the two external hires performing work that is genuinely equivalent in scope,
  complexity, and impact to the engineer who filed the concern — or does the title
  difference reflect a real difference in role expectations that was never communicated?
- What leveling criteria, if any, were used when the external hires were placed at
  their current titles? Was it market-matching, hiring manager preference, or a
  structured level assessment?

**2. Compensation comparison:**

- Are the external hires paid more, less, or equivalently to the filing engineer when
  compared at the same role scope and experience level?
- Is this primarily a title visibility concern where compensation is actually similar,
  or does the title difference reflect a genuine pay gap that needs to be corrected?

**3. Process and transparency gaps:**

- Was there any documented leveling process at the time of the external hires, or were
  titles assigned entirely at hiring manager and recruiter discretion to close offers?
- Does the filing engineer have any visibility into how leveling decisions are made, or
  is the process completely opaque — which itself drives the perception of inequity
  regardless of the underlying facts?

**4. Individual conversation specifics:**

- Has anyone had a direct, individual conversation with the engineer who filed the
  concern to acknowledge receipt and explain the review process — or has the concern
  been received but not yet responded to, which compounds distrust?
- Is this engineer broadly engaged and likely to stay through a structured review, or
  is this concern a signal of a broader retention risk that predates the title comparison?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present this concern as a sign the company has
> a systemic pay equity problem before completing the review. Bring a clear picture of
> which aspects are title-convention inconsistency fixable through the architecture
> versus genuine compensation gaps that require immediate correction independent of the
> framework timeline.

## Step 4 — Building the Leveling Framework

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build a leveling guide for the Engineering job family first, since that is
> where the pay equity concern is and where we have three conflicting title conventions."

**Skill response — leveling framework design:**

| Leveling Component | Current Status | Action Needed |
|--------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Level spine defined | Not yet — three conventions in conflict | Align with VP Engineering on a unified 5- or 6-level IC spine before writing any criteria |
| Leveling dimensions selected | Not yet | Choose 4 core dimensions: Scope, Complexity, Impact, Autonomy |
| Criteria written per level | Not yet | Write observable, behavioral criteria for each dimension at each level |
| Existing roles mapped | Not yet | Map current Engineering roles and titles to the new levels through a calibration session |
| Grade and band attached | Not yet | Anchor each level to a defined compensation band using Radford or equivalent survey data |

**Recommended level spine for Engineering (example):**

```txt
L1 — Junior Software Engineer
     Scope: Assigned tasks within a defined area; close guidance expected
     Autonomy: Requires daily check-ins; decisions reviewed before implementation

L2 — Software Engineer
     Scope: Owns feature delivery within a defined component; some ambiguity tolerated
     Autonomy: Works independently on tasks; escalates blockers proactively

L3 — Senior Software Engineer
     Scope: Owns a system or significant product area; defines approach independently
     Autonomy: Sets own direction within team priorities; minimal day-to-day oversight

L4 — Staff Software Engineer
     Scope: Drives technical direction across multiple teams or a critical platform
     Autonomy: Initiates work with cross-team impact; influences engineering-wide decisions

L5 — Principal Software Engineer
     Scope: Defines technical strategy for a domain; visible impact at company level
     Autonomy: Self-directed; shapes organizational direction without senior leader input

L6 — Distinguished Engineer / Fellow
     Scope: Company-wide technical leadership; external industry influence
     Autonomy: Fully autonomous; defines engineering norms across the organization
```

**Calibration session structure:**

1. Pre-work: VP Engineering and engineering leads submit level assessments for each
   current role against the draft criteria one week before the session
2. Opening anchor: facilitator presents the leveling dimensions and example behaviors
   to normalize how leaders interpret each level
3. Role-by-role calibration: each Engineering role is placed on the new spine with
   documented rationale; disagreements are surfaced and resolved
4. Pay equity check: after mapping, compare current compensation against the proposed
   band for each level and flag any employees outside band
5. Action assignment: roles requiring title or compensation correction are flagged with
   a defined correction timeline before the broader rollout

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Writing leveling criteria so broadly that every level
> could claim the same behaviors. A criterion that says "demonstrates strong technical
> skills" applies equally to L2 and L5. A criterion that says "designs systems
> adopted across the engineering organization without being asked to" applies only to L4
> and above and is actually differentiating.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Framework to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring the leveling framework and grade structure to the CFO and CEO before
> we communicate anything to employees. How do I frame it given the pay equity concern
> already on record?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership responds to a framework that solves the business problems they named —
headcount budgeting, pay equity risk, and flight risk — rather than appearing as an HR
process exercise. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Affirm the business problem being solved:**

"The job architecture we have built directly addresses the three issues we are managing:
the CFO now has a grade structure with defined bands for headcount planning; the pay
equity concern in Engineering has been reviewed within the new framework and we have a
documented correction recommendation; and the Product Manager retention risk is being
addressed through a career path that gives both employees a visible next step."

**Part 2 — Name the current risk directly:**

"The 94 unique titles we have today are not neutral — they are a pay equity liability,
a headcount planning problem, and a retention risk all at once. The architecture we are
presenting consolidates those 94 titles into a unified framework. Two roles in Engineering
will require a compensation correction based on the calibration results, and we are
recommending those corrections be processed before the broader employee communication."

**Part 3 — Present the rollout plan and ask for what you need:**

"We are recommending a phased communication: corrections processed quietly first,
manager briefing second, then employee communication with the full framework published.
I need your support holding the employee communication until the corrections are
processed — any communication before that creates a window where the concern is visible
and the fix is not."

> **Key principle:** Leadership responds well to a framework that demonstrates the
> business risk being reduced, not just the HR process being completed. Frame the
> architecture in terms of pay equity liability, cost planning accuracy, and retention
> — not leveling consistency as an abstract HR goal.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Job Architecture Documentation

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft leveling criteria for five job families, career path documents for
> Product and Engineering, and a manager communication guide. How do I use Claude or
> ChatGPT without producing something generic?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize your level spine, the four leveling dimensions and their definitions, the
specific job family you are drafting for, and any examples of behaviors already agreed
in the calibration session before drafting anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am a Total Rewards HR Business Partner building a job architecture
for a 220-person Series B technology company.

Context:
- Job family: Product Management
- Level spine: L1 (Associate PM) through L5 (Director of Product)
- Leveling dimensions: Scope, Complexity, Impact, Autonomy
- L3 (Senior PM) is the benchmark level — most of the current team
  sits here, and the two flight-risk employees are both at L3 asking
  what growth looks like

Please:
1. Write behavioral, observable leveling criteria for L1 through L5
   on each of the four dimensions, making sure L3 and L4 criteria
   are genuinely distinct and not interchangeable
2. Draft a career path document for the Product Management job family
   that a Senior PM could read and use to self-assess their own
   development gaps toward L4 without needing HR to interpret it
3. Draft a manager guide for using these criteria in career
   conversations, with example language for common situations:
   "You are not ready to level up yet", "Here is what you need to
   demonstrate", and "Here is what L4 scope looks like in practice
   on our team"
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with governance documentation:**

```txt
Based on this job architecture, draft a governance guide for
managers explaining how to request a new role, how to assess
whether an existing employee should be regraded, and what the
approval process is — designed to prevent ad hoc title creation
from re-emerging within six months of launch.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting behavioral leveling criteria quickly from a defined dimension framework
- Generating career path documents written in employee-readable language
- Producing governance guides and manager enablement materials at scale

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Calibration judgment on whether a specific employee's work actually meets L4 criteria
- Political awareness of which title changes will feel sensitive to specific leaders
- The trust that comes from a manager having a direct, individual career conversation

## Step 7 — Designing the Post-Implementation Governance Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Once we launch the job architecture, I do not want it to quietly revert to ad hoc
> title creation within six months. How do I build a governance plan that holds?"

**Skill response — governance design:**

Build the governance plan around three mechanisms, each with a specific owner and
decision right.

**New role creation governance:**

- Any new job title must be mapped to an existing job family and level before a
  requisition is opened — no title can be created outside the framework without HR
  and finance sign-off
- Hiring managers may not negotiate a title above the leveling assessment outcome to
  close a candidate — compensation flexibility within band is the approved mechanism
  for competitive offers, not title inflation

**Leveling review cadence:**

- A calibration session is run semi-annually to assess whether any roles have
  meaningfully shifted in scope and require a level change
- Individual leveling reviews outside the calibration cycle require documented evidence
  of scope change, not performance ratings alone

**Governance checkpoints:**

| Governance Mechanism | Owner | Frequency |
|----------------------|-------|-----------|
| New role title and level sign-off | HR + Finance | Every requisition |
| Semi-annual leveling calibration | HR + Function Heads | Every 6 months |
| Title inflation audit (roles outside framework) | HR | Quarterly |
| Band range market refresh | Total Rewards | Annually |

> **Common finding at scaling companies:** Job architecture frameworks drift fastest in
> functions where hiring velocity is highest and the function head has the most
> autonomy. A governance model that requires HR sign-off on every new title before a
> requisition opens — not after — is the single most effective mechanism for preventing
> the 94-title problem from returning within a year.

## Full Job Architecture Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (title inflation, pay equity concern, finance planning need, M&A, rapid scaling)
                    ↓
Audit the current title landscape to map inflation, compression, and inconsistency
                    ↓
Model multiple structural options and compare trade-offs before committing to a design
                    ↓
Diagnose any active pay equity or leveling concern alongside the architecture design
                    ↓
Build the leveling criteria and grade structure with calibration and market benchmarking
                    ↓
Present the framework to leadership with business-risk and retention framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft leveling guides, career path documents, and governance materials
                    ↓
Design a governance model to prevent title drift in the six months after launch
                    ↓
Report on adoption, pay equity compliance, and retention impact at each review checkpoint
```

### Common Job Architecture Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Announcing a job architecture project before the design is ready | Complete the framework and manager enablement before any employee-facing communication |
| Writing leveling criteria at a level of abstraction that every employee could claim | Write criteria in behavioral, observable terms that are specific to each level's scope |
| Designing one architecture option without modeling alternatives | Compare at least two or three structural approaches before committing to a design |
| Processing a pay equity correction after the broader rollout communication | Correct pay equity gaps before the framework is communicated to the broader employee population |
| Launching the framework without a governance model | Define new role approval, leveling review cadence, and title inflation audit before go-live |
| Treating career path documents as HR-only tools rather than employee self-navigation tools | Design career path documents for employees first, written in plain language they can use independently |
| Skipping manager enablement before employee communication | Brief and enable all people managers before any employee-facing communication about the framework |
