# People Budgeting

## Overview

The people budget is the largest line item in most organizations' operating expense — typically 60-80% of total opex for knowledge-work businesses. Yet the process for building and managing it is often poorly structured: disconnected from workforce strategy, built bottom-up from manager requests with minimal challenge, and managed as a headcount count rather than a total cost view.

Effective people budgeting connects business strategy to workforce cost, ensures the right trade-offs are made explicitly rather than implicitly, and gives HR a meaningful seat at the financial planning table.

## People Budget Components

A complete people budget covers more than salary. The full scope:

### Compensation

- Base salary by role and level
- Target variable pay (bonus, commission)
- Equity cost (amortized vesting expense)
- Salary increase budget (merit, promotion, market adjustment)

### Employer-paid benefits and contributions

- Mandatory social insurance contributions (BHXH, BHYT, BHTN in Vietnam)
- Health insurance (employer share of premium)
- Retirement plan contributions
- Other statutory benefits

### Recruiting and onboarding

- Internal recruiter fully loaded cost (allocated to the roles they fill)
- External agency fees and job advertising
- Onboarding and training cost for new hires

### Learning and development

- Training programs and licenses
- Conference and certification budget
- Tuition reimbursement

### Contingent and contractor workforce

- Contractor costs by type and function
- Consulting and professional services with a workforce component

Many organizations budget only the first category and discover mid-year that total people cost has exceeded plan because the other categories were unmanaged.

## The Headcount Plan vs. the Cost Plan

Headcount and cost are related but different. A plan expressed only in headcount leaves significant cost uncertainty unmanaged.

### Why headcount targets underspecify cost

- Roles at different seniority levels have very different cost profiles
- Hiring timing within the year affects annualized cost (a hire in January costs 12 months of salary; a hire in October costs 3 months in the budget year)
- Mix of permanent and contract roles has different cost and accounting treatment
- Open roles have a vacancy benefit (budgeted cost not yet incurred) that disappears when filled

### Building the cost view alongside headcount

For each planned role, capture:
- Role title and seniority level
- Target start date
- Base salary range midpoint (or planned salary)
- Fully loaded cost multiplier
- Annualized cost and current-year cost (based on start month)
- Budget year cost impact

Aggregate these into a monthly cost run-rate view. This reveals when the budget will be consumed and whether the hiring plan is financially feasible within the budget envelope.

## The Budget Cycle

Most organizations run a people budgeting cycle aligned with their annual financial planning process. A typical cycle:

**Strategic framing (2-3 months before fiscal year start):** Finance and HR align on business assumptions — revenue growth, key initiatives, productivity targets — that will drive workforce demand. CHRO and CFO agree on total people cost envelope before bottoms-up planning begins.

**Bottoms-up planning (6-8 weeks before fiscal year start):** Business unit leaders submit headcount plans by role. HR reviews for consistency with agreed strategy and challenges requests that are not connected to approved business plans.

**Consolidation and challenge (4-6 weeks before fiscal year start):** HR and Finance consolidate plans, identify gaps between requested headcount and budget envelope, and facilitate trade-off discussions. Plans that exceed envelope require explicit prioritization — which roles are approved, which are deferred.

**Budget approval:** Final plan approved by the CFO and board (where applicable). Headcount and cost by function by quarter locked as the plan of record.

**In-year management:** Monthly or quarterly reforecast updates actuals versus plan. Variance analysis identifies over- and under-spend by driver.

## In-Year Budget Management

Approving the budget is not the end of the process. In-year management determines whether the plan holds.

### Key variance drivers to monitor monthly

- **Hiring pace vs. plan:** Roles filling later than planned reduce cost (vacancy savings) but create delivery risk. Roles filling earlier than planned consume budget ahead of schedule.
- **Salary actuals vs. plan:** Hires made above the budgeted midpoint create upward variance. Track against budget by role.
- **Attrition vs. assumption:** Higher attrition than assumed creates vacancy savings; lower attrition reduces flexibility for new hiring within the cost envelope.
- **Mix shift:** Changes in the ratio of senior to junior roles, or permanent to contract, shift total cost without changing headcount.

### Reforecast cadence

Quarterly reforecast is a minimum; monthly is better for fast-moving organizations. The reforecast should update the full-year cost outlook based on actuals to date and expected changes in the remaining period.

### Managing to the envelope mid-year

When a reforecast shows the people budget tracking over plan, the options are:

- Slow hiring pace (extend time-to-fill targets, reduce urgency of backfills)
- Defer planned roles to the next fiscal year
- Offset with attrition savings in lower-priority areas
- Request a formal budget increase through finance governance

The worst outcome is discovering the overrun in the final quarter when options are limited. Early warning through monthly tracking gives leaders time to make deliberate choices.

## People Budget Governance

Clear governance prevents the common failure mode of individual business units making local headcount decisions that aggregate to an unmanageable enterprise cost.

### Decision rights framework

- **CHRO + CFO:** Total people cost envelope, major reforecasts, changes to compensation philosophy
- **HR Business Partner + Finance Partner:** Function-level headcount plan within approved envelope, backfill approvals within budget
- **Business unit leader:** Prioritization within approved headcount allocation, timing decisions within agreed parameters
- **Recruiter / HR Coordinator:** Execution within approved roles

Roles not approved in the budget plan require the appropriate level of governance approval before recruiting begins — not after an offer is made.
