# Job Analysis

## Overview

Job analysis is the systematic process of collecting information about a job — its tasks, responsibilities, required knowledge and skills, working conditions, and performance standards. It is the foundational activity that should underpin job descriptions, hiring criteria, compensation classification, performance standards, and development frameworks. In practice, most organizations skip or shortcut job analysis, and then wonder why their job descriptions are inaccurate, their hiring criteria are inconsistent, and their compensation classifications are contested.

## When Job Analysis Is Required

Job analysis is not necessary for every role change, but it is essential when:

- Creating a new role that does not have an existing template
- Significantly redesigning an existing role due to organizational change, technology introduction, or strategy shift
- Establishing compensation classification for a role (exempt/non-exempt, salary grade)
- Building or updating a competency framework
- Defending hiring criteria or performance standards in a legal or regulatory context
- Conducting a workforce planning analysis that requires accurate role scope definition

For routine backfills of unchanged roles, existing job documentation is sufficient if it accurately reflects the work.

## Job Analysis Methods

### Structured interviews with incumbents and managers

The most common and generally most useful method. Interview people currently doing the job (incumbents) and the managers who supervise it about:
- What tasks are performed and how frequently?
- What knowledge and skills are required to perform each task?
- What decisions does the role make independently versus with approval?
- What are the consequences of poor performance in each task area?
- What changed in the role compared to 2-3 years ago?

Interview both incumbents and managers — their perspectives often differ meaningfully, and the differences are informative. A manager who thinks a role is primarily administrative while the incumbent spends 70% of their time on analysis has a role definition problem that needs to surface before the next hire.

### Observation

For operational, technical, or manual roles where work activities are concrete and physical, direct observation produces more accurate task descriptions than interviews alone. Incumbents adapt their descriptions to what they think the interviewer wants to hear; what they actually do is observable.

### Work diaries

Ask incumbents to log their activities over a defined period (typically 1-2 weeks) — what they did, how long it took, and how important it was. Work diaries capture the full range of activities including irregular tasks that do not surface in a single interview.

### Job analysis questionnaires

Structured questionnaires — particularly the Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ) and O*NET-based instruments — provide standardized data that enables comparison across roles and connection to external benchmarks. Useful for compensation classification and workforce planning, less useful for generating specific behavioral criteria for hiring.

### Existing documentation review

Prior job descriptions, performance reviews, competency frameworks, and organizational charts provide baseline information and historical context. Do not rely on existing documentation without validation — it is often outdated, incomplete, or aspirational rather than accurate.

## Job Analysis Output: The Job Description

The primary output of job analysis is a job description that accurately reflects what the role does, what it requires, and how it fits in the organization.

### Job analysis vs. job posting

A job analysis document and a job posting serve different purposes:
- The **job analysis document** (or position description) is comprehensive, internally focused, and used for compensation classification, performance management, and organizational design. It captures the full scope of the role including irregular and administrative tasks.
- The **job posting** is externally focused, marketing-oriented, and designed to attract candidates. It highlights the most important and attractive aspects of the role without exhaustive detail.

Most organizations conflate these, producing job postings that read like bureaucratic position descriptions and position descriptions that read like job ads.

### Minimum job analysis document contents

- Role title and reporting relationship
- Role purpose statement (why does this role exist?)
- Key accountabilities: the 5-8 primary areas of responsibility, each with the performance standard or expected outcome
- Decision authority: what decisions can this role make independently?
- Key relationships: internal and external stakeholders the role regularly works with
- Required qualifications: education, experience, certifications that are genuinely required (not aspirational)
- Required competencies: knowledge, skills, and behaviors required to perform effectively
- Working conditions: location, travel, physical requirements if relevant
- Compensation classification: pay grade, exemption status (where applicable)

## Legal Considerations

Job analysis is not just an HR best practice — it is a legal risk management tool.

### Discrimination defense

When a hiring decision or employment action is challenged as discriminatory, the organization must demonstrate that the criteria used were job-related and consistent with business necessity. A documented job analysis that shows specific job requirements and how they connect to actual job tasks is essential evidence.

Hiring criteria that are not supported by job analysis are difficult to defend. "We required 5 years of experience" is easy to challenge without analysis showing why that level of experience is genuinely necessary for job performance.

### Compensation classification (Vietnam context)

Under Vietnam's Labor Code, job classification affects compensation requirements, overtime obligations, and social insurance calculations. Accurate job analysis is the foundation of defensible classification decisions. Misclassification — particularly of employees who perform management functions but are classified at lower grades — creates wage and hour liability.

### Reasonable accommodation analysis

When an employee requests an accommodation for a disability, the organization needs to assess whether the requested accommodation would allow the employee to perform the essential functions of the role. "Essential functions" must be documented to conduct this analysis. Job analysis that identifies which tasks are essential (cannot be removed or reassigned without fundamentally changing the role) versus marginal (can be reallocated) provides the foundation for accommodation analysis.
