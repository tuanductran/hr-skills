# Conducting Rigorous Job Analysis

## Overview

Job analysis is the systematic study of what a job actually requires — tasks, competencies, and working conditions — as distinct from what a job title or an outdated job description implies. It's the evidentiary foundation for job evaluation, classification, compensation benchmarking, and legally defensible hiring criteria. When job analysis is skipped or done superficially, everything built on top of it (pay grades, interview criteria, accommodation decisions) inherits the same weak foundation.

The discipline is often confused with writing a job description, but the two serve different purposes: job analysis is the data-gathering and documentation process; the job description is one output derived from it, written for an external audience.

## Data Collection Methods

| Method | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Structured interviews with incumbents | Rich, contextual detail on how work is actually done | Time-intensive, doesn't scale to many roles |
| Structured interviews with supervisors | Captures performance expectations and organizational context | May not reflect day-to-day task reality as accurately as incumbents |
| Direct observation | High accuracy for physical or procedural tasks | Impractical for knowledge work, and presence can alter behavior |
| Task inventory questionnaires | Scales across many incumbents in the same role | Requires a well-designed instrument; poor questions produce poor data |
| Existing documentation review | Fast starting point | Frequently outdated; should never be the sole source |

The strongest job analyses triangulate at least two methods rather than relying on a single source — incumbent interviews paired with a task inventory questionnaire, for example, catches gaps that either method alone would miss.

## Task Analysis

Task analysis breaks a job into its component tasks and rates each on dimensions that matter for downstream decisions:

- **Frequency** — how often the task is performed (daily, weekly, occasionally)
- **Criticality** — how consequential an error on this task would be
- **Time allocation** — approximate share of total working time
- **Difficulty** — relative complexity compared to other tasks in the role

Rating tasks on these dimensions, rather than simply listing them, is what makes job analysis useful for classification and compensation decisions — a long, unweighted task list doesn't distinguish a role's core function from its incidental duties.

## Competency Documentation

Beyond tasks, job analysis should document the underlying knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs) required to perform them competently:

- Distinguish competencies required at entry (day-one proficiency) from those developed on the job — conflating the two produces hiring criteria that screen out trainable candidates unnecessarily
- Tie each competency back to a specific task or responsibility it supports, rather than listing generic competencies disconnected from the actual work — this traceability is what makes the analysis defensible under scrutiny
- Note where a competency is legally essential (required for the role to be performed safely or lawfully) versus merely preferred, since this distinction matters directly for accommodation decisions

## Connecting Job Analysis to Job Evaluation and Classification

Job evaluation uses job analysis output to determine a role's relative value within the organization's structure — typically scored against factors like complexity, scope of impact, and required expertise. The quality of this evaluation depends entirely on the quality of the underlying analysis:

- Evaluate roles against the same factor framework consistently, using the documented task and competency data rather than incumbent tenure or personality, which can distort scoring
- Re-evaluate roles when job analysis reveals the actual work has drifted meaningfully from the role's current classification — role scope creep without reclassification is a common source of both pay equity issues and employee dissatisfaction
- Document the evaluation rationale, not just the resulting grade — this documentation is frequently what's requested first in a pay equity audit or classification dispute

## Legal Defensibility

Because job analysis underpins hiring criteria and essential-function determinations, thorough documentation matters beyond internal HR use:

- Essential functions documented through job analysis are the basis for accommodation reasonableness assessments — a task not documented as essential is harder to justify excluding a candidate over
- Selection criteria derived from documented job analysis (rather than informal manager preference) are substantially more defensible if challenged
- Keep job analysis records current — analysis conducted years ago for a role that has since evolved provides weak legal footing for today's decisions

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating job analysis as a one-time exercise at role creation.** Roles evolve; a periodic refresh cycle (especially after reorganizations or major process changes) keeps the analysis accurate.
- **Relying solely on the job description as if it were the analysis.** A JD is a downstream communication artifact, often written for marketing purposes, and shouldn't be treated as a substitute for the underlying data.
- **Skipping competency-to-task traceability**, which weakens both the hiring criteria derived from the analysis and its defensibility if challenged.
