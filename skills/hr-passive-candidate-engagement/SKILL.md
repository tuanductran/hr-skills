# Passive candidate engagement

Start and sustain conversations with people who aren't actively job-searching — through personalized outreach, value-first messaging, and multi-touch nurture sequences that build trust before ever pitching a role.

## Supported tasks

- Writing first-touch outreach messages tailored to a passive candidate's background
- Designing multi-touch nurture sequences across email, LinkedIn, and other channels
- Personalizing outreach based on a candidate's recent work, posts, or projects
- Re-engaging candidates who went cold or didn't respond initially
- Crafting value-first messages that don't lead with a job pitch
- Handling common objections ("not looking right now", "happy where I am")
- Timing outreach around candidate signals (new posts, company news, role changes)
- Building templates by persona (engineer, designer, executive, technical specialist)
- Balancing personalization with outreach volume and recruiter time
- Transitioning a warm conversation into a formal interview process
- Maintaining relationships with candidates who aren't a fit right now
- Measuring response rates and refining messaging based on what works

## Key prompts

### First-touch outreach

1. "Write a first-touch outreach message to a passive [role] candidate who recently [shipped a project / spoke at a conference / posted about a technical topic]."
2. "Draft an outreach message for a senior candidate that doesn't mention the job title in the first two sentences."
3. "Write three variations of an opening line referencing a candidate's specific background, from warm to more direct."
4. "How do I personalize outreach at scale without it feeling templated?"

### Nurture sequences

1. "Design a 4-touch nurture sequence for a passive candidate who hasn't responded, spaced over 6 weeks."
2. "Write a follow-up message for a candidate who opened the first email but didn't reply."
3. "Draft a re-engagement message for a candidate we contacted 8 months ago who wasn't ready then."
4. "What should touch 2 say differently from touch 1 to avoid sounding repetitive?"

### Handling responses and objections

1. "Write a reply to a candidate who said 'I'm happy where I am right now.'"
2. "How do I respond when a candidate is interested but says the timing is wrong?"
3. "Draft a message that keeps the door open with a candidate who declined this specific role."
4. "Write a response to a candidate asking for more detail before they'll consider a conversation."

## Tips

- Lead with something specific and true about the person, not a templated compliment — generic flattery is the fastest way to get ignored.
- Passive engagement is a relationship, not a single email; plan for multiple touches before expecting a response.
- Respect a "no" or "not now" — log it, ask permission to check back later, and don't turn nurture into pestering.
- Match channel to persona: senior technical and executive candidates often respond better to concise, direct messages than long pitches.
- Track what specific phrasing or hooks get responses so messaging improves over time rather than staying static.
