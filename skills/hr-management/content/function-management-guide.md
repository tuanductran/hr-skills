# Managing the HR Function

## Overview

Managing the HR function is the operational and strategic discipline of running HR as a business — setting strategy, managing a team, allocating resources, governing quality, and demonstrating value to the organization. CHROs and senior HR leaders who are skilled practitioners in their domains sometimes struggle with the meta-skill of running the function itself.

## HR Function Strategy

The HR function needs its own strategy — not a restatement of the business strategy, but a clear articulation of what HR will prioritize, what it will stop doing, and how it will measure success in the context of what the business needs.

### HR strategy on a page

A practical HR strategy document answers four questions:

**What does the business need from people and organization to execute strategy?** Capability requirements, organizational changes, workforce shifts, culture evolution.

**What are HR's top 3-5 priorities for the next 12-24 months?** The work that will have the most impact on business priorities. Not a comprehensive list of everything HR does.

**What will HR stop doing or do less of?** Strategy requires trade-offs. Listing priorities without listing what is being deprioritized produces a strategy that sounds good but changes nothing.

**How will we measure success?** Specific, time-bound metrics for each priority.

A strategy document longer than two pages is usually a catalog, not a strategy.

### Common strategy traps

**Prioritizing everything:** When every HR initiative is a priority, none is. Senior HR leaders who cannot name the three things they are most focused on this year have not made strategic choices.

**Mistaking activity for impact:** Measuring HR by programs run, training hours delivered, or policies updated conflates activity with outcome. Strategy should be measured by workforce outcomes (capability improvement, retention, engagement, hiring quality) not HR process outputs.

**Building for the ideal state:** HR strategies designed for where the organization wants to be in five years rather than where it is now produce programs the organization is not ready to absorb.

## Managing the HR Team

HR teams are often under-managed by HR leaders who are expert practitioners but inexperienced people managers. The skills that make a strong HR business partner or compensation specialist are not the same as the skills required to lead a team of them.

### Team structure and role clarity

Before worrying about development or performance, ensure the HR team has clear structure and role clarity:

- What does each role own? What decisions can they make independently?
- Where do roles overlap, and how are those overlaps resolved?
- How does the team coordinate on enterprise-wide work (annual compensation cycle, engagement survey, policy updates)?
- Who owns what in the HRIS and HR systems?

Ambiguous role boundaries in an HR team produce duplicated work, gaps, and interpersonal friction that HR leaders are often slow to address because they are managing the same dynamics for the business.

### Performance management for HR professionals

HR professionals are sometimes held to lower performance standards than the business populations they support — particularly on measurable outcomes. Apply the same rigor:

- Clear goals connected to HR strategy priorities
- Regular performance conversations (the same frequency HR recommends for managers)
- Honest written feedback in performance reviews
- Timely management of underperformance

A CHRO who does not hold their HR team accountable has limited credibility advising business leaders to do the same.

### HR team development

HR as a profession is evolving faster than most HR teams' skills. Development gaps that are common and consequential:

- **Data literacy:** HR professionals who cannot read and interpret data (attrition analysis, regression output, statistical significance) are increasingly disadvantaged as people analytics becomes standard.
- **Business acumen:** HRBPs who do not understand how the business they support makes money, what its competitive dynamics are, and what drives its financial performance cannot be strategic partners.
- **Consulting skills:** Diagnosing organizational problems, building evidence-based recommendations, and influencing without authority are learnable skills that most HR professionals are never explicitly trained in.
- **AI and technology literacy:** Understanding what AI tools can and cannot do is increasingly essential for HR professionals advising on AI adoption, designing AI-assisted HR processes, and evaluating HR technology.

## HR Budgeting and Resource Allocation

The HR budget is the operational constraint that forces prioritization. HR leaders who do not manage their budget actively lose credibility with Finance and lose the ability to make strategic resource decisions.

### Budget components to manage actively

- **Headcount:** Full-time HR staff and contractors. The largest component. Manage span and capacity against workload, not just against prior-year headcount.
- **Technology:** HRIS, ATS, LMS, compensation tools, engagement platforms. License costs, implementation costs, and the staff time required to run and maintain them.
- **Learning and development:** External facilitators, program design, content licenses, conference attendance. Allocate based on strategic priorities, not department seniority.
- **Recruiting costs:** Agency fees, job boards, recruiting tools. Often undercounted in the HR budget because they are tracked separately.

### Common budget management failures

- No view of fully loaded HR cost (headcount plus technology plus programs) — makes trade-off decisions impossible
- Budget managed at the annual level without monthly tracking — overruns are discovered too late
- No connection between budget allocation and HR strategy priorities — money flows to whatever was funded last year

## Demonstrating HR Value

HR functions that cannot demonstrate their impact on business outcomes are perpetually vulnerable to budget cuts and underestimated by business leaders.

### Value demonstration framework

**Connect HR metrics to business metrics:** Attrition costs money — quantify it. Time-to-fill affects revenue in sales roles and delivery capacity in product roles — quantify it. Engagement predicts retention and productivity — track the correlation.

**Measure HR outcomes, not HR activity:** Report on quality of hire, retention of high performers, manager effectiveness scores, and capability gap closure. Not on training hours, policies published, or hires made.

**Build the case for investment:** When requesting budget for a new HR initiative, build a business case that quantifies the problem being solved, the expected impact of the solution, and the ROI. This is the language Finance and CEOs speak.

**Report to the board and leadership team:** CHROs who present people data to the board regularly build organizational understanding of people risk and HR's role in managing it. One-slide people updates at the end of board meetings do not build strategic credibility.
