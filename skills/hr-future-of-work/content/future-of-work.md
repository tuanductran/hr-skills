# Future of Work

## Overview

Future-of-work planning addresses structural shifts in where, when, and how work happens — driven by remote/hybrid arrangements, AI tooling, and changing employee expectations — and how organizations adapt policy, management practice, and culture accordingly.

## Main topics

### Hybrid and remote work models

Common models range from fully remote to hybrid (fixed or flexible in-office days) to full return-to-office. Effective models are designed around actual collaboration needs rather than default assumptions about productivity or culture.

### Flexible schedules

Compressed workweeks, flexible hours, and asynchronous collaboration norms let employees manage work around personal needs while maintaining coverage and collaboration windows.

### AI's impact on job design

AI tools are changing which tasks within a role require human judgment versus automation, shifting required skills toward oversight, prompting, and exception handling rather than routine execution.

### Manager enablement

Leading distributed or hybrid teams requires different skills than leading co-located teams: more deliberate communication, outcome-based performance management, and intentional relationship-building.

### Culture and equity considerations

Flexible work models risk creating a two-tier culture where in-office employees get more visibility and opportunity than remote peers ("proximity bias"), which requires deliberate mitigation.

## Practical guidance

- Base policy on collaboration and business needs, tested through pilots where possible.
- Invest in manager training before or alongside any major work-model change.
- Monitor for proximity bias in promotion and opportunity data across work locations.
- Revisit policies periodically; future-of-work is an ongoing capability, not a one-time decision.

## Conclusion

Future-of-work planning is less about picking a single "correct" work model and more about building the organizational capability to adapt work arrangements as technology, competition, and employee expectations continue to shift.
