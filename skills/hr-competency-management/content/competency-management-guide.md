# Competency Management

## Overview

A competency framework defines the knowledge, skills, behaviors, and attributes that drive effective performance in roles across the organization. When well-designed and actively maintained, it provides a shared language for hiring, performance assessment, development, and promotion decisions. When poorly designed or abandoned after launch, it becomes shelfware — a documentation artifact that nobody uses.

Most competency framework projects fail not because of design quality but because of adoption failures: the framework is designed by HR without sufficient manager involvement, rolled out through training rather than embedded in existing processes, and allowed to drift as roles evolve without updates.

## Framework Design Principles

### Fewer, sharper competencies over comprehensive coverage

The most common design failure is trying to include every possible relevant attribute — resulting in a framework with 20-30 competencies that nobody can remember or practically evaluate. Research on competency frameworks suggests that 6-10 competencies per role or level is the range where frameworks remain usable.

Prioritize competencies that:
- Differentiate high performers from average performers in that role (not just threshold requirements)
- Are observable through behavior, not personality attribution
- Are relevant across a meaningful population (not so role-specific that they require a unique framework per job family)

### Behavioral indicators at each level

A competency without behavioral indicators is just a label. "Communication" as a competency is meaningless. "Communication — Senior IC level: Writes clear, concise documentation that allows others to understand and build on your work without requiring in-person clarification; adapts communication style to technical and non-technical audiences" is usable.

Write behavioral indicators at each proficiency level that are specific enough that a manager and employee reading the same example would agree on whether it is demonstrated. If agreement is not possible, the indicator is not specific enough.

### Calibration before launch

Before a framework is published, test it against real performance cases:
- Would using this framework have led to different assessment outcomes for employees who are known high performers versus low performers?
- Do managers applying the framework independently reach consistent conclusions?
- Are there roles or segments where the framework does not fit and needs modification?

Calibration before launch finds the gaps that will otherwise become adoption barriers.

## Integrating Competencies into HR Processes

A competency framework that is only a document is not integrated. Integration means the framework actively influences decisions in existing processes.

### Hiring integration

**Structured interview design:** Competency frameworks provide the foundation for structured interviews. Each competency targeted in the hiring process has associated behavioral interview questions designed to elicit evidence of that competency.

**Assessment design:** Technical and cognitive assessments, work samples, and case exercises can be mapped to specific competencies. This makes assessment design more deliberate and evaluation more consistent.

**Job description development:** Required competencies in job descriptions signal to candidates what the role requires and create consistency between what is advertised and what is assessed.

### Performance management integration

**Goal setting:** Competencies provide the "how" alongside the "what" in goal setting. The framework makes it possible to evaluate not just whether targets were achieved but whether they were achieved in ways consistent with organizational values and role requirements.

**Performance review conversations:** Competency-based performance reviews anchor feedback in specific behavioral examples rather than general impressions. "You demonstrated strategic thinking this year in X way; here is where there is an opportunity to develop this competency further" is more useful than "strong performer" or "needs improvement."

**Calibration sessions:** Competency frameworks provide a shared vocabulary for calibration sessions where managers discuss relative performance across their teams. Without shared criteria, calibration devolves into advocacy and subjective impression.

### Development integration

**Development planning:** Competency frameworks identify specific development priorities for each employee. "Develop executive communication competency from working to advanced level" is a concrete development goal. Without a framework, development plans are generic ("improve leadership presence").

**Learning design:** L&D teams can map learning content to competency development, making it possible to recommend specific experiences for specific development needs rather than offering a generic catalog.

**Career path guidance:** Competency requirements at each level of a career framework tell employees what they need to develop to advance. Without this connection, career path guidance is abstract.

## Governance and Maintenance

Competency frameworks degrade over time. Roles evolve, business strategy changes, and new skills become relevant. Without maintenance governance, a framework that was accurate at launch is outdated within 2-3 years.

### Maintenance triggers

- Annual review: Are the competencies still relevant to how work is being done?
- Role or organizational change: When roles significantly evolve or new role families are created, does the framework cover them?
- Strategic shift: When the business strategy changes significantly, do the competency requirements change?
- Feedback signal: When managers consistently find that certain competencies are not useful or certain behaviors are missing from the framework, that is a maintenance signal.

### Governance structure

Assign a framework owner (typically in HR or Talent Management) who is responsible for maintaining the framework and managing the review cycle. Without an owner, updates do not happen.

Establish an advisory group of managers and senior practitioners who review proposed changes before they are published. This maintains buy-in and catches practical problems that the framework owner may not see.

### Versioning and communication

When the framework is updated, communicate what changed and why. Employees and managers who have used the framework need to understand how the new version differs and what it means for current performance conversations and development plans.
