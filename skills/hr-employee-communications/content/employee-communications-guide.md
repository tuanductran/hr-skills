# Employee Communications

## Overview

Employee communications is the practice of keeping employees informed, aligned, and connected to the organization through deliberate, consistent communication across channels and contexts. It differs from change communication (which handles specific significant changes) in that it is the ongoing operating system of organizational clarity — not a response to a discrete event.

Poor employee communications is a talent retention risk, an engagement driver, and an operational problem. Employees who do not understand strategy, do not know how their work connects to organizational goals, or feel like communication is one-directional and opaque are less engaged and more likely to leave.

## What Good Employee Communications Achieves

- Employees understand the organization's strategy and their role in it
- Employees receive information relevant to their work before they need it — not after rumors have filled the gap
- Employees have clear, accessible channels to ask questions and raise concerns
- Communication is calibrated to audience — a factory worker and a software engineer need different communication in different channels
- Leaders at all levels are equipped and expected to communicate, not just the CEO

## Communication Architecture

An effective employee communications system has a defined architecture — not everything sent to everyone all the time, but the right information to the right audience through the right channel.

### Channels and their appropriate use

**All-company communications:** Leadership announcements, strategy updates, significant policy changes, company performance results. Should be meaningful and infrequent — all-company communication that arrives too frequently loses its significance.

**Team/function communications:** Updates and information relevant to a specific team or function. Typically manager-led, with support from HR or Communications where needed. The highest-frequency and most immediately relevant communication channel for most employees.

**HRIS self-service / intranet:** Policy documents, benefits information, HR forms, organizational announcements, recorded town halls. The library layer — reference information employees access when they need it.

**Email:** Appropriate for communications requiring documentation (formal policy notices, offer letters, compliance communications) and for reaching employees who are not on company communication platforms.

**Slack/Teams/messaging platforms:** Best for real-time, conversational communication within teams. Poor for formal announcements requiring documentation.

**Town halls and all-hands meetings:** High-value for strategy communication, Q&A with leadership, and cultural moments. Overused when they become routine status updates rather than meaningful connections.

**Manager briefings:** The most important channel for significant communications. Managers who brief their teams on company news before it is announced publicly, and who create space for questions and discussion, have teams with higher trust and clarity.

### Channel segmentation by employee type

Not all employees have equal access to company communication channels. Frontline employees (manufacturing, retail, field service, logistics) may not have company email or access to the intranet during working hours. Communication strategy for mixed workforces must account for channel access by employee population.

## Writing for Employees

Employee communication that is not read is not communication. The most common failure is communications written for the sender's comfort (comprehensive, formal, legally defensible) rather than the reader's comprehension.

### Core principles

**Lead with what matters to the employee.** The first paragraph should answer "what does this mean for me?" — not provide organizational context, historical background, or leadership biography.

**Use plain language.** Write at a reading level that every employee in your target audience can access without effort. If a sentence requires a second read to parse, rewrite it.

**Be specific.** "We are committed to supporting employee growth" communicates nothing. "We are expanding tuition reimbursement from 10M VND to 20M VND per year starting January 1" communicates something.

**State what is not changing.** In communications about changes, employees worry about what has not been addressed. Explicitly noting "your salary and benefits are not affected by this announcement" is more useful than assuming employees will infer it.

**End with action and contact.** What, if anything, does the employee need to do? Who do they contact with questions?

### Reviewing communications before sending

Before sending any significant employee communication, ask:

- Is the most important information in the first paragraph?
- Would an employee reading this quickly on their phone understand what it means for them?
- Have we said what is not changing as clearly as what is?
- Is there a clear action or next step?
- Is there a feedback or question channel identified?

## Measuring Communication Effectiveness

Employee communications without measurement produces volume, not outcomes.

### Metrics worth tracking

**Open and read rates** for email and intranet content — directional signal on whether content is reaching employees. Declining rates signal either volume fatigue or relevance problems.

**Engagement survey communication items** — "I receive information I need to do my job," "Leadership communicates openly," "I understand how my work connects to company strategy." These are the outcome metrics.

**Town hall attendance and Q&A participation** — are employees showing up and engaging, or treating town halls as a box to check?

**Manager communication effectiveness** — pulse survey items specifically on whether employees feel well-informed by their manager. The biggest driver of information access for most employees.

**Rumor monitoring** — when rumors about significant topics surface before official communication, it is a signal that the communication lag needs to close.
