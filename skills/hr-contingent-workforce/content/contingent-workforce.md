# Contingent Workforce Management

## Overview

Contingent workforce management covers the sourcing, engagement, and governance of non-employee labor — independent contractors, freelancers, temporary staff, and staffing agency workers — balancing flexibility with legal and financial risk.

## Main topics

### Worker classification

Classification tests (control over work, integration into the business, opportunity for profit/loss, and other jurisdiction-specific factors) determine whether a worker should legally be an employee or a contractor. Misclassification carries back-tax, benefits, and penalty risk.

### Co-employment risk

Co-employment risk arises when a company exercises too much direct control over agency-supplied workers, potentially making it a joint employer with attendant legal obligations. Clear boundaries around supervision, access, and integration help manage this risk.

### VMS/MSP programs

A vendor management system (VMS) is the technology platform for requisitioning, tracking, and paying contingent workers; a managed service provider (MSP) often administers the program and manages staffing agency relationships on the company's behalf.

### Statements of work

SOWs define project-based contractor engagements by deliverable and outcome rather than hours worked, which helps support proper independent-contractor classification.

### Spend and headcount visibility

Contingent workforce spend is often scattered across departmental budgets outside formal HR/procurement visibility, making centralized tracking essential for cost control and risk management.

## Practical guidance

- Apply jurisdiction-specific classification tests to the actual working relationship, not just contract wording.
- Set a maximum tenure or periodic review trigger for long-running contractor engagements.
- Route contingent worker access requests through a distinct, more limited process than employee onboarding.
- Centralize spend and headcount reporting across all contingent worker sources (direct, agency, SOW-based).

## Conclusion

Effective contingent workforce management gives organizations flexibility while actively managing misclassification and co-employment risk through clear governance, periodic review, and centralized visibility.
