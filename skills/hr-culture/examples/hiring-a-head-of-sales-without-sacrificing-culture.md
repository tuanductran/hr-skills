# Hiring a Head of Sales Without Sacrificing Culture

## Context

You are an HR Manager at a 60-person startup that has grown quickly and is now hiring its first **Head of Sales**. The founders love the team's current culture — high trust, direct feedback, low hierarchy — and are worried that hiring an experienced sales leader from a large, process-heavy company will "wreck the culture." You need to help them hire for real culture alignment without accidentally screening out strong candidates just because they think or work differently.

## Step 1 — Translating Leadership's "Culture Fit" Concern

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our founders keep saying they're worried about 'culture fit' for this Head of Sales hire. What do they actually mean, and is that a good way to screen candidates?"

**Expected skill response:**

- **What founders usually mean** = they want someone who shares the company's core working values (transparency, ownership, low ego) even if their background, style, or industry experience looks different from the existing team
- **The risk in "culture fit" as commonly used** = it often collapses into "someone who feels familiar to us," which can unintentionally filter out strong candidates with different backgrounds, communication styles, or working experience — especially candidates coming from more structured, process-driven companies
- **A better lens: culture add** = instead of asking "does this person feel like us," ask "does this person share our core values, and what do they bring that we currently lack" (for example, sales process discipline the team has never had to build)
- **What to actually protect** = the small number of non-negotiable values (for example, direct feedback, customer honesty, no politics) — not surface traits like communication style, seniority background, or industry pedigree
- This is a **culture add vs culture fit** conversation, not a "find someone just like us" conversation — reframing it this way earlier prevents biased screening later

## Step 2 — Writing the Culture Section of the Job Description

**Sample prompt:**

> "Write the 'How we work' section of the JD for this Head of Sales role. We're a 60-person startup, high trust, low hierarchy, direct feedback culture. Avoid generic buzzwords."

**Sample JD section generated:**

```txt
How we work

We're a 60-person team that still operates with startup instincts: fast
decisions, direct feedback, and low tolerance for politics or process for
its own sake. A few things that are true here:

- Feedback is direct and frequent — we'd rather have an uncomfortable
  conversation in week one than a quiet resentment in month six
- Ownership is real — you will build the sales process from a fairly
  blank slate, not inherit a mature playbook
- We debate decisions openly, then commit fully once a call is made
- Titles matter less than judgment — you'll work directly with the
  founders, not through layers of management

What we're looking for in a Head of Sales:

- Someone who has built sales process and infrastructure before, not
  just managed an existing one — we need the "how," not just the "what"
- Comfortable operating with more ambiguity than a larger company,
  while still bringing real process discipline with them
- Gives and receives direct feedback well — this is not a fit for
  someone who prefers formal, hierarchical communication

Not a fit if:
- You need a fully built playbook, team, and process in place before you start
- You're looking for a purely execution-focused role without ownership
  over how the function is built
```

**Skill note:**

> ⚠️ Notice what this JD does *not* say: it doesn't ask for someone who "feels like a startup person" or has "founder energy." It names specific, observable working values (direct feedback, ownership, low hierarchy) instead of vague culture-fit language that tends to reward similarity over alignment.

## Step 3 — Building the Values-Based Interview Question Set

**Sample prompt:**

> "Create an interview question set that tests real values alignment for this role, not just personality or likability. I need to know what a strong answer sounds like."

**Generated question set:**

### Values and Working-Style Questions (for the hiring panel)

| # | Question | What it tests | What a strong answer looks like |
|---|---------|---------------|----------------------------------|
| 1 | "Tell me about a time you gave a direct piece of feedback to your manager or a senior leader. What happened?" | Comfort with direct, low-hierarchy feedback culture | Describes a specific, real situation, the actual words used, and the outcome — not a vague "I believe in transparency" statement |
| 2 | "Describe a sales process you built from scratch, not inherited. What was the messiest part?" | Genuine ownership and builder mindset vs pure execution experience | Talks concretely about ambiguity, false starts, and iteration — not just a polished final-state description |
| 3 | "Tell me about a decision you disagreed with that your team or leadership made. What did you do?" | Disagree-and-commit behavior vs quiet resentment or unilateral override | Shows they voiced disagreement directly, then either changed the decision through argument or committed fully — not passive compliance or going around the decision |
| 4 | "What part of a more structured, process-heavy environment would you genuinely miss here?" | Honest self-awareness about the trade-offs of this environment | Names something specific and credible (for example, defined approval chains, dedicated ops support) rather than claiming no trade-offs exist |
| 5 | "How do you handle a high performer who is difficult to work with?" | Alignment on "no politics" and direct-feedback norms under pressure | Describes addressing the behavior directly and early, not protecting the person because of their output |

### Screening Questions (HR can ask directly in the phone screen)

| # | Question | What it tests |
|---|---------|--------------|
| 1 | "What does 'low hierarchy' mean to you in practice, based on places you've worked?" | Whether their definition matches the company's actual norms, not just the word |
| 2 | "What's the most direct feedback you've ever received at work, and how did you respond in the moment?" | Genuine comfort with directness, not just stated comfort |
| 3 | "What would make you leave a role in the first six months?" | Surfaces mismatches early, before a full loop is invested |

## Step 4 — Evaluating References for Culture Signals

**Sample prompt:**

> "How do I structure reference checks to actually surface culture and working-style signals, not just 'were they good at the job'?"

**Reference check checklist generated by skill:**

### ✅ Strong signals to listen for

- [ ] Reference describes specific examples of the candidate giving or receiving direct feedback
- [ ] Reference can describe how the candidate handled disagreement with leadership, with a concrete story
- [ ] Reference mentions the candidate built or changed a process, not just operated within one
- [ ] Reference describes the candidate as someone who raised problems early rather than let them surface later
- [ ] When asked about weaknesses, the reference gives a real, specific answer rather than a deflected non-answer

### ⚠️ Worth probing further

- Reference only speaks in general positive terms ("great person," "everyone loved them") without specific examples
- Reference struggles to describe how the candidate handled conflict or disagreement
- Reference describes the candidate as needing significant structure or oversight to be effective

### ❌ Concerning signals

- Reference describes the candidate as avoiding direct conversations or escalating through others instead
- Reference mentions the candidate struggled in flatter, lower-process environments in the past
- Multiple references independently mention the same interpersonal friction pattern

## Step 6 — Understanding an Ambiguous Interview Answer

**Sample prompt:**

> "A candidate said: 'At my last company, when I disagreed with a pricing decision from the VP, I raised it once in the room, got overruled, and then just made sure my team executed it well without re-litigating it afterward.' Is this a strong culture signal for us?"

**Skill explanation:**

- **What this shows** = the candidate raised disagreement directly and in the room, rather than staying silent or going around leadership afterward — this matches the "direct feedback" value well
- **What this also shows** = once overruled, they committed fully rather than quietly undermining the decision or continuing to relitigate it with their team — this is a strong "disagree and commit" signal
- **What it does not tell you** = whether they raised the disagreement early enough, or whether they pushed hard enough before accepting the decision — worth a specific follow-up like "how hard did you push back before dropping it?"
- **Assessment: Strong signal, with one follow-up needed** — this is a meaningfully better answer than either "I just went along with it" (no directness) or "I kept pushing after the decision was made" (no commitment), but it's worth clarifying how much genuine debate happened before the decision was final.

## Step 7 — Distinguishing Culture Fit From Culture Add

**Sample prompt:**

> "One of our interviewers said this candidate 'doesn't quite feel like us.' What should I do with that feedback?"

**Skill explanation:**

| Dimension | Culture Fit (as commonly misused) | Culture Add |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|--------------|
| Core question | "Does this person feel similar to our existing team?" | "Does this person share our core values, and what do they bring that we lack?" |
| What it evaluates | Communication style, background, personal rapport | Alignment on 2–4 non-negotiable working values, plus distinct strengths |
| Common failure mode | Filters out candidates who think or communicate differently, reinforces homogeneity | Requires interviewers to separate "different from us" from "misaligned with our values" |
| Good interview follow-up | "Push the interviewer: what specifically felt off — a stated value, or just a different style?" | Ask what specific gap the candidate would fill that the current team doesn't cover |
| When it's a real signal | Rarely, on its own | When tied to a specific, named value (for example, avoiding direct feedback) |

> When you hear "doesn't feel like us," always ask the interviewer to translate that into a specific, observable value or behavior. If they can point to one (for example, "they deflected every question about giving direct feedback"), that's a real signal. If they can't get more specific than a feeling, it's worth treating as a possible bias flag rather than a disqualifier.

## Full Culture-Aligned Hiring Workflow Summary

```txt
Reframe "culture fit" concern into named, specific values
                    ↓
Write JD with observable behaviors, not vague culture language
                    ↓
Screen: ask 2–3 targeted values questions in the phone screen
                    ↓
Interview loop: values-based behavioral questions + sales competency
                    ↓
Reference checks: probe for concrete feedback and ownership examples
                    ↓
Score sales skill and culture alignment on SEPARATE scorecards
                    ↓
HR debrief: translate any "doesn't feel like us" feedback into specifics
                    ↓
Offer / No Offer decision
```

### Common HR Mistakes When Hiring for Culture

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Treating "culture fit" as a stand-in for personal rapport | Require interviewers to name a specific value or behavior, not a feeling |
| Mixing culture and skills into one combined score | Score them separately — a values gap and a skills gap require different decisions |
| Screening out candidates from more structured environments by default | Ask what they'd genuinely miss, rather than assuming they can't adapt |
| Only checking culture alignment in one interview | Build values signals into the phone screen, interview loop, and references |
| Accepting vague reference answers ("great person," "everyone loved them") | Push references for specific, concrete examples of feedback and conflict handling |
| Assuming stated company values reflect actual day-to-day behavior | Validate values against real decisions (who got promoted, what got tolerated), not just the values page |
| Using "not a culture fit" as a catch-all rejection reason | Document the specific value or behavior that drove the decision, for consistency and fairness |
