# Building a Capability Framework and Leadership Pipeline for a Scaling Company

## Context

You are the L&D Manager at a 220-person B2B SaaS company that has grown from 80 to 220 people in 18 months. During that growth, people management was handled informally — strong individual contributors were promoted to manage teams without any structured development or readiness criteria. Now the Head of People has identified three critical problems: twelve of the company's fifteen team leads have never received formal management training, two high-potential engineers have resigned in the last quarter citing "no visible career path," and the CEO is asking the board to see a leadership succession plan for the first time. You have been asked to build the capability framework, the leadership development program, and a career pathway structure — starting essentially from a list of problems with no existing L&D infrastructure.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "We promoted a lot of individual contributors into management during rapid growth, we have two high-potential resignations citing no career path, and the CEO suddenly needs a succession plan for the board. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — three simultaneous problems with no existing L&D infrastructure — start with two priority areas before building any programs:

**Retention risk containment (the high-potential resignation concern):**

- Have direct, individual conversations with any remaining identified high-potentials to understand whether career visibility is the actual driver or a proxy for a broader dissatisfaction
- Identify whether a quick, interim signal — a visible development conversation, a named project opportunity, a role title acknowledgment — can reduce the immediate flight risk while the longer framework is built
- Move quickly — the second departure will be harder to explain than the first, and it will signal to the broader team that the company has no development path to offer

**Structural diagnosis before program design (the manager capability and succession concerns):**

- Assess the current state of the twelve team leads before designing any training — are they struggling with the same gaps or different ones?
- Identify what "succession readiness" actually means in your company's context before presenting a plan to the CEO — a succession plan with undefined criteria will not hold up to board scrutiny
- Map the capability gaps and the career path gaps separately; they need different interventions and trying to solve both at once with a single training program is the most common failure mode

> **Avoid:** Do not open a learning platform vendor conversation or begin designing a program until the capability gaps are diagnosed. An LMS with no skills framework behind it, and a leadership program with no readiness criteria, will appear to solve the problem while leaving the underlying gaps untouched.

## Step 2 — Modeling Structural Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few options for addressing both the manager capability gap and the career path visibility problem so I can compare trade-offs before proposing anything."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Unified leadership development program (managers and high-potentials in one cohort):**

```txt
Approach: 6-month cohort-based program combining management fundamentals,
          leadership skills, and career development for all 12 team leads
          and identified high-potentials simultaneously
Investment: Moderate — facilitator time, content licensing, manager time
Trade-off: Builds community and visible signal quickly, but mixing
           current managers with aspiring managers creates conflicting
           needs in the same room; high-potentials may feel their
           development is being treated as equivalent to fixing a
           management gap, which can land poorly
```

**Option B — Parallel tracks (manager capability program separate from career development framework):**

```txt
Approach: Manager fundamentals program for the 12 team leads;
          separate career pathway map and IDP process for
          high-potentials and individual contributors
Timeline: Manager program: 90-day cohort; career framework: 60-day build
Trade-off: Addresses both problems with appropriate separation, but
           requires more simultaneous L&D bandwidth; risk is the
           career framework taking longer than the retention window allows
```

**Option C — Sequenced approach (career visibility fix first, manager program second):**

```txt
Approach: Career pathway map and visible development conversations
          launched in 4-6 weeks to address retention risk; manager
          fundamentals program sequenced in the following quarter
Timeline: Career framework: 4-6 weeks; manager program: quarter 2
Trade-off: Addresses the highest-urgency problem (flight risk) first,
           but defers the manager capability problem for another quarter
           while team leads continue managing without development support
```

**Benchmark context:**

Research on manager effectiveness development generally shows that cohort-based programs with peer learning components outperform self-paced individual training for behavior change, particularly for first-time managers. Career frameworks are most credible when they are co-created with the teams they apply to rather than handed down from HR, which adds 2-4 weeks to the design timeline but substantially increases adoption.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the dual urgency — retention risk that cannot wait a quarter, and manager gaps that are actively compounding team health — a modified Option B is the strongest path: a rapid-build career visibility framework within 4-6 weeks, running simultaneously with the manager program design, so the manager cohort launches in the same quarter. This gives the CEO a visible succession-readiness signal and gives high-potentials a visible development path without making them wait for the manager program to finish first.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Underlying Capability Gaps

**Sample prompt:**

> "Twelve team leads have never had formal management training. Before I design anything, I need to understand what they actually need versus what I assume they need. How do I diagnose this?"

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before designing any program, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Role clarity and decision rights:**

- Do team leads have a clear understanding of what their management role includes versus what they are expected to leave to their own manager?
- Is the capability gap about skill (they do not know how to have a performance conversation) or about clarity (they do not know it is their job to have one)?

**2. Specific skill gaps versus confidence gaps:**

- Are team leads avoiding management conversations because they lack the skill, or because they lack the confidence and organizational backing to act?
- A team lead who has never been told "you are empowered to make this call" will look like they have a capability gap when they actually have a role clarity and psychological safety gap

**3. Workload and time constraints:**

- How much time are team leads actually spending on people management versus individual contributor work?
- If team leads are still carrying a full IC workload, no management training will transfer — the structural condition for management work needs to exist before the training lands

**4. Individual variation across the twelve:**

- Are all twelve in the same situation, or are there two or three who are further along and could be developed as peer coaches rather than program participants?
- The strongest team leads in the group are often the most underused L&D resource — identifying them early saves facilitation cost and builds credibility internally

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present the manager gap as a training problem that a program will fix. Bring a clear picture of which gaps are skill-based and addressable through learning, which are role clarity gaps addressable through expectation-setting, and which are structural gaps (workload, time, authority) that need to be fixed before any training lands.

## Step 4 — Building the L&D Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build an L&D plan that addresses the manager capability gap, the career visibility problem, and gives the CEO something credible to show the board — using a structured framework."

**Skill response — phased L&D plan:**

| Phase | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Diagnosis | Not done | Capability assessment with all 12 team leads; career aspiration conversations with high-potentials |
| Career Framework | Not in place | Build career pathway maps for each function with visible criteria; launch IDP process |
| Manager Development | Not in place | Design 90-day cohort program with peer learning, coaching, and real-work application components |
| Succession Readiness | Not defined | Define succession criteria for key roles; map current readiness against those criteria |
| Measurement | Not planned | Define behavior change and business impact metrics before any program launches |

**Stakeholder-specific sequencing:**

1. High-potentials and identified flight risks — individual development conversations within 2 weeks, before any program is announced, given the retention urgency
2. Team leads — capability assessment conversations framed as development-focused, not evaluative, before program design is finalized
3. CEO and Head of People — present a phased plan with a succession readiness framework for the board before the full program is complete; the framework itself is a credible deliverable even before the program runs
4. Broader organization — career pathway maps published once validated, with manager briefing before employee communication

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequencing matters: team leads should not learn about a "management capability program" before having an individual conversation about their own development — that framing will land as a performance concern, not a development investment
- Build in explicit co-creation with team leads on the career frameworks; frameworks handed down without their input will be seen as HR's framework, not the organization's

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the board succession plan request as a deliverable that requires a complete leadership program to be finished first. A well-structured succession readiness framework with defined criteria and current-state gap mapping is a credible board deliverable on its own — it shows maturity and a plan, which is what the board is actually asking for.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this L&D plan to the CEO and Head of People before the board presentation. How do I frame it given we are starting from essentially no L&D infrastructure?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership responds to a plan that shows a credible sequence with visible near-term deliverables rather than a long-horizon program with nothing to show immediately. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Name the current state honestly and frame the plan:**

"We have three capability problems running in parallel: a manager readiness gap across twelve team leads, a career visibility gap driving retention risk among high-potentials, and an undefined succession framework. I've modeled the options and I'm recommending a phased plan — career framework in 4-6 weeks, manager cohort launching in parallel, and a succession readiness map ready for the board in 8 weeks."

**Part 2 — Name the current risk directly:**

"The two high-potential departures are not isolated. Without a visible career framework in place within the next 4-6 weeks, we should expect continued attrition among the people we most need to retain. The manager capability gap is also actively compounding team health — under-supported team leads are the most common source of early attrition in high-growth companies."

**Part 3 — Present the plan and ask for what you need:**

"I need three things to move: protected time from team leads for the capability diagnostic conversations this week, CEO sponsorship of the career framework launch to signal it is a company commitment rather than an HR project, and a 2-hour working session with you to define the succession readiness criteria before I build the board-facing framework."

> **Key principle:** Leadership at high-growth companies responds well to a plan that shows they will have something credible to show externally within weeks, not quarters — frame near-term deliverables explicitly and sequence the longer program as the reinforcement, not the headline.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft L&D Content and Frameworks

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft the career pathway maps, the manager development program outline, and the succession readiness framework. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without producing generic HR templates?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the specific roles, levels, capability gaps identified in the diagnostic, and the tone appropriate for your organization before generating anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am the L&D Manager building a capability framework for a 220-person
B2B SaaS company that has grown rapidly and has no existing L&D
infrastructure.

Context:
- 12 team leads promoted from IC roles with no formal management development
- High-potential attrition citing no visible career path
- CEO needs a succession readiness framework for a board presentation
- Engineering, Product, and GTM functions each need separate career pathways

Please:
1. Draft a career pathway framework for an engineering function with
   3 tracks (IC, technical lead, management) and visible progression
   criteria at each level
2. Draft a 90-day first-time manager cohort program outline with
   module topics, peer learning components, and a real-work
   application project structure
3. Draft a succession readiness framework with defined criteria for
   key leadership roles, a current-state readiness rating method,
   and a gap-to-action mapping format suitable for a board presentation
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with measurement planning:**

```txt
Based on this plan, draft a 90-day L&D program measurement framework
with specific behavior change indicators and business impact metrics
to track at the 30, 60, and 90-day marks to confirm the programs are
building real capability rather than producing completion metrics alone.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting structured career pathway criteria quickly across multiple functions
- Generating program outlines with module sequencing and learning objectives
- Producing succession framework templates suitable for executive and board audiences

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Judgment on which capability criteria are appropriate for your company's specific stage and culture
- Reading the room in diagnostic conversations with team leads and high-potentials
- The organizational credibility that comes from frameworks co-created with the people they apply to

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "The manager program is launching next month and the career frameworks are being published this week. How do I make sure none of this quietly fades once the launch energy passes?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Are team leads actively applying the management frameworks introduced in the program, or are they reverting to pre-program behavior under workload pressure?
- Are high-potentials and ICs engaging with the career pathway maps and having development conversations with their managers, or have the frameworks been published and ignored?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Is there observable behavior change in how team leads are running 1:1s, giving feedback, and handling performance conversations?
- Have any high-potential flight risks stabilized, or is the attrition pattern continuing despite the career framework launch?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would team leads describe the manager program as having changed how they work day to day, or as a series of sessions they attended but did not transfer?
- Is the succession readiness framework being used actively in talent reviews, or has it become a static document?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Manager cohort behavior change check-in | L&D Manager + cohort facilitator | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Career framework adoption pulse check | L&D Manager + HRBPs | 30, 60, 90 days |
| High-potential retention and development status | Head of People | Monthly |
| Succession readiness framework update | L&D Manager + CEO | Quarterly |

> **Common finding at high-growth companies:** L&D programs frequently show strong enthusiasm immediately after launch because the investment itself signals that the company cares. The real test is whether behavior has genuinely changed 60–90 days later — without a deliberate transfer and reinforcement cadence, informal reversion to pre-program patterns is the most common failure mode, and it is invisible without a check-in structure to surface it.

## Full L&D Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (rapid scaling, capability gap, retention risk, leadership request, board requirement)
                    ↓
Diagnose the capability gaps and career visibility problems before designing any program
                    ↓
Model multiple strategic options and compare trade-offs
                    ↓
Build a phased L&D plan sequenced with the urgency and infrastructure realities
                    ↓
Present the recommendation to leadership with near-term deliverables and risk framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft career frameworks, program outlines, and succession documentation
                    ↓
Design a 90-day stabilization plan with defined behavior change checkpoints
                    ↓
Report on capability progress and program adoption to leadership at each checkpoint
```

### Common L&D Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Launching an LMS or program before diagnosing what gaps actually exist | Run a capability diagnostic with affected managers and employees before designing any program |
| Finalizing one program option without modeling alternatives | Model 2–3 L&D approaches and compare trade-offs before recommending one |
| Treating manager capability gaps as training problems when they are role clarity or workload problems | Diagnose whether gaps are skill-based, clarity-based, or structural before designing any intervention |
| Building career frameworks in HR and publishing them without co-creation | Involve the teams the frameworks apply to in the design phase; frameworks imposed from HR are rarely adopted |
| Assuming L&D impact is visible from completion metrics alone | Define behavior change and business impact measures before launch, not after |
| Treating career visibility and manager development as the same problem | Diagnose and build them separately; they require different interventions and different timelines |
| Skipping a stabilization and transfer plan after launch | Build a 90-day check-in cadence with named owners before declaring the program complete |
