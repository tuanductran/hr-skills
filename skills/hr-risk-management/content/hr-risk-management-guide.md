# HR Risk Management

## Overview

HR risk is often treated as a compliance checklist rather than a continuous management discipline. The organizations that manage it well treat it the same way they treat financial or operational risk: with regular identification cycles, documented mitigation plans, owners, and escalation paths.

This guide covers how to build and maintain a practical HR risk management framework — not a theoretical one.

## The Four HR Risk Categories

### Compliance and legal risk

Violations of employment law, anti-discrimination statutes, wage and hour regulations, workplace safety requirements, and data privacy obligations. This category tends to be the most visible because it produces external consequences (fines, lawsuits, regulatory action).

### People and talent risk

Inability to attract or retain critical talent, unplanned attrition of key roles, succession gaps in leadership, and workforce capability misalignment with business strategy. This category often builds slowly and is underestimated until a business-critical role is vacant.

### Employee relations risk

Escalated grievances, harassment or misconduct allegations, hostile work environment claims, retaliation risks from disciplinary action, and breakdown of employee trust in management. Poor employee relations risk management tends to produce clustered incidents rather than isolated ones.

### Operational HR risk

HR process failures: payroll errors, HRIS data integrity issues, onboarding failures for critical hires, I-9 documentation gaps, benefits administration errors, and HR system outages. These risks are often undercounted because individual incidents seem minor until they accumulate.

## Building the HR Risk Register

A risk register is the core tool. It should be a living document, not an annual exercise.

### Minimum fields per risk entry

- **Risk description** — specific, not generic. "Key-person dependency in engineering leadership" is useful. "Talent risk" is not.
- **Category** — compliance/legal, people/talent, employee relations, operational
- **Likelihood** — assessed on a defined scale (high/medium/low or 1-5)
- **Impact** — assessed on a defined scale, defined by business consequence not HR inconvenience
- **Risk rating** — likelihood × impact, used for prioritization
- **Owner** — a named person, not a function
- **Current controls** — what is already in place to reduce this risk
- **Mitigation actions** — what additional steps are planned
- **Review date** — when this entry is next reassessed

### What to include that most HR teams miss

- Concentration risk in critical skill sets (when 1-2 people hold unique knowledge)
- Regulatory change risk (upcoming legal changes that create new obligations)
- AI and technology deployment risk (new tools creating new compliance or employee relations exposure)
- Third-party workforce risk (contractors, vendors, and outsourced functions that carry employment-related liability)

## Conducting the Risk Identification Cycle

Run a formal risk identification cycle at least twice a year, and when significant organizational changes occur (restructuring, M&A, leadership change, rapid growth, new market entry).

### Sources to draw from

- Open HR cases and litigation history
- Exit interview themes
- Engagement survey data and trends
- Compliance audit findings
- External regulatory developments
- Business strategy changes (growth plans, new geographies, workforce transformation)
- Near-misses that did not escalate to formal incidents

### Who to involve

The CHRO or HR director should not conduct risk identification alone. Pull in:

- Legal counsel for compliance and regulatory intelligence
- Finance for workforce cost and headcount risk
- Business unit heads for people and talent risk specific to their functions
- IT and security for data privacy and HR technology risk
- The CEO or COO for strategic workforce risk

## Escalation Design

Not all risks escalate through the same path. Define escalation thresholds before an incident rather than improvising during one.

### Escalation triggers by category

**Compliance/legal:** Any credible allegation of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation; any regulatory inquiry; any wage or benefits calculation error affecting more than a small number of employees.

**People/talent:** Unplanned departure of a C-suite, VP, or other role with no succession plan; attrition rate exceeding defined threshold in a critical function; failed offer for a business-critical role after extended search.

**Employee relations:** Any allegation involving a senior leader; any situation with litigation indicators; any pattern of complaints suggesting systemic issues rather than isolated incidents.

**Operational:** Payroll error affecting more than a defined percentage of employees; HRIS data breach; I-9 audit trigger; benefits enrollment failure affecting a material number of employees.

## Key Metrics for HR Risk Monitoring

These metrics signal emerging risk before it becomes an incident:

- Voluntary attrition rate by function and level (rising attrition in critical functions is an early warning)
- Time-to-fill for critical roles (lengthening indicates talent risk)
- Open HR cases by category and age (accumulating unresolved cases indicate employee relations risk)
- Compliance training completion rate (declining completion is a leading indicator of compliance risk)
- Grievance and complaint volume trend (directional change matters more than absolute level)
- Manager effectiveness scores (low-scoring managers are a concentrated employee relations risk)
