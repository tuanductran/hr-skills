# Building an HR Risk Register

## Overview

An HR risk register turns vague unease about "things that could go wrong" into a structured, ownable, and reviewable inventory. Without one, HR risk management defaults to reacting to whichever issue is loudest this week — usually employee relations fires — while slower-moving risks like documentation gaps, key-person dependency, or pay equity drift go unmonitored until they become expensive.

A good risk register does three things a mental list can't: it forces explicit likelihood and impact estimates, it assigns a named owner to each risk, and it creates a review cadence that catches drift before it becomes a crisis.

## Risk Categories

Group risks into buckets so ownership and mitigation strategy stay clear. Most HR risk registers use five categories:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Compliance | Wage and hour violations, misclassification, missing I-9s, EEO reporting gaps |
| Operational | Key person dependency, understaffed HR function, broken onboarding process |
| People | Toxic manager patterns, unresolved harassment complaints, burnout clusters |
| Reputational | Pay equity gaps, DEI backlash, public misconduct allegations |
| Strategic | Talent pipeline gaps for critical roles, culture erosion during rapid scaling |

A risk that spans categories — a harassment complaint is both a people risk and a compliance risk — should be logged once under its primary category with a cross-reference note, not duplicated.

## Scoring Likelihood and Impact

Use a simple 1–5 scale for both dimensions rather than a false-precision percentage. Precision here is less valuable than consistency across raters.

**Likelihood (1–5):** how probable is this risk materializing in the next 12 months, given current controls?

**Impact (1–5):** if it materializes, how severe is the effect on legal exposure, cost, retention, or reputation?

Multiply the two for a risk score (1–25). Scores above 15 need an active mitigation plan and executive visibility, not just a note in the register. Scores below 6 can be monitored on a longer cycle.

Avoid the common trap of every risk scoring "high" — if 80% of entries land above 15, the scale isn't discriminating and the register loses its prioritization value. Recalibrate against a few clearly low-impact risks as anchors.

## Register Fields

At minimum, each entry should capture:

- **Risk description** — one sentence, specific enough to be actionable ("Overtime tracking for hourly staff at Site B is inconsistent" beats "wage and hour risk")
- **Category** — from the five buckets above
- **Likelihood and impact scores** — with the resulting risk score
- **Current controls** — what's already in place, even if imperfect
- **Owner** — a named individual, not a function
- **Mitigation plan and target date** — for anything above the review threshold
- **Status** — open, in progress, mitigated, accepted
- **Last reviewed date**

Resist adding fields "just in case." A register with 20 columns doesn't get maintained; one with 8 does.

## Review Cadence

- **Quarterly:** full register review with HR leadership — re-score every open risk, close resolved ones, add new ones surfaced since the last cycle
- **After major events:** trigger an off-cycle review after a restructuring, acquisition, executive departure, or significant employee relations case — these events routinely create or elevate risks that a calendar-based cadence would catch too late
- **Annually:** present a summarized view to executive leadership or the board, focused on the top 5–10 risks by score and trend direction (improving, stable, worsening)

## Common Failure Modes

- **The register becomes a compliance-only list.** People and strategic risks get dropped because they're harder to quantify. Keep at least one representative from each category even if scoring feels subjective.
- **No one owns follow-through.** A risk with an owner but no target date for mitigation just sits. Every above-threshold risk needs both.
- **The register is built once and never revisited.** A risk register that isn't reviewed quarterly is a historical document, not a risk management tool.
- **Scores are inflated to justify headcount asks.** This erodes trust in the register as an honest instrument and should be actively guarded against during calibration sessions.

## Linking the Register to Escalation

The register should feed directly into an escalation protocol: any risk newly scored above the high-risk threshold, or any risk whose status moves from "mitigated" back to "open," should trigger a defined notification path — typically HR leadership within 48 hours, and executive leadership within a week for legal-exposure risks. Without this link, the register documents risk without actually managing it.
