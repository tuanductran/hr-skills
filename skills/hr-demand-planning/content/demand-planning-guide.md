# Workforce Demand Planning

## Overview

Workforce demand planning is the process of translating business activity into headcount requirements — answering how many people, with what skills, in what locations, are needed to deliver the business plan. It is the demand side of the workforce planning equation, and without it, hiring and development decisions are disconnected from actual business need.

## Gathering Demand Signals

Demand signals come from multiple sources that need to be consolidated and reconciled.

**Business unit plans:** Leaders submit headcount requirements tied to their approved business plans. These are the primary signal but require challenge — managers systematically request more than they need.

**Revenue and volume models:** In businesses where headcount scales with output (customer support, sales, operations), demand can be modeled from revenue or volume targets using productivity ratios.

**Project and initiative pipelines:** Approved strategic initiatives create time-bound demand for specific skills. Project-based demand needs to be tracked separately from run-rate headcount.

**Technology change:** Automation, AI deployment, or system changes that affect productivity change the headcount required to deliver the same output.

## Demand Modeling

Translate business signals into headcount requirements:

- Establish productivity ratios for driver-based functions (revenue per sales head, tickets per support agent)
- Apply attrition assumptions to existing headcount to determine gross replacement need
- Separate approved demand (tied to funded business plans) from aspirational demand (wish-list additions)
- Create a time-phased view: when is each role needed, not just how many are needed in total

## Demand and Supply Alignment

The gap between demand and supply is the net hiring need. This gap should be reviewed with Finance before recruiting begins:
- Demand that exceeds budget requires prioritization — which roles are funded and which are deferred?
- Supply surplus (headcount exceeding demand) surfaces restructuring or redeployment decisions
- Skill-specific gaps may require build/buy/borrow decisions beyond simple headcount

## Governance

Demand should be locked at defined planning cycle milestones before recruiting begins. Mid-cycle demand changes — the constant "we need to hire someone now that we did not plan for" — are the primary driver of TA team overload and plan-versus-actual variance. Governance that requires a defined approval process for unplanned demand requests reduces this significantly.
