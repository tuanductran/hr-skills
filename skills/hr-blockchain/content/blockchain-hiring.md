# Blockchain Hiring

## Overview

Hiring for blockchain and Web3 engineering roles requires distinguishing genuine protocol and smart contract engineering skill from surface-level enthusiasm, in a space that evolves quickly and spans several largely non-interchangeable technical ecosystems.

## Main topics

### Role types

Common roles include Smart Contract Engineer (writes and tests on-chain contract logic), Protocol Engineer (works on the underlying blockchain/consensus layer), and Blockchain Security Engineer (audits contracts for vulnerabilities).

### Ecosystem specificity

Skills in Solidity/EVM-based chains, Rust/Solana, and Move-based chains (Aptos, Sui) are not fully interchangeable; job descriptions and screening should be explicit about the required ecosystem.

### Security emphasis

Because smart contract bugs can directly result in financial loss, security awareness — familiarity with common vulnerability classes (reentrancy, integer overflow, access-control errors) and audit processes — is a critical hiring criterion, not a nice-to-have.

### Verifying real experience

Open-source contribution history, deployed contract addresses, and hackathon participation often provide more reliable signal than resume claims alone, given how new and fast-moving the space is.

## Practical guidance

- Specify the required blockchain ecosystem and language explicitly in job postings and screening.
- Include a security-focused interview component given the high stakes of on-chain vulnerabilities.
- Verify claimed experience against shipped code, contribution history, or deployed contracts where possible.
- Avoid hype-driven language in postings; focus on concrete technical requirements.

## Conclusion

Effective blockchain hiring combines ecosystem-specific technical screening, a strong security evaluation component, and verification of real shipped experience — critical in a domain where engineering mistakes carry direct financial consequences.
