# Launching hiring in a new country

## Context

A US-headquartered SaaS company wants to hire its first three engineers in Germany. The company has no existing German entity and the timeline is six months. HR must determine whether to use an EOR or establish a GmbH, set up payroll and benefits, and make the first hires compliantly.

## Step 1: Decide on entry structure

Sample prompt: "Compare entity establishment vs. EOR for hiring our first three engineers in Germany, including cost, timeline, and compliance risk."

Expected response: EOR is faster (2-4 weeks) and avoids upfront entity cost but is more expensive per employee and limits some employment structures. GmbH establishment takes 3-6 months and requires a local registered address, notary fees, and corporate bank account. At three headcount, EOR is typically the right first step; revisit the entity decision at 10-15 employees.

## Step 2: Build the HR readiness checklist

Sample prompt: "Write a checklist of employment law requirements we must address before making our first hire in Germany."

Expected response:

- Employment contracts must be in writing and include probation period (up to 6 months), notice requirements, working time rules, and vacation entitlement (minimum 20 days by law, market standard 25-30).
- Statutory benefits include statutory health insurance contribution, pension contribution, unemployment insurance, and accident insurance — all as employer co-contributions.
- Works council rights apply once 5 or more employees are present; plan for consultation obligations from the start.
- GDPR applies to all employee data from day one.

## Step 3: Design local compensation

Sample prompt: "Benchmark compensation for a Senior Software Engineer in Berlin against local market norms."

Expected response: A market range in EUR covering base salary, an outline of the statutory social contributions layered on top (approximately 20-21% employer burden), and common supplemental benefits (meal vouchers, public transport subsidy, home office allowance) that are competitive in the Berlin tech market.

## Step 4: Prepare managers

Sample prompt: "Prepare a briefing for our US hiring managers on management practice differences when leading a team in Germany."

Expected response: Key differences include stronger employee protections and works council rights limiting unilateral changes, direct feedback culture, preference for structure and clear role definition, legally mandated vacation usage, and lower tolerance for last-minute schedule changes. Recommend a pre-hire cultural orientation session for any manager leading a cross-border team for the first time.

## Workflow summary

Use `hr-global-expansion` to sequence entry-structure decisions, compliance readiness, compensation design, and manager preparation before the first hire lands — avoiding costly corrections after employment begins.
