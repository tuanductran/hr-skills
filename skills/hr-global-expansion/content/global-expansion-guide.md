# HR for Global Expansion

## Overview

Expanding into new countries is one of the most HR-intensive business decisions an organization can make. Every new jurisdiction brings distinct employment law, labor market conditions, compensation norms, cultural practices, and mandatory benefits. Getting the people foundation right determines whether the expansion can execute its business plan.

## Entry Strategy: EOR vs. Legal Entity

The first HR decision in a new country is how to employ people there.

**Employer of Record (EOR):** A third-party organization employs workers in the target country on your behalf, handling compliance, payroll, and benefits. Your organization manages the workers' day-to-day work.

Advantages: fast (weeks, not months), no entity setup, compliance risk managed by the EOR.
Disadvantages: higher per-employee cost, limited policy control, not suitable for large or permanent workforces.

**Legal entity:** Registering a subsidiary or branch and employing directly.

Advantages: full control, lower per-employee cost at scale, required for certain regulated activities.
Disadvantages: 3-6 months minimum to establish, ongoing administrative overhead, full compliance responsibility.

The common pattern: start with EOR for the first 2-5 employees while assessing the market, establish an entity when the presence is confirmed at scale.

## Compliance Essentials

Every country requires research across these categories before the first hire:

- **Employment contract requirements:** Form, language, required terms, probation rules
- **Working hours and overtime:** Maximum hours, overtime rates, rest requirements
- **Mandatory social contributions:** Employer rates, employee rates, salary caps
- **Mandatory benefits:** Leave minimums, parental leave, public holidays
- **Termination requirements:** Notice periods, severance, redundancy process
- **Work authorization:** Requirements for foreign workers
- **Data privacy:** Applicable data protection law for employee data

Do not rely on general knowledge for compliance in specific countries. Engage local employment counsel for each new market.

## Vietnam Expansion Context

For organizations entering Vietnam:

- Employment contracts must be in Vietnamese (bilingual contracts acceptable; Vietnamese is the governing language)
- Social insurance (BHXH/BHYT/BHTN): employer contributions approximately 17.5-21.5% of salary up to statutory ceiling
- Probation periods defined by the Labor Code, varying by role type
- Termination requires specific grounds defined in the Labor Code — at-will employment does not exist
- Work permits required for foreign employees before employment begins
- Personal income tax withholding obligations apply from the first payroll

## Compensation in New Markets

Compensation must be calibrated to the local market — not converted from headquarters salary using exchange rates. A salary appropriate in Ho Chi Minh City is not calculated by converting a Singapore or US salary.

Sources for Vietnam compensation benchmarking: Anphabe Salary Report, Mercer Vietnam survey, Towers Watson Vietnam, and local recruiter market intelligence. LinkedIn Salary data is less reliable for Vietnam than for more mature markets.
