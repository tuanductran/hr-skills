# HR Crisis Management

## Overview

A workforce crisis is any event that disrupts the organization's ability to operate, threatens employee safety or wellbeing, or creates significant people risk. Crises range from a senior leader misconduct allegation to a natural disaster affecting a facility, from a data breach exposing employee information to a layoff of significant scale. HR's role is to prepare before crises occur, coordinate effectively during them, and lead organizational recovery after.

Organizations that manage crises well share one characteristic: they prepared before the crisis. Organizations that improvise crisis response consistently make avoidable errors that extend impact and erode trust.

## Crisis Typology for HR

### Safety and health crises

- Workplace accident or fatality
- Public health emergency affecting the workforce (pandemic, outbreak)
- Natural disaster affecting employee safety or workplace access
- Active threat or security incident

### People and conduct crises

- Serious misconduct allegation against a senior leader
- Large-scale employee relations incident
- Significant discrimination or harassment complaint with legal implications
- Whistleblower complaint triggering regulatory investigation

### Business crises with workforce impact

- Significant layoff (affecting 10%+ of workforce)
- Facility closure
- Business unit sale or spin-off
- Rapid leadership transition (sudden departure of CEO or senior executive)

### Technology and data crises

- HR data breach exposing employee personal information
- Payroll system failure affecting pay delivery
- HRIS outage affecting benefits enrollment or critical processes

## Crisis Preparation

### Crisis response plan

An HR crisis response plan defines, before any crisis occurs:
- Who is on the crisis response team and what role each plays
- Decision authority matrix: who decides what during a crisis
- Escalation triggers: what conditions require escalating from HR to executive leadership to board
- External resource contacts: employment counsel, PR/communications support, investigators, mental health EAP contacts
- Communication templates for common crisis types (layoff, leader departure, data breach notification)
- Logistics protocols: employee notification channels, media response procedures, family notification procedures in safety incidents

### Table-top exercises

The best preparation for a crisis is a simulated one. Run annual table-top exercises with the HR and leadership team: present a realistic crisis scenario and work through the response. Surface the gaps in your plan before they become gaps in a real response.

Common table-top finding: nobody knows who is authorized to communicate externally during a crisis, or the authorization chain is too long to move quickly when it matters.

## Crisis Response in the Moment

### The first 60 minutes

The first hour of a crisis response is the most consequential. Common first-hour failures:
- Key decision-makers not reachable
- No agreement on who has authority to communicate
- External communication happening before internal communication is ready
- Facts not confirmed before communication goes out

First-hour priorities:
1. Confirm the facts to the extent possible
2. Convene the crisis team
3. Assess employee safety (if relevant)
4. Identify who must be notified and in what sequence (employees, leadership, board, regulators, external parties)
5. Agree on what can be communicated now and what must wait for more information

### Communication during crisis

The principles of crisis communication:
- **Internal before external:** Employees should not learn about a crisis affecting them from media or external sources
- **Factual and calibrated:** Communicate what is known with appropriate uncertainty about what is not known yet
- **Frequent updates:** Silence during a developing crisis breeds rumor. Regular updates ("we are continuing to assess and will update you by X") are better than waiting for complete information
- **Named channels and contacts:** Provide specific channels for employees to ask questions and get support

### Employee support and wellbeing

In safety crises, the immediate priority is employee physical safety. In all crises, employee psychological safety and wellbeing require attention. Activate EAP resources and communicate their availability. Identify employees who may be most severely affected and ensure they receive direct outreach.

## Post-Crisis Recovery and Learning

Every significant crisis should produce a formal after-action review:
- What happened and what was the impact?
- What worked well in the response?
- What would we do differently?
- What changes to our crisis plan, protocols, or capabilities are needed?

Document findings and implement changes before the next crisis. Organizations that treat each crisis as a learning opportunity — without punishing individuals for decisions made in good faith with incomplete information — develop crisis management capability over time.
