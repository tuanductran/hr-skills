# Repositioning the HR function as a strategic partner

## Context

A new VP of HR joins a 600-person manufacturing and distribution company where the HR team of six is perceived as slow, reactive, and primarily administrative. The CHRO asks HR to "be more strategic," but the team has no shared goals, measures activity rather than outcomes, and spends more than 70% of its time on transactional requests. The VP needs to redesign the HR operating model, reset stakeholder expectations, and build the team's capability to deliver business value — starting with what already exists and without adding headcount.

## Step 1: Audit current HR time allocation and identify the shift

Sample prompt: "How do we shift the HR team's time allocation from predominantly administrative and reactive work toward strategic HR business partnership without adding headcount?"

Expected response:

Start with a two-week time audit where each HR team member logs how they spend time by category: transactional/administrative, reactive support, proactive partnerships, program design, and data analysis. Most HR teams discover that 60–75% of time is transactional before the audit — having the data removes the debate.

From the audit, identify:

- Which transactional tasks can be automated or self-served by managers (for example, routine policy questions answered through an internal knowledge base)
- Which tasks belong to managers rather than HR (for example, attendance tracking, scheduling conflicts)
- Which specialist activities are consuming HRBP time that a coordinator could handle

Redesign the team structure so coordinators absorb the transactional load, generalists handle operational HR partnership, and HRBPs focus on workforce planning, manager coaching, and organizational health.

## Step 2: Define HR team structure and role clarity

Sample prompt: "Design an HR team structure for a 600-person company with six HR professionals — including role definitions, reporting lines, and how HRBP, specialist, and coordinator work is divided."

Expected response:

| Role | Count | Primary focus |
|---|---|---|
| VP HR | 1 | Strategy, executive partnership, HR function leadership |
| HRBP (senior) | 2 | Business unit partnership, org health, manager coaching |
| HR Generalist | 1 | Employee relations, policy, compliance |
| HR Coordinator | 1 | Onboarding, offboarding, scheduling, HRIS data |
| Talent Acquisition | 1 | Recruiting for open roles, hiring process management |

Clear handoff points: HRBPs own manager relationships and people strategy conversations; the generalist owns ER cases and compliance; the coordinator owns all logistics and data entry; talent acquisition owns the recruiting pipeline.

## Step 3: Set HR team OKRs connected to business outcomes

Sample prompt: "Write OKRs for the HR team for the next two quarters that connect HR deliverables to measurable business outcomes rather than HR activity metrics."

Expected response:

- **Objective**: Reduce unplanned attrition in the operations function by 15% in two quarters
  - Key result 1: Complete manager effectiveness assessments for all 18 operations managers by end of Q1
  - Key result 2: Deliver targeted manager coaching plans for the five managers with the lowest team retention scores by Week 6
  - Key result 3: Conduct stay interviews with 40 high-risk employees in operations and share themes with the COO by end of Q1

- **Objective**: Shift HR team capacity from transactional to strategic work
  - Key result 1: Reduce HRBP time on administrative tasks from 65% to 35% through self-service and coordinator delegation by end of Q2
  - Key result 2: Launch manager self-service for top 15 routine HR requests by end of Q1

## Step 4: Build the quarterly HR business review

Sample prompt: "Design a quarterly HR business review for the VP HR to present to the executive team — covering what HR delivered, what the people data says about the business, and what HR recommends for next quarter."

Expected response: A structured 30-minute presentation format with four sections:

1. **People scorecard** — headcount, attrition (voluntary and involuntary), time-to-fill, engagement score trend, open ER cases
2. **What HR delivered this quarter** — against OKRs, with outcomes not activities
3. **People risk and opportunity signals** — what the data says about where business leaders need to pay attention
4. **HR recommendations for next quarter** — two to three prioritized asks or investments with business rationale

## Workflow summary

Use `hr-hr-management` to redesign the HR operating model, shift team capacity toward high-value work, and build the credibility and reporting cadence that earns HR a seat at the strategic table.
