# HR Operating Model Design

## Overview

The HR operating model defines how HR delivers its services to the organization — how HR work is organized, who does what, and how HR relates to the business it supports. The dominant model in enterprise HR — the three-legged stool of HR Business Partners, Centers of Excellence, and Shared Services — has been the standard for two decades. It is also increasingly under pressure as organizations question whether the model actually delivers the strategic partnership it promises.

This guide covers the components of the dominant HR operating model, its trade-offs, and the design decisions that determine whether it works in a specific organizational context.

## The Three-Legged Stool Model

### HR Business Partners (HRBPs)

HRBPs are embedded with business units, acting as strategic advisors and relationship managers between the business and the rest of HR. In theory, they diagnose people problems, translate business needs into HR solutions, and coordinate with CoEs and Shared Services to deliver them.

**Where it works:** When HRBPs have genuine strategic relationships with senior business leaders, when they have the diagnostic skills to distinguish structural problems from people problems from culture problems, and when the rest of the HR model (CoEs and Shared Services) is sufficiently capable that HRBPs do not need to do everything themselves.

**Where it fails:** When HRBPs become relationship managers without substantive HR expertise — conversant with the business but unable to design solutions. When there is no clear division between what HRBPs do and what CoEs do, leading to duplication and confusion. When business unit headcount is too small to justify a dedicated HRBP, but the model insists on the title.

### Centers of Excellence (CoEs)

CoEs are specialized teams with deep expertise in a specific HR domain — Talent Acquisition, Learning and Development, Compensation and Benefits, Employee Relations, Organization Development. They design programs and frameworks that the broader HR function implements.

**Where it works:** When CoE expertise is genuinely superior to what individual HRBPs or generalists could develop, when CoEs design scalable solutions rather than bespoke work for each business unit, and when there is clear governance on when business units engage CoEs versus handling locally.

**Where it fails:** When CoEs design programs that are not adopted because they are too generic, too complex, or not bought in by the business. When CoEs focus on program design and let implementation fall to HRBPs who do not have the domain expertise. When CoE capacity is insufficient for the demand they face.

### Shared Services / HR Operations

Shared Services handles transactional HR processes — payroll administration, benefits enrollment, HRIS data management, HR reporting, onboarding logistics, basic employee inquiry resolution. The purpose is to centralize high-volume, process-oriented work to achieve efficiency and consistency.

**Where it works:** When the transactional processes are well-defined, technology-enabled, and consistently executed. When employees and managers can self-serve routine needs through the HRIS without HR intervention. When Shared Services has clear escalation paths to CoEs or HRBPs for complex requests.

**Where it fails:** When the self-service layer is inadequate and every transaction requires HR involvement. When Shared Services receives work that should stay with the business (e.g., manager requests for HR to handle routine people management conversations). When Shared Services quality is poor and erodes employee trust in HR.

## Design Decisions

### What does an HRBP actually own?

The HRBP role is notoriously unclear across organizations. Before designing the model, define specifically what HRBPs do and do not do:

**Typical HRBP accountabilities in a functional model:**
- Organization diagnosis and business partnership at senior leader level
- Talent review and succession planning coordination for the business unit
- Organization design support for significant structural changes
- Employee relations escalations above a defined threshold
- Change management for significant business initiatives
- Workforce planning input from the business unit into enterprise planning

**Not typically HRBP accountabilities (should go to CoEs or Shared Services):**
- Compensation benchmarking and offer decisions (Compensation CoE)
- Training delivery (L&D CoE)
- Payroll queries (Shared Services)
- Standard onboarding (Shared Services)
- Routine ER case management below the escalation threshold

If HRBPs are doing work from the second list, they are not available for the first. This is the most common HR operating model dysfunction.

### HRBP to employee ratio

Ratios vary significantly by organizational complexity:

- Very complex (constant organizational change, high ER volume, significant people strategy work): 1 HRBP : 100-150 employees
- Typical knowledge-work organizations: 1 HRBP : 200-400 employees
- Stable, process-oriented organizations: 1 HRBP : 500-1000 employees

These are ranges, not standards. The right ratio depends on what HRBPs are accountable for and how much complexity they manage.

### Generalist vs. specialist HRBPs

In smaller organizations, HRBPs are generalists — they cover all HR domains for their business unit. In larger organizations, there may be specialist HR support aligned to business units alongside or instead of generalist HRBPs (e.g., a dedicated TA partner and a dedicated OD partner for a large business unit, rather than one generalist HRBP trying to cover everything).

Specialist alignment increases depth but reduces the single-point-of-contact simplicity. The trade-off is worth it when the domain volume justifies specialization.

### HR technology as an operating model enabler

The HR operating model cannot function without the technology layer:

- **HRIS (core):** Employee data, org structure, basic manager and employee self-service
- **ATS:** Recruiting process and pipeline management
- **LMS:** Learning content delivery and completion tracking
- **Performance management system:** Goal setting, review workflow, continuous feedback
- **Payroll:** May be HRIS-integrated or separate
- **Analytics and reporting:** People data accessible to HR and business leaders

Technology maturity significantly affects what Shared Services can absorb. An HRIS with robust self-service reduces Shared Services headcount needed for routine transactions. An HRIS with poor self-service moves that work to HRBPs or Shared Services agents, reducing HR strategic capacity.
