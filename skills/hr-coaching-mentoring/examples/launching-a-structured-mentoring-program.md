# Launching a structured mentoring program

## Context

An L&D manager at a 900-person professional services firm wants to launch a structured mentoring program for 40 high-potential individual contributors identified in the most recent talent review. The company has run informal mentoring in the past but participation dropped off after six weeks, mentors felt unprepared, and mentees reported that conversations lacked direction. The L&D manager needs to design a 6-month program with a structured framework, deliberate matching, clear mentor enablement, and a measurement plan that shows outcomes before the program ends.

## Step 1: Design the program structure

Sample prompt: "Design a 6-month structured mentoring program for 40 high-potential individual contributors — including matching criteria, conversation frameworks, check-in cadence, and how program success is measured."

Expected response:

**Program structure:**

- Duration: 6 months, with a formal mid-point check-in and a closing reflection session
- Meeting cadence: One 60-minute session per month minimum; participants encouraged to add a 30-minute check-in mid-month if needed
- Group size: 40 mentee-mentor pairs

**Matching criteria (in priority order):**

1. Development goal alignment — match mentees to mentors whose career path or domain expertise directly serves the mentee's stated goal
2. Cross-functional pairing — avoid matching within the same direct reporting line to reduce conflict of interest
3. Geography and time zone — prioritize timezone compatibility for recurring scheduling
4. Seniority gap — mentor should be at least two levels above mentee, ideally with experience in the role the mentee aspires to

**Session framework:**

- Session 1 (kickoff): Establish the mentee's development goal for the program, agree on how the relationship will work, and set ground rules for confidentiality
- Sessions 2–5 (working sessions): Mentee brings a current challenge or decision; mentor uses coaching questions before sharing their own perspective or experience
- Session 6 (closing): Mentee reflects on what they have learned and what they will do differently; mentor shares their own observations; both complete a program reflection survey

## Step 2: Enable mentors before the program starts

Sample prompt: "Create a mentor onboarding guide covering what is expected of mentors at a professional services firm, how to structure the first mentoring conversation, how to handle a mentee who disengages, and what the mentor's boundaries should be."

Expected response:

A mentor guide covering four areas:

**What mentors are and are not:**

- Mentors share experience, perspective, and honest feedback — they are not coaches, therapists, sponsors, or performance reviewers
- Mentors are not responsible for the mentee's results — they create conditions for the mentee's own thinking; the mentee owns the direction

**The first session:**

- Ask the mentee to come prepared with their development goal and one specific challenge they want to discuss
- Spend the first 20 minutes understanding the mentee's context, ambitions, and what they hope to get from this relationship
- Establish a simple agreement: how often you will meet, how you prefer to communicate between sessions, and what confidentiality means for your conversations

**When the mentee disengages:**

- If a mentee misses two consecutive sessions without notice, send a direct message acknowledging the silence and asking if they want to continue — don't assume
- If disengagement continues, contact the program coordinator rather than continuing to chase; forced mentoring is not effective

**Mentor boundaries:**

- Do not share mentee disclosures with their manager or HR unless the content involves imminent safety risk
- Do not advocate for the mentee in compensation, promotion, or performance decisions without their explicit knowledge
- If you discover a conflict of interest (for example, you may have involvement in a decision affecting the mentee), disclose it to the program coordinator immediately

## Step 3: Measure outcomes during the program

Sample prompt: "How do we measure the outcomes of a mentoring program beyond participation rates — and what leading indicators tell us whether the program is actually working before the 12-month review?"

Expected response:

**Leading indicators (measurable at the 3-month mark):**

- Session completion rate: are pairs meeting at least monthly?
- Mentee goal clarity score: from a short pulse survey — "I have a clearer sense of my development direction than I did at the start of the program" (1–5 scale)
- Mentor confidence score: "I feel prepared to support my mentee effectively" — low scores flag pairs that need a check-in
- Pair health signal: program coordinator conducts a 10-minute check-in call with each pair at the 6-week mark to surface any mismatches early

**Outcome indicators (measurable at 6 months):**

- Mentee self-report: specific capabilities developed, decisions made differently, or actions taken as a result of the mentoring relationship
- Retention: compare 12-month voluntary attrition for program participants versus comparable talent-review-identified peers who did not participate
- Career movement: promotion or stretch assignment rate for participants at the 12-month mark
