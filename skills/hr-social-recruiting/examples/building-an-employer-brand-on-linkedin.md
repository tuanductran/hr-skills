# Building an Employer Brand on LinkedIn

## Context

You are Head of Talent at a 60-person B2B SaaS company. Leadership has approved a quarterly focus on employer branding because sourcing alone isn't scaling fast enough — the company wants more inbound interest so recruiters spend less time on cold outreach. You have a company LinkedIn page with almost no activity, no defined content strategy, and no employee participation. You need to build this from scratch over one quarter.

## Step 1 — Auditing the Current State

**Sample prompt:**

> "Here's our current LinkedIn company page: [description/screenshot]. What's missing before we start posting content?"

**Expected skill response — audit checklist:**

- [ ] **About section**: Does it explain what the company does in plain language, or is it internal jargon that means nothing to an outside candidate?
- [ ] **Cover image and logo**: Current, professional, and reflective of the brand?
- [ ] **Recent activity**: Any posts in the last 90 days? Silence signals a company that isn't actively engaging its audience
- [ ] **Employee tagging**: Are current employees' profiles linked to the company page (so their activity shows company affiliation)?
- [ ] **Careers/job visibility**: Are open roles easy to find from the page itself?

**HR note:** Fixing the foundational page elements before posting content matters — a compelling post that links to an empty, stale company page undermines the credibility the content just built.

## Step 2 — Optimizing the Recruiter/Talent Team Profile

**Sample prompt:**

> "Should employer branding content come from the company page or from my personal recruiter profile?"

**Expected skill response:**

- Both — but personal profiles (especially the recruiter's and hiring managers') typically drive more reach and trust than company pages, because LinkedIn's algorithm favors individual accounts and people trust people over brands
- Recruiter profile should be optimized per the headline/photo/About guidance covered in LinkedIn sourcing work — but layered with a content angle:

| Element | Employer-branding-specific guidance |
|---|---|
| Headline | Include what you're building/hiring for, not just your title |
| About | Include your recruiting philosophy and what makes the company worth joining, written personally |
| Featured section | Pin 2-3 of your best-performing posts or the current top-priority job openings |
| Posting cadence | Aim for consistent presence (a few posts per week) rather than sporadic bursts |

## Step 3 — Building a Content Calendar

**Sample prompt:**

> "Build me a 12-week LinkedIn content calendar mixing culture, hiring, and thought leadership content for a B2B SaaS company."

**Expected skill response:**

| Week | Theme | Post idea |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Introduction | "Why we're posting more here" — set expectations, introduce the team |
| 2 | Culture | Behind-the-scenes: a normal Tuesday on the engineering team |
| 3 | Hiring | Open role with real context: why it exists, what success looks like at 90 days |
| 4 | Thought leadership | Engineering lead shares a lesson from a recent technical decision |
| 5 | Employee spotlight | Interview-style post with a recent hire on their first month |
| 6 | Process transparency | What the interview process actually looks like, step by step |
| 7 | Milestone | Product launch, customer milestone, or team growth update |
| 8 | Hiring | Second open role, different framing (team story, not just requirements) |
| 9 | Culture | Honest post about a challenge the team worked through |
| 10 | Thought leadership | Hiring manager perspective on what makes a great candidate for this team |
| 11 | Employee spotlight | Different team/department to show breadth |
| 12 | Recap | Quarter recap — what shipped, who joined, what's next |

**Monthly theme rotation for ongoing cadence:** hiring focus → culture/team stories → thought leadership → process transparency → employee spotlights → honest challenges, repeating with fresh specifics each cycle.

## Step 4 — Writing a Hiring Announcement That Isn't Just a Job Ad

**Sample prompt:**

> "Write a LinkedIn post announcing we're hiring a Senior Product Designer, but make it feel like content, not a job ad."

**Expected skill response:**

```text
We're growing the design team — and this hire matters more than most.

Right now, one designer owns product decisions across three surfaces.
That's not sustainable, and it's not fair to the depth this product
deserves. We're bringing in a Senior Product Designer to partner
directly with our two biggest customer-facing workflows.

What makes this role interesting: you'll be the second design hire
at a company that already has strong customer research infrastructure
but has never had two designers to actually divide and go deep.

If you've ever wanted more ownership than a big-company design org
gives you, but with more support than doing it entirely alone —
this might be it.

Link to the role in comments. Happy to answer questions here too.
```

**Why this works:**

- Opens with a real organizational reason the role exists, not "we're excited to announce"
- Gives a specific, honest picture of current state (one designer, unsustainable) rather than only positive framing
- Speaks directly to the kind of candidate who would resonate with that specific trade-off
- Keeps the actual link out of the post body (in comments) — LinkedIn's algorithm has historically deprioritized posts with outbound links directly in the text

## Step 5 — Culture and Storytelling Content

**Sample prompt:**

> "Write a culture post about a recent team offsite that doesn't feel like generic corporate content."

**Expected skill response:**

```text
We spent two days last week not shipping anything on purpose.

The engineering team's offsite goal wasn't a hackathon or a roadmap
review — it was just: what's actually slowing us down day to day?

Turns out the answer wasn't tooling. It was that we'd quietly stopped
doing design reviews before starting big features, and everyone felt
it but nobody had said it out loud.

We fixed our process. Small thing. But the honest conversation to get
there is the part I actually want to talk about — that kind of
directness doesn't happen by accident, it's something we work at.
```

**HR note:** The strongest culture content shows a real, specific moment (including an imperfect one) rather than a polished, generic "we had a great time!" post. Specificity and honesty are what make culture content believable and shareable.

## Step 6 — Enabling Employee Advocacy

**Sample prompt:**

> "How do we get employees to actually share and engage with our content instead of ignoring it?"

**Expected skill response:**

- Make sharing genuinely easy: give employees a pre-written comment or reshare caption they can adapt, not just a raw link
- Let people add their own voice — don't require verbatim reposting; encourage a personal take alongside a share
- Recognize and amplify when people do participate — a quick "thanks for sharing!" or featuring their comment publicly reinforces the behavior
- Start with a small group of naturally social, engaged employees rather than mandating participation company-wide — organic enthusiasm reads authentically; forced participation often produces stiff, low-engagement posts
- Educate on what's appropriate to share (confirm nothing confidential) so people feel safe participating without needing case-by-case approval

**Checklist:**

- [ ] Identify 5-8 naturally active employees across different teams to start
- [ ] Give them early access/context on posts before they go live
- [ ] Provide adaptable draft captions, not mandatory scripts
- [ ] Publicly acknowledge participation to build a positive feedback loop
- [ ] Avoid making advocacy feel like a job requirement — keep it invitational

## Step 7 — Measuring Engagement and Impact

**Sample prompt:**

> "What should we actually track to know if this employer branding effort is working after one quarter?"

**Expected skill response:**

| Metric | What it shows | How to track |
|---|---|---|
| Follower growth on company page | Awareness reach | LinkedIn native analytics |
| Post engagement rate (not just impressions) | Content quality/resonance | Comments + shares relative to reach, tracked over time |
| Inbound applications tagged "LinkedIn/referral from content" | Direct hiring impact | ATS source field, or ask candidates directly in screening |
| Employee participation rate | Advocacy program health | % of invited employees who engaged per post |
| Response rate on sourcing outreach (secondary effect) | Whether brand awareness is making cold outreach warmer | Compare sourcing response rates before/after the content push |

> ⚠️ **Warning:** Employer branding is a compounding, slow-return channel — don't judge success after 2-3 posts. Evaluate trend lines over the full quarter, and expect the clearest impact to show up as improved response rates on sourcing outreach and unprompted inbound interest, not instant application volume.

## Full Workflow Summary

```text
Audit and fix company page fundamentals
                    ↓
Optimize recruiter/hiring-manager personal profiles
                    ↓
Build a 12-week content calendar across 6 rotating themes
                    ↓
Write hiring, culture, and thought-leadership posts with real specifics
                    ↓
Enable employee advocacy with an invitational, low-friction approach
                    ↓
Track follower growth, engagement rate, and inbound source-of-hire
                    ↓
Review trend lines quarterly, not per-post
```

### Common Mistakes in Employer Branding on LinkedIn

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Posting only from the company page, never personal profiles | Prioritize recruiter/hiring-manager personal posts for reach |
| Generic "we're excited to announce" hiring posts | Give a real, specific reason the role exists |
| Only sharing polished, positive content | Include honest, specific stories — even ones with a real challenge |
| Mandating employee sharing | Make it invitational and easy, not required |
| Judging results after a few posts | Evaluate over a full quarter, focused on trend lines |
| Posting sporadically | Maintain consistent, scheduled cadence even at low volume |
