# Recruiting Passive Candidates

## Context

You are a Technical Recruiter at a scale-up building a long-term pipeline for **Engineering Manager** roles — not for an immediate opening, but because leadership expects to need 2–3 EMs over the next two quarters as the company grows. You've been told to start building relationships now, rather than starting from zero once a req actually opens. You've never done relationship-based sourcing before; all your prior experience is reactive, req-driven outreach.

## Step 1 — Understanding the Passive Candidate Mindset

**Sample prompt:**

> "What's actually different about recruiting someone who isn't job searching at all, versus someone who's just not applying to job boards?"

**Expected skill response:**

- A **fully passive candidate** isn't evaluating any opportunities — they're not comparing offers, not updating their resume, and often not even aware they'd be open to something better until it's presented well
- This means your first interaction cannot lead with "we're hiring" — it has to lead with genuine relevance or interest, because there's no urgency pulling them toward you
- The goal of early-stage passive engagement isn't conversion — it's building enough trust and familiarity that when a real opportunity exists, you're not a cold stranger
- This requires patience: expect weeks to months of light touchpoints before a role-specific conversation makes sense, especially for a role that doesn't exist yet

## Step 2 — Identifying Passive Candidates Worth Investing In

**Sample prompt:**

> "How do I identify Engineering Manager-caliber people worth building a relationship with, before I even have a role open?"

**Expected skill response — signal checklist:**

- [ ] Currently in an IC role showing clear leadership behavior (mentoring mentions, "led a project," technical decision ownership) — potential first-time EM
- [ ] Currently an EM at a company of similar or adjacent stage/size — natural lateral fit
- [ ] Active in engineering leadership content (writes or comments thoughtfully on management topics)
- [ ] Speaks at meetups/conferences on team leadership, technical strategy, or engineering culture
- [ ] Tenure pattern suggests they might be reaching a natural transition point (2.5–4 years at current company, without being so recent it looks unstable)

**HR note:** For a pipeline-building exercise (not an active req), be more selective than usual — you're investing real relationship time, so prioritize genuinely strong signals over broad volume.

## Step 3 — Warming the Relationship Before Any Recruiting Ask

**Sample prompt:**

> "How do I start engaging with someone on LinkedIn without it looking like I'm about to pitch them a job?"

**Expected skill response — a staged approach:**

| Stage | Action | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Follow/connect with a light, honest note (not role-related) | Get on their radar without pressure |
| 2 | Thoughtfully comment on 1–2 of their posts over several weeks | Build visibility and show genuine engagement, not drive-by |
| 3 | Share or reference something relevant they wrote, if genuinely useful | Demonstrates you actually read their content |
| 4 | Light, low-stakes direct message — not a pitch | Open a real conversation |
| 5 | Only after some rapport — mention what you're building, without an ask | Plant awareness for the future |

**Sample connection note (Stage 1):**

> *"Hi [Name] — following your posts on engineering management, especially the piece on scaling a team through a re-org. Not reaching out about anything specific, just wanted to connect."*
> ⚠️ **Warning:** Do not comment or engage only when you're about to make an ask. Candidates can tell the difference between genuine engagement built over time and transactional engagement that starts right before a pitch — the latter reads as insincere and can damage trust rather than build it.

## Step 4 — Commenting Strategy

**Sample prompt:**

> "What makes a good, genuine comment on someone's post versus one that looks like I'm just trying to get noticed?"

**Expected skill response:**

| Weak comment | Strong comment |
|---|---|
| "Great post!" | Adds a specific reaction to a specific point they made, or a related experience |
| "This is so true, love this" | Asks a genuine follow-up question about their approach |
| Generic emoji-only reaction | A short, thoughtful addition that shows you actually read and understood the point |

**Example:**

> Post: *"The hardest part of becoming an EM wasn't the 1:1s, it was learning to be comfortable not being the smartest person in the room technically anymore."*
>
> Weak: "So true! 🙌"
>
> Strong: *"This resonates — curious how long it took before that stopped feeling like a loss and started feeling like a different kind of contribution?"*

## Step 5 — Networking Beyond Direct Messages

**Sample prompt:**

> "Besides LinkedIn DMs, how else can I build relationships with passive EM candidates?"

**Expected skill response:**

- Attend (or have hiring managers attend) local engineering leadership meetups and conferences where target candidates speak or participate — in-person or live-event relationship building is significantly higher-trust than cold digital outreach
- Engage in relevant Slack/Discord communities for engineering leaders, following the same "participate before recruiting" principle used in other community platforms
- Invite candidates to genuinely valuable, non-recruiting content — a panel discussion, an AMA, or a talk your own engineering leaders are hosting — as a low-pressure way to build familiarity
- Ask current employees for warm introductions to former colleagues they respected — internal referral networks are often the highest-trust passive-sourcing channel available

## Step 6 — Personalized Outreach Once Rapport Exists

**Sample prompt:**

> "I've been engaging with someone for about a month now — light comments, one DM exchange. A related EM role is likely to open next quarter. How do I bring it up?"

**Expected skill response:**

> *"Hi [Name] — good chatting a bit ago about [thing from previous exchange]. Wanted to mention something concretely, not as a hard pitch: we're likely opening an Engineering Manager role here in the next quarter or two, and given what you've shared about [specific interest/experience], I think it could be a strong mutual fit down the line. No urgency at all — just wanted it on your radar in case timing ever aligns. Happy to keep the conversation going either way."*

**Why this works:**

- References the actual prior relationship, not a cold reintroduction
- Explicitly removes pressure ("no urgency at all") — appropriate for a role that isn't open yet
- Gives a real timeline expectation, so the candidate isn't left wondering
- Leaves the relationship open regardless of their answer

## Step 7 — Handling Rejection or "Not Interested"

**Sample prompt:**

> "The candidate said they're happy where they are right now. How should I respond, and should I keep engaging with their content?"

**Expected skill response:**

> *"Totally understand, and glad to hear you're in a good spot! I'll leave the door open — feel free to reach out anytime if that changes, and I'll still be around if you ever want to compare notes on engineering leadership stuff."*

- Continue light, genuine engagement (occasional thoughtful comments) if it was authentic in the first place — don't disappear the moment there's no immediate hiring outcome
- Do not follow up again with a role-specific ask for a meaningful period — respect the "not now" as a real answer, not an objection to push past
- Log the relationship status in your CRM/ATS talent pool with context, so a future recruiter (or future you) doesn't restart from zero or duplicate outreach

> **HR note:** A graceful, low-pressure response to "not interested" is itself part of the long-term brand-building — candidates talk to each other, and a recruiter who respects "no" well is more likely to be remembered positively if circumstances change later.

## Step 8 — Building a Long-Term Talent Pipeline

**Sample prompt:**

> "How do I organize and maintain this pipeline so it's actually useful when the role opens, instead of a list of cold contacts by then?"

**Expected skill response — pipeline structure:**

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Name / current role / company | Basic identification |
| Relationship stage | Cold → Warming → Warm → Engaged → Not now → Active candidate |
| Last touchpoint + date | Prevents duplicate or overly-frequent outreach |
| Specific interests/signals noted | Enables genuinely personalized re-engagement later |
| Next planned touchpoint | Keeps the relationship active without over-messaging |

**Checklist for pipeline health:**

- [ ] Review the pipeline monthly, not just when a role opens
- [ ] Re-engage warm contacts with genuine, non-role-related touchpoints periodically
- [ ] Remove or deprioritize contacts who've clearly moved on or gone cold for a long period
- [ ] When a role does open, prioritize outreach order by relationship warmth, not just fit on paper

## Full Workflow Summary

```text
Identify strong passive-candidate signals (leadership behavior, content, tenure)
                    ↓
Begin light, genuine engagement (follow, comment, share) — no ask
                    ↓
Build rapport over weeks via authentic comments and light DMs
                    ↓
Introduce the opportunity only once real rapport exists, with no pressure
                    ↓
Handle "not now" gracefully and keep the relationship open
                    ↓
Maintain an organized, living pipeline with relationship stage and context
                    ↓
Re-engage by warmth when a real role opens, not from a cold list
```

### Common Mistakes When Recruiting Passive Candidates

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Leading with the job pitch on first contact | Build genuine engagement first, over weeks |
| Engaging only right before making an ask | Sustain authentic engagement regardless of immediate need |
| Treating "not interested" as an objection to overcome | Respect it, stay warm, don't push |
| Losing track of relationship history | Maintain a structured, living pipeline with context |
| Expecting fast conversion | Plan for a multi-week to multi-month relationship-building timeline |
