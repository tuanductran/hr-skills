# Building a Talent Intelligence Practice

## Overview

Talent intelligence turns external market signal — compensation benchmarks, competitor hiring patterns, talent market supply and demand — into input for strategic people decisions. It's distinct from internal people analytics, which looks inward at the existing workforce; talent intelligence looks outward at the market the organization competes in for talent, and its value comes from informing decisions before they become urgent, not from producing reports after the fact.

## Core Data Domains

| Domain | What it answers | Common sources |
|---|---|---|
| Compensation benchmarking | Are we competitive on pay for this role and market? | Salary survey providers, published compensation data, recruiter-reported offer data |
| Talent supply and demand | How tight or loose is the market for this capability? | Job posting volume trends, platform-reported talent pool sizes, time-to-fill benchmarks |
| Competitor hiring patterns | Where and how fast are competitors building capability we also need? | Public job postings, LinkedIn-derived headcount trend data, industry reporting |
| Talent movement trends | Where does talent in this function typically come from and go to? | Aggregated career-path data from professional networks |

No single source is reliable enough to act on alone — triangulate at least two independent sources before treating a market signal as solid enough to inform a real decision.

## Compensation Benchmarking

- Benchmark against a defined, relevant peer set — company size, industry, and geography all affect what "competitive" actually means, and a mismatched peer set produces benchmarks that mislead more than they inform
- Refresh benchmarks on a regular cycle, not just when a specific hiring or retention issue prompts a one-off look — compensation benchmarks age faster than most other people data, especially in tight labor markets
- Present benchmark data with its confidence level and source transparency — a benchmark drawn from a small sample size or an unrepresentative peer set should be flagged as such, not presented with false precision

## Reading Talent Supply and Demand Signals

- Rising job posting volume for a specific capability, combined with lengthening average time-to-fill industry-wide, signals a tightening market that should inform both compensation strategy and lever choice (build vs. buy) for that capability
- Distinguish genuine structural tightness (a capability in fundamentally short supply) from temporary or seasonal fluctuation, since the appropriate response differs — structural tightness may justify a build strategy; temporary tightness may just require patience or a compensation adjustment
- Segment supply and demand analysis by geography and seniority level, since aggregate national or industry-wide figures often obscure meaningful local or level-specific variation

## Competitive Hiring Intelligence

- Track competitor hiring patterns for signal about their strategic direction, not just headcount comparison — a competitor rapidly building a specific function often signals a strategic pivot worth understanding
- Use publicly available data (job postings, public headcount trend estimates) rather than any method that would require accessing non-public competitor information — competitive intelligence should stay within clearly ethical and legal bounds
- Present competitive intelligence with appropriate caution about inference — observed hiring patterns are a signal, not confirmed insight into a competitor's actual strategy

## Building Decision-Ready Dashboards

Talent intelligence dashboards should be built around the decisions they inform, not a general data dump:

- Structure the dashboard around specific recurring decisions (compensation review cycles, build-vs-buy calls, location strategy) rather than displaying every available metric
- Update on a cadence that matches how quickly the underlying data actually changes — compensation data might refresh quarterly, while competitive posting data can be monitored more continuously
- Pair quantitative benchmarks with a brief interpretive summary for decision-makers who won't have time to interpret raw data themselves

## Common Pitfalls

- **Acting on a single data source without triangulation**, especially for compensation decisions where sample bias in survey data is common.
- **Using stale benchmarks** in a fast-moving labor market, where a benchmark refreshed a year ago may no longer reflect current conditions.
- **Building dashboards that report data without connecting it to a specific decision**, producing a resource that's referenced rarely because it doesn't map to how decision-makers actually work.
