# Talent Market Intelligence

## Overview

Talent intelligence is the practice of systematically gathering, analyzing, and applying external labor market data to workforce decisions. It answers questions like: Where does the talent we need actually work? What are they being paid? How competitive is our compensation? Which companies are we losing candidates to and why? What skills are emerging that we need to get ahead of?

Organizations that make hiring, compensation, and workforce planning decisions without market intelligence are working with incomplete information. Talent intelligence closes that gap.

## What Talent Intelligence Covers

### Labor market supply and demand

How many people in the external market have the skills you need? Where are they concentrated geographically? Is the supply growing or shrinking? Is demand from competing employers increasing?

Supply and demand data helps TA leaders set realistic time-to-fill expectations, identify where sourcing will be easy versus difficult, and build the business case for compensation adjustments when market tightening creates pressure.

### Compensation benchmarking

What is the market paying for specific roles, skills, and experience levels in your relevant geography? How does your compensation compare to the companies competing for the same talent?

Compensation benchmarking requires care about which peer groups you compare against. Comparing a Vietnamese tech startup's engineering salaries to Google's global median is not useful. The relevant benchmark is the companies your candidates are also interviewing at and the companies your employees are leaving for.

### Competitor talent intelligence

Which companies are growing their headcount in functions that compete with yours for talent? Which are contracting? What is their employer brand positioning? What are their employees saying publicly about compensation and culture?

This intelligence informs employer brand strategy, sourcing prioritization, and workforce planning scenarios.

### Skills and capability trends

Which skills are growing in external demand faster than supply is catching up? Which skills are declining in market value? What emerging skills are not yet in your talent strategy but will be relevant in 12-24 months?

Skills trend intelligence informs both recruiting strategy (which skills to source now before the market tightens) and L&D investment (which skills to build internally before they become expensive to hire).

## Primary Intelligence Sources

### LinkedIn Talent Insights

Provides headcount data, skills distribution, hiring trends, and talent flow analysis for companies and geographies. Most useful for supply/demand analysis and competitor talent mapping. Requires LinkedIn Recruiter license at scale.

### Job posting data

Analyzing job postings — from Burning Glass/Lightcast, Indeed, LinkedIn, and local boards like VietnamWorks — reveals what skills competitors are hiring for, how quickly they are growing specific functions, and what compensation they are signaling publicly.

### Compensation surveys

Structured survey data (Mercer, Radford/Aon, Willis Towers Watson, Anphabe for Vietnam) provides benchmark compensation by role, level, and industry. More reliable than scraping salary ranges from job postings but requires participation or purchase.

### Candidate interview feedback

Systematic debriefs on candidates who declined offers or withdrew from processes are primary intelligence. What competing offers did they receive? What factors drove their decision? This data is perishable — collect it at the point of decision, not weeks later.

### Recruiter market knowledge

Internal recruiters who are active in their talent segment carry significant embedded market intelligence — compensation expectations, where candidates are interviewing, what drives their decisions. Building a process to surface and document this intelligence systematically converts individual knowledge into organizational knowledge.

### LinkedIn and community signals

Public posts, engagement in professional communities, and conference speaking circuits reveal which practitioners are active, respected, and potentially available. Less structured than survey data but surfaces candidates and trends before they appear in formal data.

## Structuring Talent Intelligence for Decision-Making

Intelligence only creates value when it reaches the right decision-maker at the right time in a format they can act on.

### Role-specific briefs

Before opening a critical or difficult search, produce a talent brief: supply and demand summary for the target profile, competitive compensation benchmark, key competing employers, realistic time-to-fill estimate, and recommended sourcing approach. Gives the hiring manager calibrated expectations and the recruiter a sourcing plan.

### Quarterly talent market updates

A brief quarterly update to CHRO and relevant business leaders covering: compensation market movements in critical functions, competitor hiring activity, skills emerging in demand, and any labor market shifts affecting near-term hiring plans. Keeps talent strategy connected to market reality.

### Real-time competitive alerts

Set up monitoring for competitor job postings and talent announcements in your highest-priority segments. Significant competitor hiring surges in your talent pools are a leading indicator of increased competition and longer time-to-fill.

## Common Mistakes in Talent Intelligence

**Benchmarking against the wrong peer set:** Comparing compensation to companies that do not compete for your talent produces misleading data. The relevant peer set is "companies our candidates are also talking to and our employees are leaving for."

**Treating survey data as current:** Compensation surveys are typically 12-18 months old by the time they are published. In fast-moving markets (tech, especially), they understate current market rates. Supplement with real-time signals from offer data and candidate feedback.

**Collecting intelligence without distributing it:** A talent intelligence team that produces reports no one reads has not created value. Build distribution into the program design from the start.

**Conflating LinkedIn headcount with company size:** LinkedIn self-reported headcount data has significant noise. Use directional trends (growing, shrinking, stable) rather than treating specific numbers as precise.
