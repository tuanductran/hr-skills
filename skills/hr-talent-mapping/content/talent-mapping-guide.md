# Talent Mapping

## Overview

Talent mapping is the process of building a structured picture of external talent in a specific function, role category, or geography — before a hiring need is active. It identifies who the relevant talent is, where they work, how they are positioned in the market, and how accessible they might be.

Done well, talent mapping reduces search time when a role opens, improves calibration between TA and hiring managers, and informs workforce planning with concrete market data rather than assumptions.

## When Talent Mapping Is Worth the Investment

Talent mapping requires significant up-front effort. It is most valuable when:

- **Strategic or leadership roles** where the talent pool is small and relationships take months to develop
- **Highly competitive skill segments** where the cost of starting cold is high (engineering leadership, specialized technical roles, niche domain expertise)
- **Planned future needs** that are known in advance — a business entering a new market, a function that will scale significantly in the next 12 months
- **Succession planning** where a shortlist of external candidates is needed to complement internal successors

It is less valuable for high-volume, generalist roles where the talent pool is large and inbound channels are efficient.

## Building a Talent Map

### Step 1: Define the target profile

Before any research, agree with the hiring stakeholder on what you are mapping. A vague brief produces an unusable map.

Minimum definition:
- Role scope and seniority level
- Required skills and experience (must-have versus nice-to-have)
- Target geography or remote eligibility
- Compensation range (informs which companies and seniority levels are realistic)
- Success criteria for the first 12 months in role

### Step 2: Define target organizations

Identify the organizations where your target profile is most likely to work. Sources:

- Your own organization's competitive intelligence on who does similar work
- Industry reports and rankings for the relevant function
- LinkedIn company searches filtered by function and headcount
- Job posting analysis — which organizations are hiring in this space?
- Recruiter network knowledge of where strong practitioners concentrate

Do not limit the map to obvious competitors. In Vietnam's tech market, the best product managers might be at large internet companies, fast-growing startups, regional offices of multinationals, or adjacent industries. Cast the net broadly in the research phase and narrow later.

### Step 3: Research individuals

For each target organization, identify the specific people who fit the profile. Research typically covers:

- Current title and tenure in role
- Prior experience and career trajectory
- Visible skills and domain focus (GitHub, publications, conference talks, LinkedIn activity)
- Indications of potential mobility (recent promotion or lack of one, company contraction or restructuring, tenure at current organization)
- Connection to your existing network or the hiring manager's network

Depth of research scales with the seniority and scarcity of the target. For senior individual contributor and director-level maps, expect 1-3 hours per person for thorough profiling. For broader landscape maps, lighter research on more individuals is appropriate.

### Step 4: Tier and prioritize

Not all mapped candidates are equal. Tier the map before presenting it:

- **Tier 1:** Strong profile match, potential mobility indicators, accessible (warm connection or responsive to cold outreach)
- **Tier 2:** Strong profile match, stable situation, may require longer relationship build
- **Tier 3:** Relevant background, some gaps against the target profile, worth monitoring but not immediate priority

### Step 5: Validate with the hiring manager

Present the map as a working document, not a finished deliverable. Walk through representative Tier 1 profiles with the hiring manager to test whether your calibration matches theirs. Misalignment found in the mapping phase is cheap; misalignment found six months into a search is expensive.

## Maintaining a Talent Map

A talent map has a shelf life. People change roles, companies restructure, and market conditions shift.

### Minimum maintenance cadence

- **Quarterly refresh** for active search maps: update role changes, add new entrants, remove candidates who have taken themselves off the market
- **Biannual refresh** for strategic pipeline maps not attached to an immediate search
- **Event-triggered refresh:** industry announcements, layoffs at target companies, competitor restructuring

### Signals to monitor between refreshes

- LinkedIn activity changes (new role, promoted, open to work)
- Company news at target organizations (funding, growth, contraction, leadership changes)
- Conference presentations and publications indicating growing or evolving expertise
- Recruiter network intelligence on who is in the market

## Integrating Talent Maps with CRM and ATS

A talent map that lives in a spreadsheet in the recruiter's local folder has no organizational value. Integrate map outputs into your CRM:

- Create CRM records for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 contacts with sourcing notes attached
- Tag records with the map date and target role category so they surface in future searches
- Link map contacts to the relevant talent pool segment in the CRM
- When a role opens, the talent map becomes the first activation point rather than a standing-start research exercise

## Reporting Talent Maps to Stakeholders

The hiring manager and CHRO are the primary consumers of talent map output. Calibrate reporting format to audience:

**For hiring managers:** Focus on specific individuals, profile summaries, and realistic assessment of accessibility. They want to understand who is out there and what it will take to hire them.

**For CHRO or workforce planning:** Focus on market landscape findings — total addressable talent pool size, geographic distribution, concentration of talent in specific companies, compensation data, and assessment of search difficulty. This feeds workforce planning assumptions.
