# Building Individual-Level Talent Maps

## Overview

Where a market map identifies where talent sits at a category level, talent mapping goes a step further — tracking specific named individuals, internal and external, against specific current or future roles. It's the practice that turns succession planning and search preparation from a reactive scramble when a role opens into a maintained, proactive bench that can be activated quickly.

## Building the Internal Bench

- Identify roles warranting a maintained bench — typically critical or hard-to-fill positions, and roles with a defined succession need — rather than attempting to maintain named benches for every role in the organization, which isn't sustainable
- For each bench, name specific internal candidates with an honest readiness assessment (ready now, ready in 1–2 years, longer-term potential) rather than a flat list without differentiation
- Cross-reference bench candidates against actual development activity — a candidate marked "ready in 1–2 years" with no active development plan against the specific gap isn't actually progressing toward readiness, regardless of the label

## Building the External Bench

- Maintain named external candidates for critical roles alongside the internal bench, sourced through ongoing market mapping and passive candidate engagement rather than starting external search from scratch when a role opens
- Keep external bench relationships genuinely warm through periodic, low-pressure engagement — a stale list of names collected once and never re-contacted has much less practical value than an actively maintained relationship
- Note realistic mobility signals for external bench candidates (tenure patterns, expressed interest, market conditions) to help prioritize who's actually likely to be reachable when the need arises

## Mapping Against Future Roles, Not Just Current Openings

Talent mapping delivers the most strategic value when it's built proactively against roles the organization anticipates needing to fill, not only against roles that are currently open:

- Connect talent mapping to workforce and succession planning explicitly — the roles prioritized for mapping should trace back to identified future organizational needs
- Revisit the future-role assumptions periodically, since organizational strategy and structure shift, and a bench built against an outdated future-role assumption loses its practical value
- Maintain mapping for both growth roles (net-new positions anticipated from strategy) and succession roles (anticipated departures from retirement, promotion, or attrition)

## Maintaining Map Currency

A talent map that isn't actively maintained degrades quickly — people change roles, companies, and circumstances continuously:

- Set a defined refresh cadence for reviewing and updating bench status, rather than treating the map as a one-time exercise revisited only when a role suddenly opens
- Assign clear ownership for maintaining each bench, since without a named owner, map maintenance is the first thing deprioritized under competing workload
- Retire candidates from the map who are no longer realistic (confirmed uninterested, moved to an unreachable circumstance) rather than letting the map accumulate stale entries that inflate its apparent depth without adding real value

## Confidentiality and Data Sensitivity

Talent maps, especially internal succession benches, involve sensitive individual-level assessment data:

- Restrict access to bench and readiness data to those with a genuine need — broad visibility into succession assessments can create morale issues if candidates or their peers become aware of where they stand
- Be thoughtful about whether and how bench status is communicated to the individuals themselves — practices vary by organizational culture, but an undisclosed assessment that shapes real opportunity decisions without the person's awareness carries its own risk
- Store external bench data (names, contact information, assessment notes) with the same data governance discipline applied to any other candidate data, including consent and retention considerations

## Common Pitfalls

- **Building benches for every role rather than prioritizing genuinely critical or hard-to-fill positions**, which spreads maintenance effort too thin to sustain.
- **Letting external bench relationships go cold** between active search cycles, losing much of the time advantage the practice is meant to create.
- **Treating a readiness label as fixed** rather than connecting it to active, tracked development progress.
