# HR Audit

## Overview

An HR audit systematically reviews HR policies, records, and processes against legal requirements and internal standards to identify compliance gaps before they become costly problems.

## Main topics

### Audit scope and types

Common HR audit types include personnel file/documentation audits, wage-and-hour classification audits, pay equity audits, leave-of-absence handling audits, and broader policy compliance reviews. Scope should be defined clearly before starting.

### Sampling methodology

For large employee populations, statistical sampling (e.g., random sampling stratified by department or location) is more practical than a full review, while still providing defensible coverage.

### Conducting the audit

Audits typically combine document review (personnel files, policies), data analysis (pay distributions, classification data), and process walkthroughs (interviewing HR staff about how a process is actually executed versus how it's documented).

### Findings and risk prioritization

Findings should be categorized by risk level — legal exposure, frequency of occurrence, and severity of potential consequence — so leadership can prioritize remediation effort where it matters most.

### Remediation and follow-up

A corrective action plan assigns clear ownership and deadlines to each finding, followed by a scheduled follow-up review to confirm the gap was actually closed, not just documented.

## Practical guidance

- Define audit scope and objectives in writing before starting.
- Use statistical sampling for large populations rather than attempting full manual review.
- Loop in legal counsel promptly when findings suggest potential violations.
- Track remediation to completion with a follow-up review, not just a findings report.

## Conclusion

A well-run HR audit turns scattered compliance risk into a prioritized, actionable remediation plan — but its value depends on clear scope, sound sampling, and disciplined follow-through on corrective actions.
