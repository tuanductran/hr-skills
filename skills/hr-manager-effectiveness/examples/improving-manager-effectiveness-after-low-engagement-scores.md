# Improving manager effectiveness after low engagement scores

## Context

An HRBP at a 700-person technology company supports 15 first-line engineering managers. The most recent engagement survey shows that three managers have team scores 20 points below the company average on the manager effectiveness index — specifically on "my manager gives me useful feedback," "my manager supports my development," and "I feel comfortable raising concerns with my manager." Two of the three managers also have attrition rates above 30% over the past 12 months. The HRBP needs a structured approach to assess each manager, deliver targeted coaching, and track improvement over 90 days before escalating to performance management.

## Step 1: Diagnose what is driving the low scores

Sample prompt: "Identify the top three manager effectiveness gaps in the engineering function based on engagement survey results and attrition data, and recommend targeted interventions for each."

Expected response:

| Gap | Evidence | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Feedback quality and frequency | Low scores on "useful feedback"; managers describe 1:1s as status updates | Feedback skills workshop + 1:1 structure template |
| Development conversations | Low scores on "supports my development"; engineers report no career conversations | Career conversation guide + quarterly development check-in cadence |
| Psychological safety | Low scores on "comfortable raising concerns"; 2 of 3 managers have high quit rates | Coaching on listening behaviors + team retrospective facilitation |

## Step 2: Build the targeted development plan

Sample prompt: "Write a development plan for a manager who consistently delivers strong business results but receives low upward feedback scores on team development and psychological safety."

Expected response: A 90-day development plan with three phases:

**Phase 1 (Days 1–30):** Awareness and commitment

- HRBP shares aggregated upward feedback results in a coaching conversation, not a performance conversation
- Manager completes a self-assessment against the manager effectiveness competency framework
- Manager and HRBP agree on two to three specific behavioral commitments

**Phase 2 (Days 31–60):** Skill building and practice

- Manager attends feedback skills workshop and runs structured 1:1s using a new template
- HRBP observes one team meeting and provides written coaching notes
- Manager holds individual career conversations with each direct report using a provided framework

**Phase 3 (Days 61–90):** Consolidation and early measurement

- Pulse check with two to three direct reports (informal, not formal survey)
- HRBP check-in with manager's leader on observed behavioral changes
- Manager and HRBP review progress against behavioral commitments and decide whether 90-day plan closes or escalates

## Step 3: Brief the business leader on the manager risk

Sample prompt: "Write talking points for an HRBP conversation with a business leader about a manager who is a people risk — where the business leader is protective of the manager because of their results."

Expected response:

- Open with the business impact, not the HR concern: "I want to share some data that's becoming a retention risk in [manager]'s team."
- Present attrition cost in concrete terms: "We've lost four engineers from this team in 12 months. At [company] average replacement cost, that's approximately $[amount] in recruiting and ramp-up costs."
- Name the pattern specifically: "The engagement data and exit interview themes consistently point to [specific behaviors]. This isn't a one-time issue — it's a pattern."
- Offer a clear path forward: "I'm already working with [manager] on a 90-day development plan. What I need from you is to reinforce these expectations in your 1:1s with them and to hold the line on people accountability in their performance review."
- Be direct about the consequence of inaction: "If the pattern continues after the development plan, we'll need to have a different conversation about fit for this role."

## Workflow summary

Use `hr-manager-effectiveness` to diagnose management quality gaps from existing people data, design targeted development plans that move specific behaviors, and build the business leader partnership needed to hold managers accountable for team health alongside delivery results.
