# Reference Checking

## Overview

Reference checking is one of the most consistently underinvested and poorly executed steps in the hiring process. When done well, it surfaces critical information that interviews cannot — how the candidate actually behaved over time, how they handled adversity, and whether the narrative they presented maps to the experience of people who worked with them. When done poorly — a perfunctory call to confirm employment dates and get a generic positive endorsement — it is a compliance exercise that adds process without adding information.

This guide covers how to design a reference check process that consistently produces decision-relevant information.

## What Reference Checks Can (and Cannot) Tell You

### What references reliably surface

- Whether the candidate's account of their role, responsibilities, and impact is consistent with how their colleagues and managers experienced it
- How the candidate behaved when things were difficult — under pressure, in conflict, when they made mistakes
- Specific competency evidence in a domain the interviewer may not have assessed in depth
- Red flags that did not emerge clearly in interviews — management style issues, reliability, ethics concerns
- Confirmation of specific claims made in the process (led a specific initiative, built a specific team, achieved a specific outcome)

### What references cannot reliably tell you

- Objective performance ranking (reference givers are selected by the candidate and have positive bias)
- Future performance in a different context
- Compensation history (increasingly regulated; confirm local rules before asking)
- Information the reference provider is unwilling to share due to legal caution at their organization

### The selection bias problem

Candidates choose their references. This means reference givers are a positively biased sample by design. The value in reference checking comes from getting beyond the rehearsed positive narrative — through specific questions about failure, conflict, and weakness — not from expecting the references to be a balanced view.

## Designing the Reference Check Process

### Who should be a reference

Strong references are people who have directly managed the candidate, been managed by the candidate, or worked closely with them as a peer in a substantive context. Not: former colleagues the candidate is socially close to but did not work with substantially.

For senior roles, insist on:
- At least one former direct manager
- At least one former direct report (for any leadership role)
- At least one cross-functional peer

### Number of references

Three references minimum for professional roles. Five for senior or executive roles. More references create more signal and reduce the influence of one enthusiastic or one cautious reference.

### Timing in the process

Reference checks should happen after the hiring decision is directionally made and before the written offer is signed. They are a due diligence step, not an assessment step — which means they should not be used to make the initial hire decision, but can and should be a reason to reverse a conditional decision or adjust offer terms.

Do not conduct references in parallel with active assessment unless the process is unusually long — it is disrespectful of the reference's time if the candidate is ultimately not selected.

## Conducting the Reference Call

### Introduction and framing

Open by explaining who you are, the role the candidate is being considered for, and that you would like to have an honest conversation that helps you set the candidate up for success if they join. Framing it as "how can we best support this person" reduces the reference provider's defensive instinct.

Confirm how the provider knew the candidate, in what capacity, and for how long.

### Core questions

**Performance and impact:**
- "How would you describe [candidate]'s overall performance in their role?" (Open, not leading)
- "What were their most significant contributions during your time together?"
- "What would you say were their two or three greatest strengths?"

**Development areas:**
- "What were the areas where you gave them the most development feedback?" (Softer framing than "weaknesses" — produces more honest answers)
- "What is one area where they would benefit from coaching or support in their next role?"

**Specific competency probes (calibrated to the role):**
- For leadership roles: "Tell me about how they developed and managed their team. What did they do well? Where did they struggle?"
- For individual contributors: "Tell me about a project where they had to navigate significant ambiguity or setbacks. How did they handle it?"
- For any role: "Tell me about a time when their work fell short of expectations. What happened and how did they respond?"

**Rehire question — the most important single question:**
- "Would you rehire [candidate]?" / "Would you work with them again?"
- If yes: "Without hesitation, or with some reservations?" (The hedge matters)
- If no or with reservations: "Can you tell me more about what drives that?"

Hesitation or qualification on the rehire question is a significant signal. Press for specifics.

**Closing:**
- "Is there anything you think would be helpful for me to know as we think about how to set [candidate] up for success?"
- "Is there anything about this role or context I have described that you think might be particularly relevant to them — either as a strength or a challenge?"

### Reading between the lines

Reference providers at organizations with strict legal policies may be unable to say much beyond employment confirmation. Watch for:

- Enthusiastic versus measured endorsement (tone matters as much as content)
- Qualifications and hedges on otherwise positive statements ("very smart, just sometimes...") — always follow up
- Pauses before answering specific questions
- Unprompted redirection away from a topic
- Willingness or reluctance to provide specific examples

### Off-list references

For senior roles, consider reaching out to people in your network who have worked with the candidate and are not on the reference list. This bypasses the selection bias problem. Do this only for very senior hires, and with discretion — back-channel references that are disclosed to the candidate by the reference provider create relationship problems.

## Documenting and Using Reference Feedback

Document reference notes promptly after each call. Notes should capture specific examples and quotes, not just general impressions.

If reference feedback surfaces meaningful concerns:
- Do not suppress or downweight it to protect an otherwise good process investment
- Discuss the specific concern with the hiring manager with the evidence from the reference
- Consider whether a focused additional interview or assessment is warranted
- In serious cases (ethics concerns, material discrepancy from what the candidate represented), this is grounds to pause or reverse the hiring decision

If reference feedback is uniformly positive with no substantive development areas identified, that itself is data — either the references are providing a rehearsed response, or you did not push hard enough on development questions. Uniform positive references that produce no useful information are a process failure.
