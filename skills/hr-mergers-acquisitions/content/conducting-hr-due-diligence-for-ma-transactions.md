# Conducting HR Due Diligence for M&A Transactions

## Overview

HR due diligence is often treated as a compliance checkbox late in the deal process — confirming there are no glaring legal liabilities before signing — rather than as substantive input to deal valuation and structuring. Done well, HR due diligence surfaces people risks and culture-fit concerns that materially affect deal value and post-close integration difficulty, and does so early enough to actually influence deal terms rather than only being documented after the decision is essentially made.

## Scope of HR Due Diligence

A thorough HR due diligence review covers several distinct areas, each with different risk implications for the deal:

- **Workforce composition and cost** — headcount, compensation structure, benefits obligations, and any unusual arrangements (retention agreements, change-of-control provisions) that affect deal economics
- **Legal and compliance exposure** — outstanding employment litigation, regulatory compliance gaps, misclassification risk, and pension or benefits liabilities that could transfer with the deal
- **Key talent risk** — identification of individuals whose departure post-close would materially affect the acquired business's value, and assessment of retention risk for each
- **Culture and organizational fit** — working norms, decision-making style, and cultural characteristics that will affect how difficult post-merger integration is likely to be

Culture fit assessment is frequently under-weighted relative to the more quantifiable financial and legal review, despite being one of the strongest predictors of whether the deal's anticipated synergies actually materialize post-close.

## Assessing Culture Fit

- Assess culture through structured interviews and observation, not just documented values statements, which often describe aspirations rather than actual lived organizational norms
- Focus on decision-making style, pace, and risk tolerance specifically — these operational culture dimensions tend to create more integration friction than more visible surface-level differences
- Document culture fit findings with enough specificity to inform integration planning, not just a general "good fit" or "concerning fit" summary that provides no actionable detail for the eventual integration team

## Identifying Key Person Retention Risk

- Identify individuals whose institutional knowledge, client relationships, or leadership is genuinely critical to the acquired business's ongoing value, distinct from a generic "important people" list based on title alone
- Assess actual retention risk for each identified key person — deal-related uncertainty itself is a retention risk factor, independent of any dissatisfaction with the current employer
- Build retention strategy recommendations (retention agreements, early integration communication, role clarity) into the due diligence output, since identifying the risk without a mitigation recommendation limits its usefulness to deal decision-makers

## Workforce Cost and Structural Analysis

- Model the fully loaded cost of the acquired workforce using the same rigor applied to internal workforce economics, since compensation structures, benefits generosity, and any change-of-control obligations can differ meaningfully from acquirer norms and affect post-close cost assumptions
- Identify structural redundancy or gaps that will need to be addressed post-close, distinguishing genuine synergy opportunities from areas where combining the organizations will require net-new investment rather than cost reduction
- Flag any unusual compensation or benefits commitments (executive contracts, non-standard equity arrangements) that will need specific handling in deal structuring or post-close harmonization

## Feeding Findings Into Deal Structuring

HR due diligence findings should influence deal terms, not just be filed as background documentation:

- Translate significant people risks into specific deal structuring recommendations — retention agreement funding, purchase price adjustments for identified liabilities, or specific closing conditions tied to key person retention
- Present findings to deal leadership in terms they can act on directly, framed against deal value and risk rather than in HR-specific terminology that doesn't map clearly to deal decision-making
- Maintain close coordination with legal and finance due diligence workstreams, since people risks frequently intersect with both (employment litigation exposure, compensation liability valuation)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating HR due diligence as a late-stage compliance check** rather than substantive input that could and should influence deal terms.
- **Under-weighting culture fit assessment** relative to more easily quantified financial and legal factors, despite its strong link to post-close integration success.
- **Identifying key person risk without a corresponding retention strategy recommendation**, limiting the finding's practical usefulness to deal decision-makers.
