# HR in Mergers and Acquisitions

## Overview

M&A transactions fail at a remarkably high rate — estimates range from 50-75% failing to deliver their intended value. The most commonly cited reasons are people-related: culture clash, talent attrition, leadership conflict, and integration execution failure. HR's role in M&A is to make these failure modes visible early and design against them.

HR in M&A spans two phases with very different work: due diligence (before the deal closes, assessing people risk) and integration (after the deal closes, combining the organizations). Both require discipline, confidentiality, and a clear mandate from deal leadership.

## HR Due Diligence

### What HR due diligence covers

HR due diligence is an assessment of people-related risks and obligations that affect deal value. The findings inform deal pricing, deal structure, and integration planning.

**Workforce and compensation data:**
- Total headcount by function, location, and employment type (permanent, contractor, part-time)
- Compensation structure: base salary, variable pay, equity plans, benefits
- Outstanding equity obligations (unvested options, RSUs) and how they are treated at deal close
- Compensation anomalies: significant compression, off-cycle increases, or non-standard arrangements
- Payroll processing and compliance history

**Legal and compliance liabilities:**
- Active or pending employment litigation
- Open regulatory investigations or complaints
- Labor union contracts and their terms
- Non-compete and non-solicitation agreements with key employees
- Classification issues (contractors who may be misclassified as employees)
- Immigration status of key employees (work authorization tied to current employer)
- Severance obligations and change-of-control provisions

**Benefits and HR programs:**
- Benefits plan structure and cost
- Unfunded pension liabilities (significant in older organizations)
- Deferred compensation plans
- Change-of-control triggers in benefits plans

**Culture and talent risk:**
- Key person dependencies and flight risk assessment
- Cultural assessment of the target organization
- Management team assessment and retention likelihood
- Employee sentiment data if available

### How to conduct HR due diligence under confidentiality constraints

M&A processes are typically confidential until close. HR due diligence must be conducted without alerting the target organization's employees that a deal is in progress, which limits access to detailed employee-level data until later stages.

**Document review phase:** Access to data room materials — offer letters, benefits plan documents, workforce reports, org charts, compensation surveys, employment agreements, litigation records. This phase does not require employee contact.

**Management interviews phase:** Structured conversations with the target's HR leadership (and sometimes Finance and Legal). These occur under NDA and focus on people program design, compliance posture, and culture.

**Key talent assessment:** Sometimes conducted during the due diligence process for identified retention-critical individuals. Handled carefully to avoid triggering anxiety or premature disclosure.

### Prioritizing due diligence findings

Not all due diligence findings are equal. Classify findings by:

- **Deal-breakers:** Findings that fundamentally change deal economics or create unacceptable risk (e.g., significant undisclosed pension liability, material litigation, systematic wage theft)
- **Price-adjustors:** Findings that affect deal value and should be reflected in pricing (e.g., above-market compensation requiring adjustment, unfunded equity obligations)
- **Integration issues:** Findings that create integration work but do not affect deal economics (e.g., different performance management cycles, incompatible benefit structures)

## Pre-Close Integration Planning

Integration planning begins before deal close, often before public announcement. HR integration planning covers:

### Day 1 readiness

Day 1 is the first day of combined operations after deal close. Employees of the acquired organization will have fundamental questions on Day 1 that must be answered:

- Who do I report to?
- Does anything change about my pay, benefits, or employment terms?
- Will I still have a job?
- Who is my HR contact?

If these questions cannot be answered on Day 1, confidence in the integration erodes immediately.

### Retention planning

Identify key talent in the acquired organization that must be retained for deal value to be realized. Build retention packages before close, not after talent starts leaving.

Retention risk is highest immediately after announcement and at the 6-month and 12-month marks. Design retention mechanisms with appropriate vesting schedules that incentivize staying through critical milestones.

### Cultural assessment and integration design

The cultural distance between acquirer and target is one of the strongest predictors of integration success or failure. Assess both cultures before close and design an integration approach that accounts for that distance.

Integration is not assimilation. Acquirers that impose their culture wholesale on an acquired organization typically lose the talent that made the acquisition valuable. Design an approach that preserves what matters in the acquired culture while building toward a coherent combined culture.
