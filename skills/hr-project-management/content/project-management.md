# HR Project Management

## Overview

HR project management applies standard project discipline — charters, timelines, risk management, stakeholder communication — to HR-specific initiatives such as system implementations, policy rollouts, and organizational change programs.

## Main topics

### Project charters and scope

A charter defines the project's objective, sponsor, scope boundaries, success criteria, and high-level timeline, serving as the reference point when questions or scope changes arise later.

### Stakeholder mapping

HR projects typically touch multiple functions (IT, legal, finance, business unit leaders) beyond HR itself. Mapping stakeholders by influence and interest level shapes the communication plan and governance structure.

### Governance structures

Larger HR initiatives often need a steering committee for major decisions and a working group for day-to-day execution, with clear escalation paths between the two.

### Risk management

A risk register tracks identified risks, their likelihood/impact, and mitigation owners, reviewed regularly rather than only at project kickoff.

### Change control

A formal change control process evaluates and approves scope changes mid-project, preventing uncontrolled scope creep that derails timelines and budgets.

## Practical guidance

- Write the charter and get sponsor sign-off before significant work begins.
- Map stakeholders and build the communication plan before, not during, a crisis.
- Review the risk register on a recurring cadence, not just at the start.
- Close every project with a lessons-learned session to improve the next one.

## Conclusion

Applying disciplined project management to HR initiatives reduces the chaos common in cross-functional programs and improves the odds that HR projects land on time, on budget, and with stakeholder buy-in.
