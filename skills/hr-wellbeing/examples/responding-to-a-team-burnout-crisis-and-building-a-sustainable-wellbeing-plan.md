# Responding to a Team Burnout Crisis and Building a Sustainable Wellbeing Plan

## Context

You are the HR Business Partner supporting a 140-person customer success and engineering organization that has been operating under sustained pressure for the past two quarters following a major product launch and a competitor-driven sales push. In the last month, two engineers have taken unplanned medical leave citing exhaustion, a customer success team lead broke down in a 1:1 describing feeling unable to keep up, and the team's engagement pulse score dropped 12 points quarter over quarter. Leadership has asked you to address the immediate situation, design a wellbeing response, and make sure this does not quietly repeat next quarter — starting essentially from a string of individual incidents with no existing wellbeing program in place.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "We've had two engineers go on medical leave for exhaustion, a team lead broke down describing feeling overwhelmed, and engagement just dropped 12 points. There's no wellbeing program in place. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — multiple acute incidents already occurring and no existing program — start with two priority areas:

**Immediate crisis stabilization (the people already affected):**

- Check in directly and privately with the two engineers on leave to understand their support needs and ensure they are not being pressured to return before they are ready
- Have a direct, supportive conversation with the team lead who broke down, separate from any performance conversation, focused entirely on their wellbeing and workload
- Confirm whether either situation requires immediate manager coaching to prevent further harm to the individuals involved

**Root-cause workload diagnosis (the systemic signal):**

- Treat the 12-point engagement drop and the two leave cases as connected signals, not isolated incidents
- Identify whether workload across the team has structurally increased since the product launch, or whether the pressure is concentrated in specific roles
- Determine how long this pressure has been sustained, since a two-quarter pattern suggests a structural issue rather than a temporary spike

The leave cases and the engagement drop are not separate problems — they are likely the same underlying workload issue surfacing through different signals. You need a concrete read on workload before designing any program response.

> **Avoid:** Do not respond with a generic wellness initiative (a wellness week, a one-time survey) without first addressing the acute cases directly and understanding whether this is a workload-structural issue. A team already showing burnout symptoms will read a surface-level response as dismissive of what they are actually experiencing.

## Step 2 — Modeling Intervention Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few options for how we respond to this burnout pattern so I can compare trade-offs before proposing anything to leadership."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Immediate workload reduction (pause non-critical work, redistribute load):**

```txt
Approach: Pause lower-priority initiatives for 4-6 weeks, redistribute
          critical work across a wider pool of contributors, bring in
          temporary contractor support for the most overloaded roles
Risk profile: Lowest immediate burnout risk, but may delay other
              business commitments and requires leadership buy-in to
              reprioritize publicly
Trade-off: Most directly addresses root cause, but requires leadership
           to visibly de-prioritize work shortly after a major product
           push, which may be a hard sell
```

**Option B — Support layer addition (EAP promotion, manager training, no workload change):**

```txt
Approach: Increase visibility and access to existing EAP resources,
          train managers on recognizing burnout signals, encourage
          use of existing flexible work options
Risk profile: Moderate — addresses support and awareness but does not
              change the underlying workload driving the symptoms
Trade-off: Easier and faster to implement, but risks looking like a
           surface-level response if workload itself is not addressed,
           especially given the team lead's direct disclosure
```

**Option C — Phased combined response (immediate workload relief for the most affected team, paired with org-wide wellbeing program design):**

```txt
Approach: Immediate workload reduction and contractor support for the
          engineering and customer success teams showing acute signals,
          paired with a parallel effort to design a broader wellbeing
          program (manager training, EAP redesign, ongoing risk
          monitoring) for rollout over the following quarter
Risk profile: Lowest combined risk — addresses the acute situation
              directly while building durable infrastructure to catch
              the next instance earlier
Trade-off: Requires more coordination and a slightly longer timeline
           for the full program, but avoids both under-responding to
           the crisis and over-indexing on a reactive, one-time fix
```

**Benchmark context:**

Engagement score declines of 5 to 10 points are typical during sustained high-pressure periods, but a 12-point drop combined with two unplanned medical leaves in one month signals risk beyond the typical range — this pattern calls for a workload-level response, not a support-awareness response alone.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the team lead's direct disclosure and two leave cases in the same month, Option B's support-only approach risks being read as dismissive of a workload problem employees are already naming directly, but a phased version of Option C — immediate workload relief for the most affected teams this week, paired with a structured wellbeing program built over the following quarter — addresses the acute risk while building the infrastructure to prevent recurrence.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Underlying Risk Pattern

**Sample prompt:**

> "Two engineers are on leave, a team lead disclosed feeling overwhelmed, and engagement dropped sharply. I need to understand what's really driving this before I finalize a response."

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming the cause, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Workload and staffing concerns:**

- Has actual headcount kept pace with the work added since the product launch and competitor-driven push, or has the same team absorbed increased scope?
- Are specific roles or teams carrying disproportionate load compared to the rest of the organization?

**2. Manager awareness and response concerns:**

- Did managers have the training or framework to recognize early burnout signals before they escalated to medical leave?
- Was the team lead's disclosure the first signal raised, or were there earlier informal signals that were not escalated to HR?

**3. Timing and sustained-pressure gaps:**

- How long has this elevated pressure been sustained, and was there ever a planned recovery period built in after the initial launch push?
- Has the pressure created a normalized expectation of overtime that employees feel unable to push back on?

**4. Individual case specifics:**

- For the two employees on leave specifically, is this workload-driven, or are there other contributing personal factors that should shape a respectful, individualized return-to-work approach?
- Has a direct, private conversation happened with the team lead to understand whether their disclosure reflects a personal capacity concern, a team-wide pattern, or both?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present this as a sign individual employees lack resilience. Bring a clear picture of which factors are structural and addressable through workload and staffing decisions versus which require direct, individualized support for the specific people affected.

## Step 4 — Building the Wellbeing Response Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build a wellbeing response plan for this situation, since leadership wants this addressed but we have no existing program to build from."

**Skill response — phased response plan:**

| Response Stage | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Acute case support | Two employees on leave, one team lead disclosed distress | Individual check-ins and tailored support plans for each; confirm leave is not rushed |
| Workload diagnosis | Not yet assessed | Audit current workload distribution across engineering and customer success teams |
| Manager training | Not yet built | Train managers on early burnout signal recognition and appropriate response |
| Workload relief | Not yet implemented | Pause non-critical initiatives and add temporary support for the most affected teams |
| Ongoing monitoring | Not yet planned | Build a 90-day risk monitoring cadence using workload and engagement signals (see Step 7) |

**Stakeholder-specific communication sequence:**

1. The two employees on leave and the team lead — individual, private conversations first, focused on their specific needs and free of any performance framing
2. Team managers — direct briefing on the workload relief plan and burnout recognition training, equipping them to support their teams day to day
3. Broader affected teams — a short, honest update acknowledging the pressure of the last two quarters and the concrete steps being taken, without singling out individuals

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequence matters: the affected individuals should never learn about a broader team response before having their own situation addressed directly — that sequencing itself would compound the harm
- Build in explicit space for managers to ask questions about how to support their teams, rather than treating the plan as a one-directional policy rollout

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the two leave cases and the team lead's disclosure as three separate, unrelated incidents. A two-quarter sustained pressure period with multiple acute signals in the same month is very likely one underlying workload pattern surfacing in different ways.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this response plan to the leadership team. How do I frame it given the product launch and sales push were both leadership priorities?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership responds to a credible plan that protects business momentum while directly addressing real risk, rather than appearing to relitigate prior priorities. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Name the pattern with data, not anecdotes alone:**

"Over the past two quarters, sustained pressure from the product launch and competitor response has shown up as two unplanned medical leaves and a 12-point engagement drop in the same month. This is a workload pattern, not three isolated incidents."

**Part 2 — Recommend immediate and structural action together:**

"I'm recommending immediate workload relief for engineering and customer success — pausing non-critical initiatives for four to six weeks and adding temporary contractor support — paired with manager training on early burnout recognition and a 90-day monitoring cadence so we catch the next instance earlier."

**Part 3 — Present the plan and ask for what you need:**

"I need your support publicly de-prioritizing the paused initiatives so managers and teams understand this is a deliberate decision, not a quiet deprioritization that creates confusion. I also need budget approval for temporary contractor support and manager training over the next quarter."

> **Key principle:** Leadership responds well to a plan that protects the business outcomes they care about while showing the wellbeing risk is being actively managed — frame workload relief as protecting the team's ability to sustain the next push, not as a concession against business priorities.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Wellbeing and Manager Communication

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft communication for the affected employees, the managers, and the broader team update. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without it sounding generic or clinical?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the situation, the support already offered to affected individuals, and the tone appropriate for a sensitive wellbeing topic before drafting anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am an HR Business Partner responding to a burnout pattern in a
140-person engineering and customer success organization following
two quarters of sustained pressure from a product launch and
competitor-driven sales push.

Context:
- Two engineers have taken unplanned medical leave citing exhaustion
- A customer success team lead disclosed feeling unable to keep up
- Team engagement dropped 12 points quarter over quarter
- We are pausing non-critical initiatives for 4-6 weeks, adding
  temporary contractor support, and training managers on early
  burnout recognition

Please:
1. Draft talking points for a manager training session on recognizing
   and appropriately responding to early burnout signals
2. Draft a short, honest team update acknowledging the pressure of
   the last two quarters and the concrete steps being taken, without
   identifying the specific affected individuals
3. Suggest 3-4 likely questions team members will ask and draft
   honest, direct answers to each
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with stabilization planning:**

```txt
Based on this plan, draft a 90-day wellbeing monitoring framework
with specific workload and engagement questions to check at the
30, 60, and 90-day marks to confirm the risk has actually decreased.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting clear, empathetic talking points for sensitive conversations quickly
- Anticipating likely employee questions and concerns about workload changes
- Generating a reusable wellbeing monitoring framework

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Reading the room and adjusting tone in the actual individual conversations with affected employees
- Clinical judgment on what level of support a specific situation requires
- The trust that comes from a leader being personally present and responsive during a sensitive disclosure

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Wellbeing Monitoring Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Once the immediate response is in place, I don't want this to quietly repeat next quarter once the pressure builds again. How do I build a monitoring plan?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the monitoring plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Has the workload relief (paused initiatives, contractor support) actually reduced hours and after-hours activity for the affected teams, or has work quietly crept back to prior levels?
- Are the two employees who took leave receiving an appropriately paced, individualized return-to-work transition rather than a default full return?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Has the engagement score begun recovering toward baseline, or are early signals suggesting the underlying workload issue has not actually been resolved?
- Are managers consistently using the burnout recognition training in practice, or has it become a one-time session with no follow-through?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would the affected teams describe their current workload as sustainable, or as a temporary reprieve before pressure returns?
- What structural staffing or process adjustments, if any, are needed based on what has been learned this quarter to prevent the pattern from repeating?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Workload and overtime signal review | HRBP + Team Managers | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Return-to-work check-in (affected employees) | HRBP | Weekly for first 30 days, then biweekly |
| Engagement pulse re-check | HR Operations | 60, 90 days |
| Manager training reinforcement | Wellbeing Program Manager | 90 days |

> **Common finding at fast-growing companies:** Wellbeing risk frequently appears resolved immediately after a workload relief period, because the temporary pause itself creates visible relief. The real test is whether workload stays sustainable once paused initiatives resume — without a deliberate monitoring cadence, pressure quietly returns to prior levels and the same pattern repeats the following quarter.

## Full Employee Wellbeing Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (leave case, disclosure, engagement decline, sustained pressure)
                    ↓
Model multiple intervention options and compare trade-offs
                    ↓
Diagnose the workload, manager, and individual factors driving the risk
                    ↓
Build a phased wellbeing response plan alongside immediate case support
                    ↓
Present the recommendation to leadership with risk and business-protection framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft tailored manager and employee communication
                    ↓
Design a 90-day wellbeing monitoring plan with defined checkpoints
                    ↓
Report on recovery and sustainability to leadership at each checkpoint
```

### Common Employee Wellbeing Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Responding to burnout signals with a generic wellness event | Pair any response with a direct workload diagnosis before designing the intervention |
| Addressing only the acute leave cases without examining the structural pattern | Diagnose whether multiple incidents reflect one underlying workload or staffing issue |
| Treating a burnout disclosure as a personal resilience problem | Diagnose structural workload factors alongside individual support needs before responding |
| Building manager training only after a crisis has already occurred | Train managers on early signal recognition alongside program design, not after an incident |
| Assuming workload relief alone resolves the underlying issue | Pair temporary relief with a monitoring cadence to confirm the change is sustained |
| Treating return-to-work as complete once the employee is back at their desk | Build a phased, individualized return-to-work plan with defined check-ins |
| Skipping a stabilization plan after the immediate crisis fades | Build a 90-day monitoring cadence with named owners before declaring the response complete |
