# Offer Management

## Overview

An offer is not the end of the recruiting process — it is one of the highest-risk stages. Offer acceptance rates below 85% are a signal of problems in compensation competitiveness, candidate experience, or process design. How an offer is constructed, communicated, and negotiated determines whether the investment made in the search converts to a hire.

## Offer Construction

### Components of a complete offer

A well-constructed offer clearly communicates every component of the total package:

**Base salary:** Annual gross salary. In Vietnam, clarify whether quoted as gross (before tax) or net (after tax) — this is a common source of confusion and should be explicit.

**Variable pay:** Target bonus percentage or amount, plan structure, typical payout range based on performance, and when the first eligible period begins.

**Equity:** For startups and tech companies — option grant size, vesting schedule (typically 4-year with 1-year cliff), strike price (for options), and current 409A or share valuation context. Most candidates cannot evaluate equity without these details.

**Benefits:** Health insurance coverage and premium structure, mandatory social insurance (BHXH/BHYT/BHTN), any supplemental coverage. Differentiate between mandatory statutory benefits and company-provided benefits.

**Leave:** Annual leave days, sick leave policy, any additional leave provisions (paternity/maternity beyond statutory, mental health days, etc.)

**Other material terms:** Remote or hybrid arrangement, start date, probation period, and any performance review timeline relevant to compensation.

**Non-standard conditions:** Any non-compete, non-solicitation, IP assignment, or background check requirements that affect the offer.

### Setting the offer level

The offer level should be set based on three inputs:

**Market competitiveness:** Where does this total package sit relative to the candidate's alternatives? Offers that come in at or below the market median for a competitive candidate create risk. Offers that are clearly above market for the profile reduce negotiation and improve close rate.

**Internal equity:** How does this package compare to peers in the same role and level inside the organization? Offers that create internal equity issues generate immediate problems even if the hire accepts.

**Budget authorization:** What has been approved for this role? If market rate is above the approved range, surface this to the hiring manager and finance before extending an offer — not after the candidate declines.

### The verbal offer conversation

Do not send an offer letter without a verbal offer call first. The verbal call serves several purposes:

- Confirms the candidate is still interested before generating offer documentation
- Surfaces competing offers or concerns the candidate has not previously raised
- Allows you to frame the offer with context before the candidate reads numbers in isolation
- Creates an opportunity to ask the candidate to articulate how they are thinking about the decision

The verbal call should be conducted by the recruiter, or for senior roles, the recruiter with the hiring manager. Walk through every component clearly and confirm the candidate has no questions before ending the call.

## Offer Negotiation

### Responding to counteroffers

When a candidate comes back with a counter, the first step is to understand what is driving it — is this about base salary, total compensation, equity, a competing offer, or something non-monetary like title or start date?

A counter that is purely about base salary may have room to close with an equity adjustment or a sign-on bonus. A counter driven by a competing offer at a specific number requires a different response than a counter driven by the candidate's personal financial target.

**Questions to ask before negotiating:**

- "Can you help me understand what's driving this? Is there a specific number you need to reach, or are you comparing this to another offer?"
- "If we could get to X, would you be ready to move forward?" (Test the close before going back for approval)
- "What else beyond compensation is important for you in making this decision?"

### What to negotiate vs. what not to negotiate

Negotiable: base salary within the approved range, sign-on bonus (which is one-time and has no ongoing cost), equity (within the approved grant), start date, title (with appropriate approvals), remote or hybrid arrangement.

Less negotiable: role scope, reporting structure, team composition, probation terms (which may have legal implications), benefits not offered to peers in the same role.

Not negotiable: anything requiring policy exceptions that HR is not authorized to grant. Be clear about these rather than creating false hope.

### Managing competing offers

When a candidate discloses a competing offer, treat it as information, not as a negotiation tactic to dismiss. Ask:

- What is the other offer, and what is driving the candidate's consideration of it? (The other offer may be objectively better in some dimension — knowing this is better than losing the hire after extending the best package you can)
- What would make this role the clear choice for the candidate? (Sometimes the answer is non-monetary and actionable)
- What is the timeline the candidate is working with? (Understanding their actual deadline helps prioritize)

Do not pressure candidates to decline competing offers before your process is complete. Candidates who are pressured and then receive a worse experience remember it.

## Offer Acceptance and Declination

### Handling acceptance

After verbal acceptance: send the written offer promptly (within 24 hours). Confirm the start date, onboarding logistics, and any pending steps (background check, document collection). Maintain contact between acceptance and start date — accepted candidates who feel ignored during the gap sometimes renege.

### Handling declination

When a candidate declines, ask for honest feedback. What drove the decision? This is primary intelligence on your compensation competitiveness, candidate experience, and employer brand positioning.

Thank the candidate clearly and genuinely. They may be a candidate for a future role, a referral source, or someone who will form impressions of your organization in their professional network. How you handle a decline is part of the candidate experience.

### Post-acceptance attrition (reneges)

Candidates who accept and then renege before their start date are a meaningful operational problem. Common causes:

- Counteroffer from current employer accepted after initial acceptance
- Competing offer received after acceptance
- Concerns about the role or organization that were not fully resolved
- Personal circumstances

Maintain contact between acceptance and start date to reduce renege risk. Early warning signs: reduced responsiveness to onboarding communications, requests to delay the start date. Address these proactively rather than waiting for a renege notification.
