# Managing the Offer Lifecycle

## Overview

The offer stage is where months of recruiting effort either converts or is lost, often over process friction that has nothing to do with the candidate's actual interest — a slow approval chain, an unclear negotiation posture, or a poorly constructed package that undersells the total offer. Treating offer management as a distinct discipline, with its own workflow and decision framework, meaningfully reduces both acceptance-rate loss and inconsistent internal practice across recruiters.

## Constructing the Offer Package

An offer package should be assembled from a consistent internal framework, not built ad hoc per candidate:

- **Anchor to a compensation band**, not the candidate's stated expectations alone — bands keep offers internally consistent and defensible, while still leaving room for legitimate variation based on experience and scope
- **Present total compensation clearly**, not just base salary — when equity, bonus, and benefits are a meaningful part of the package, candidates who don't see them itemized often underweight them relative to a competing offer that's presented more transparently
- **Build in a small amount of negotiation room deliberately**, rather than presenting a number with no flexibility and then conceding immediately when challenged — inconsistent flexibility across candidates for the same role creates internal equity problems that surface later

## Designing the Approval Workflow

Approval delay is one of the most common, avoidable causes of lost offers — a strong candidate with a competing offer and a deadline will often take the faster-moving option even if it's not their first choice.

- Map the current approval chain and count actual steps and average time per step — most organizations underestimate how many approvals a given offer requires until it's mapped explicitly
- Pre-approve compensation bands and standard package structures so most offers don't require case-by-case senior approval — reserve escalation for genuine exceptions (above-band offers, non-standard terms)
- Set an internal SLA for approval turnaround and track adherence, the same way recruiting SLAs are tracked elsewhere in the funnel

## Negotiation Strategy

- Prepare recruiters and hiring managers with a shared understanding of where genuine flexibility exists (band range, start date, remote arrangement) versus where it doesn't, so candidates don't receive inconsistent signals from different people in the process
- Distinguish negotiation driven by a genuine competing offer from negotiation driven by general anchoring — asking clarifying questions about the specifics of a competing offer, respectfully, helps calibrate an appropriate response rather than matching every claimed offer at face value
- Avoid multiple rounds of small concessions, which tend to erode trust and invite further negotiation rather than closing it — a single, well-considered counter is usually more effective than an extended back-and-forth

## Handling Counteroffers

When a candidate's current employer counters after they've accepted or are about to accept:

- Treat a counteroffer acceptance as a signal worth taking seriously rather than personally — it often reflects unresolved factors (compensation, career path, unaddressed concerns) that existed before the candidate started looking, and a counteroffer only surfaces them
- Where policy allows, have an honest conversation with the candidate about what's actually driving the counteroffer decision, rather than immediately withdrawing or escalating — a data point for future retention strategy either way
- Track counteroffer-related withdrawal rates over time; a rising rate in a specific function or level often signals a market or internal compensation gap worth addressing proactively rather than one-candidate-at-a-time

## Offer Status Tracking

Managing offer status across multiple concurrent roles requires the same operational discipline as pipeline tracking earlier in the funnel:

- Track each offer's stage explicitly (drafted, approved, extended, under negotiation, accepted, declined) rather than relying on informal recruiter memory, especially at volume
- Set expiration dates on outstanding offers and follow up proactively rather than letting them lapse silently, which creates ambiguity for both the candidate and internal planning
- Analyze decline reasons in aggregate on a recurring basis — a pattern of declines tied to compensation, timeline, or a specific stage in the process is a signal worth acting on, not just noting per-candidate

## Common Pitfalls

- **Letting approval delay silently cost strong candidates** without ever measuring how much time the process actually takes end to end.
- **Inconsistent negotiation flexibility across recruiters or hiring managers**, which creates internal equity problems that surface later when employees compare notes.
- **Treating every counteroffer acceptance as a one-off** rather than looking for a pattern that points to a broader retention or compensation issue.
