# Writing Job Descriptions That Attract the Right Candidates

## Overview

A job description does two jobs at once, and most drafts optimize for only one: it's a marketing document meant to attract qualified candidates, and it's a reference document that sets expectations and, in many jurisdictions, carries legal weight around essential functions and accommodation. Descriptions that read like internal HR documents fail at attraction; descriptions that read like pure marketing copy fail at setting accurate expectations and can create legal exposure if they misstate the role.

## Structuring for Both Audiences

- **Lead with what the person will actually do**, not a list of company superlatives — candidates scanning a posting decide within seconds whether to keep reading, and generic opening paragraphs ("We are a fast-growing, innovative company...") waste that window.
- **Separate "must-have" from "nice-to-have" requirements explicitly**, and keep the must-have list genuinely short. Research consistently shows that inflated requirement lists disproportionately discourage qualified candidates from underrepresented groups from applying, while doing little to filter out unqualified ones who apply regardless.
- **State compensation range** wherever legally required or practically useful — omitting it in markets where candidates expect it is an increasingly common source of early drop-off, independent of the actual offer being competitive.

## Writing Inclusive, Bias-Aware Language

- Avoid gendered or culturally coded language that research has linked to lower application rates from women and other underrepresented groups — words like "dominant," "ninja," or "rockstar" skew applicant pools even when unintentional.
- Write requirements as outcomes, not credentials, where the underlying capability is what actually matters — "able to design and run structured interviews" rather than "5+ years of interviewing experience," which excludes qualified candidates whose experience doesn't map neatly to a tenure requirement.
- Avoid stacking degree and years-of-experience requirements without a clear reason tied to the role's actual demands — this is one of the most common and easily fixed sources of an unnecessarily narrow applicant pool.

## Setting Accurate Expectations

Overselling a role produces early attrition once the reality doesn't match the pitch — a candidate who accepts based on an inflated description is a retention risk within the first year, not a recruiting success. Balance genuine enthusiasm about the opportunity with:

- An honest description of the challenges and ambiguity in the role, especially for early-stage or high-growth environments where the job will look different in six months than it does at hire
- Accurate reporting structure and scope — don't imply broader authority or a larger team than actually exists
- Realistic growth trajectory language — avoid implying a promotion timeline the organization can't actually commit to

## Structure and Formatting

A well-structured JD generally follows this order, each section short enough to scan:

1. **Role summary** — two to three sentences on what the role does and why it exists
2. **Key responsibilities** — five to eight outcome-oriented bullets, not a duty checklist
3. **Requirements** — split into must-have and nice-to-have, each kept short
4. **What success looks like** — a brief note on what the role achieves in its first 6–12 months, which helps candidates self-select more accurately than a responsibilities list alone
5. **Compensation and benefits** — as specific as policy and jurisdiction allow
6. **Company and team context** — kept brief; candidates who want more will look at the careers site

## Testing and Iterating

Treat job descriptions as living documents, not one-time artifacts:

- A/B test title variations and opening paragraphs where volume justifies it — small wording changes can measurably shift application volume and quality
- Review conversion data by funnel stage after each posting cycle — a JD that generates high application volume but low qualified-candidate conversion needs revision, not just a larger funnel
- Retire and rewrite JDs that haven't been updated in over a year — role scope drifts, and an outdated JD sets expectations that no longer match the actual job

## Common Pitfalls

- **Writing the JD as an internal requisition document and posting it externally unchanged.** Internal approval language, org-chart references, and internal acronyms confuse external candidates.
- **Requirement inflation** — adding "just in case" requirements that no incumbent in the role actually meets, which narrows the pool without improving hire quality.
- **Neglecting mobile readability.** A large share of candidates browse postings on mobile; long unbroken paragraphs and dense formatting cost applications regardless of content quality.
