# Writing Effective Job Descriptions

## Overview

A job description is a candidate's first substantive experience of the role and the organization. Most job descriptions are written to protect the organization from scope creep or legal challenge — which produces a document that is comprehensive, defensive, and unpersuasive. The candidates most likely to be persuaded by a defensive job description are the candidates most desperate for any offer.

Effective job descriptions are written for the reader — a specific candidate profile who has options, is assessing the role against alternatives, and needs to understand quickly whether this is worth their time.

## The Purpose of a Job Description

A job description serves two purposes simultaneously, which is where most fail:

**Attract:** Convince a qualified candidate who is not currently looking that this role is worth serious consideration. This requires clear communication of why the role is compelling — the problem it solves, the impact it has, what makes it interesting.

**Screen:** Give a candidate enough information about requirements and context to self-assess fit. A job description that successfully screens produces more qualified applicants, not fewer total applicants.

These purposes conflict when description writers prioritize comprehensiveness over persuasion, or legal coverage over clarity. The best job descriptions hold both — honest about what is hard, clear about what is required, and compelling about what makes the work worth doing.

## Anatomy of an Effective Job Description

### Role title

Use the title the candidate will recognize in the market, not the internal grade or the idiosyncratic title your organization uses. "Senior Software Engineer" is findable and meaningful. "Software Development Engineer III" is an internal artifact.

If your internal title differs from the market title, use the market title in the job posting and note the internal title if needed.

### Opportunity summary (2-4 sentences)

The opening should answer: what is this role for, and why does it matter? Write it for the candidate who is skimming 20 job descriptions and deciding which to read carefully.

**Weak:** "We are a fast-growing company looking for a talented Senior Software Engineer to join our dynamic team."

**Strong:** "Our payments infrastructure team owns the systems that process 2M+ transactions daily. We are growing the team to support our Southeast Asia expansion and are looking for a Senior Software Engineer who wants to work on high-throughput, low-latency distributed systems with real business consequences."

The strong version tells the candidate what the engineering challenge is, what the scale is, and what growth context makes the role timely. It screens in people who want to work on distributed systems at scale and screens out people who do not.

### Role overview (1 paragraph)

What does this person do? What is the scope — what are they responsible for, what decisions do they make, what do they build or manage? This is not a list of tasks — it is a coherent description of the role's purpose and scope.

### Responsibilities

List the core responsibilities — what this person will actually spend most of their time doing. 5-8 bullets is optimal. More than 10 indicates the role is either too broad or the description is listing tasks rather than responsibilities.

Write each bullet as a responsibility (owns X, leads Y, builds Z) not a task list (attend meetings, write documents, coordinate with stakeholders). The difference:

**Task:** Participates in technical design reviews.
**Responsibility:** Owns technical design decisions for the authentication subsystem, including design review facilitation and final architecture sign-off.

### Qualifications

Split into must-have and nice-to-have.

**Must-have** should be genuine requirements — things the candidate needs to do the job from day one. If you can onboard someone without a qualification, it is not a must-have.

Common must-have inflation: requiring years of experience as a proxy for skill level, requiring specific tool experience when equivalent tools would transfer, requiring a degree when the role does not require academic knowledge.

**Nice-to-have** signals what would accelerate onboarding or differentiate candidates. It is not a second must-have list. If you find yourself requiring 8 out of 10 nice-to-haves, revise the must-have list.

### Team and working environment

Who will this person work with? What does the team look like? What is the working model (remote, hybrid, in-office, Vietnam-specific location)? What is the engineering culture or team dynamic?

This section answers the question candidates ask themselves: "Do I want to work with these people in this environment?"

### Compensation and benefits (optional but recommended)

Publishing a compensation range increases application rate from qualified candidates, reduces time spent on candidates outside the range, and signals organizational transparency. Where local market norms support it, include a range.

In Vietnam's market, compensation transparency is growing. Candidates increasingly share compensation information with each other; not publishing a range does not prevent candidates from knowing market rates.

## Inclusive Language

Job descriptions that use gendered language, unnecessarily exclusive requirements, or culturally specific idioms reduce the diversity of the applicant pool.

### Common inclusive language issues

**Gendered language:** "He will oversee..." "She should have..." Use "you" or "the successful candidate."

**Jargon and culture-specific idioms:** "Rock star engineer," "ninja developer," "crushing it" — these are culturally specific and often read as exclusionary to candidates who are not already insiders to that culture.

**Unnecessary requirements:** Years of experience cutoffs, specific degree requirements for roles where demonstrated skill is more relevant, and credential requirements that correlate with socioeconomic access rather than job performance all reduce diversity unnecessarily.

**Aggressive performance language:** "We only hire the best," "this role is not for the faint-hearted," "we move fast and break things" — this language signals a culture that may not be welcoming to candidates from underrepresented groups and those with different cultural backgrounds.

Tools like Textio and Gender Decoder help identify language patterns that affect applicant pool diversity. Manual review for the issues above is useful even without tools.
