# Designing a Build-Buy-Borrow-Bot Talent Strategy

## Overview

Most organizations default to "buy" (external hiring) as the answer to nearly every talent gap, largely because it's the most familiar lever, not because it's consistently the best one. A deliberate talent supply chain strategy treats build, buy, borrow, and bot as genuine alternatives, each with a different cost, speed, and risk profile, and matches the lever to the specific gap rather than reaching for the same tool every time.

## The Four Levers

| Lever | Definition | Best fit |
|---|---|---|
| Build | Develop the capability internally through training and stretch assignments | Gaps with lead time available and a strong existing talent base to develop from |
| Buy | Hire externally for the capability | Urgent gaps or genuinely new capabilities the organization has never had |
| Borrow | Access the capability through contractors, freelancers, or partnerships | Temporary or fluctuating need, or capability needed briefly for a specific project |
| Bot | Automate the task the capability was needed for | Well-defined, repeatable tasks where automation is mature enough to substitute for headcount |

Evaluate each significant talent gap against all four levers explicitly before defaulting to buy — the discipline of forcing the comparison, even briefly, regularly surfaces a faster or cheaper option than external hiring.

## Evaluating Build vs. Buy

- **Time to proficiency** — building takes longer to reach full proficiency than hiring an already-qualified external candidate, but the gap narrows considerably for capabilities where the organization has strong existing adjacent skill to build from
- **Cost comparison** — buying often has a higher upfront cost (compensation premium for external, urgent hires) while building has a higher ongoing investment (training cost, temporary productivity loss during development) — compare total cost over a realistic time horizon, not just the initial price
- **Retention risk** — internally built talent, once developed, carries a flight risk if the organization doesn't create a clear path to use the new capability; factor this into the build decision, not just the development cost

## Talent Pool Management

Talent pools — groups of internal or external candidates pre-identified against future need — are what make "build" and "buy" strategies proactive rather than reactive:

- Maintain internal talent pools tied explicitly to identified succession or capability needs, not a generic high-potential list disconnected from specific future roles
- Maintain external talent pools (through a talent CRM or market mapping) for capabilities the organization anticipates needing but doesn't have an immediate opening for, so buy decisions can move faster when the need materializes
- Review pool composition against evolving business strategy regularly — a pool built against last year's priorities may no longer match what the organization actually needs

## Forecasting Talent Availability

Both build and buy strategies depend on an accurate read of talent availability, internally and externally:

- For internal availability, use skills intelligence and succession data to identify realistic internal candidates, factoring in genuine readiness timelines rather than optimistic assumptions
- For external availability, use market mapping and talent intelligence data to assess how competitive the market is for a given capability — a tight external market strengthens the case for building or borrowing instead
- Revisit availability assumptions periodically, since both internal readiness and external market conditions shift meaningfully over time

## Building the Supply Chain Strategy Document

A talent supply chain strategy should be a living reference, not a one-time exercise:

1. Map current and projected capability gaps against the workforce plan
2. For each significant gap, document the recommended lever (or combination) and the rationale
3. Name the specific actions required to execute the chosen lever — a development plan for build, a sourcing plan for buy, a contractor relationship for borrow, a process redesign for bot
4. Set a review cadence to revisit lever choices as gaps close or business priorities shift

## Common Pitfalls

- **Defaulting to buy without evaluating the alternatives**, missing faster or cheaper options available through build, borrow, or bot.
- **Building talent pools with no connection to a specific identified future need**, producing a generic list that doesn't actually accelerate a real hiring or succession decision.
- **Treating the supply chain strategy as static** once documented, rather than revisiting it as gaps close and business priorities evolve.
