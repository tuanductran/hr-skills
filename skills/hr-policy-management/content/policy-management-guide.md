# HR Policy Management

## Overview

Most organizations have too many HR policies, written too long, updated too infrequently, and stored where employees cannot find them. The result is policies that exist on paper but do not govern actual behavior — which is legally worse than having no policy, because it creates documented standards the organization demonstrably fails to meet.

Effective policy management treats the policy library as a product: it has owners, it is maintained, it is tested against real usage, and it is retired when it no longer serves its purpose.

## Principles of Effective HR Policy

### Write for the reader, not for legal protection

Policies written to protect the organization from liability are typically written in a way that employees cannot understand and managers will not use. A policy that employees cannot understand is not a policy — it is a document.

Write in plain language. Use the second person ("you"). Explain what to do in the situation an employee is actually in, not the exception the lawyers are worried about. Legal review should catch what plain language misses, not replace plain language.

### Shorter is more likely to be read

A policy that covers the standard case clearly in one page is more effective than an exhaustive policy covering every edge case across six pages. Employees read short documents. They do not read long ones.

Where edge cases need coverage, use a separate FAQ document linked from the policy, not additional policy length.

### Policies should state what to do, not only what not to do

Prohibition-only policies tell employees what is not permitted but leave them without guidance on what is. A social media policy that is only a list of prohibited behaviors does not tell employees what good social media use looks like. Balance prohibitions with clear guidance on appropriate behavior.

### Every policy needs an owner

Without a named owner, policies are never updated, never tested, and quietly become obsolete. The owner is responsible for the accuracy of the policy and the cycle of its review.

## Policy Lifecycle Management

### Drafting

Before writing a new policy, confirm a policy is the right solution. Policies are appropriate when:

- A consistent standard needs to apply across the organization
- There are compliance or legal requirements to document
- A recurring situation requires clear guidance at scale

Policies are not appropriate for:
- Addressing a single incident or individual behavior
- Situations better handled by manager judgment
- Areas where the organization is not prepared to enforce the standard

Draft the policy from the perspective of the most common reader in the most common situation. Test the draft on a sample of the target audience before publishing.

### Review cycle

Every policy should have a defined review date. Standard intervals:

- **Annual review:** Policies in areas with active regulatory change (compensation, leave, data privacy, anti-discrimination)
- **Biennial review:** Stable operational policies (expense reimbursement, remote work, dress code)
- **Event-triggered review:** Any policy in an area where a legal, regulatory, or organizational change has occurred

Reviews should check: Is this policy still accurate? Is it still legally compliant? Is the language still clear? Are employees actually following it? Are managers enforcing it consistently?

### Retirement

Retired policies should be explicitly archived, not simply removed from the active library. Employees and managers who acted on a former policy may need to reference it. Document the retirement date and the reason.

## The Employee Handbook

The employee handbook is not the policy library. It is the curated, employee-facing collection of the policies most relevant to day-to-day employment — written in a tone and format appropriate for an employee's first read.

### What belongs in the handbook

- Employment relationship basics (at-will status where applicable, hours, classification)
- Anti-harassment and anti-discrimination policies
- Leave and time off policies
- Code of conduct and ethics
- Confidentiality and data protection basics
- How to raise concerns or complaints

### What does not belong in the handbook

- Detailed HR operational procedures (belong in HR's internal SOPs)
- Benefit plan documents (link out; these are governed by plan documents)
- Manager-specific guidance (belongs in a manager guide, not the employee handbook)
- Every policy the organization has ever written (creates a document employees will never read)

### Acknowledgment and versioning

Employees should acknowledge the handbook at onboarding and when material changes are made. Maintain version history with change dates. This is relevant if a policy question arises about which version of a policy applied at a particular time.

## Policy Communication and Rollout

A policy that is not communicated is not enforceable. Rollout planning is part of policy management, not an afterthought.

### Communication planning checklist

- Who needs to know about this policy and by when?
- What channel is most likely to reach them effectively?
- Does the policy require manager communication to teams, or can it go directly to employees?
- Is training or a Q&A session needed to support understanding?
- Where will the policy live, and how will employees find it?
- How will you confirm receipt or acknowledgment?

### Communicating policy changes

Be direct about what has changed and why. Employees do not read policy comparisons — tell them in plain language what is different and what it means for them. Where changes are operationally significant, give adequate notice before enforcement.

## Policy Governance Structure

Larger organizations need a governance structure that prevents policy proliferation and ensures consistency.

### Policy council or review body

A cross-functional group (HR, Legal, Compliance, Communications) that reviews proposed new policies before publication, ensures policies do not conflict with each other, and approves retirement of outdated policies.

### Policy inventory

A single source of truth listing every active policy: name, owner, last review date, next review date, and the employee population it applies to. Without an inventory, policies accumulate silently and become unmanageable.

### Tiered authority

Define who can create and publish policies at each scope:

- Enterprise-wide policies: CHRO and Legal approval required
- Business unit or regional policies: HR business partner and local legal
- Team-level operating procedures: manager discretion, no formal policy status
