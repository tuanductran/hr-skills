# Writing Policies That Hold Up

## Overview

Most HR policies fail in one of two ways: they're so vague they can't guide a real decision, or so rigid they break the first time a manager encounters an edge case the drafter didn't anticipate. A policy that holds up under pressure is specific enough to be applied consistently, but leaves room for documented judgment where the underlying principle, not the letter of the rule, is what matters.

Policies are also legal artifacts. A poorly worded policy can create obligations the organization didn't intend (an "at will unless documented otherwise" handbook that reads like a contract) or expose it to disparate treatment claims when enforcement doesn't match what's written.

## The Anatomy of a Defensible Policy

Every policy should answer five questions explicitly. If a manager or employee has to guess the answer to any of these, the policy needs revision.

1. **Who does this apply to?** Full-time, part-time, contractors, specific locations or entities — scope ambiguity is one of the most common sources of inconsistent enforcement.
2. **What is the rule?** Stated as a clear standard, not a description of intent. "Managers must approve overtime in advance" is a rule. "We value work-life balance" is not.
3. **What are the exceptions, and who approves them?** Every policy has edge cases. Naming the approval authority up front prevents ad hoc exceptions that create precedent no one intended.
4. **What happens if the policy isn't followed?** Consequences should be proportionate and consistent with how similar violations are handled elsewhere in the handbook.
5. **Who owns this policy going forward?** A named owner (role, not just "HR") responsible for periodic review and updates when law or practice changes.

## Plain Language Without Losing Precision

Legal accuracy and readability aren't in tension as often as drafters assume. Two techniques help:

- **Replace passive, hedged phrasing with direct statements.** "It is expected that employees will generally aim to arrive on time" becomes "Employees are expected to arrive on time." The second version is both clearer and easier to enforce consistently.
- **Define terms that carry legal weight before using them.** If a policy references "immediate family" for bereavement leave, define it in the policy rather than assuming shared understanding — this is a frequent source of inconsistent application across managers.

Where a policy must reflect a specific legal requirement (leave entitlements, accommodation processes, wage and hour rules), keep the legally load-bearing language precise even if it reads more formally than the surrounding text, and pair it with a plain-language summary above it.

## Structuring the Employee Handbook

A handbook is a collection of policies, not a single document, and should be organized so employees can find what they need without reading the whole thing:

- **Group by employee lifecycle stage or theme** (employment basics, conduct standards, leave and time off, compensation and benefits, technology and safety) rather than alphabetically or by department of origin.
- **Keep a single source of truth per policy.** If overtime rules appear in both the handbook and a separate compensation policy document, they will eventually drift out of sync. Cross-reference instead of duplicating.
- **Version and date every policy individually**, not just the handbook as a whole — this matters when a dispute turns on which version of a policy was in effect on a given date.

## The Update and Communication Cycle

A policy that's updated but never communicated might as well not have changed, from an enforcement-consistency standpoint. Every substantive policy update should include:

- **A trigger for review:** new legislation, a court ruling in a relevant jurisdiction, an internal incident that exposed a gap, or a scheduled annual review — don't rely solely on ad hoc triggers.
- **A communication plan proportionate to the change.** A typo fix needs no announcement. A change to leave eligibility needs a dedicated communication, not just a buried handbook update.
- **A record of acknowledgment** for material changes, especially those affecting pay, discipline, or termination — this is often the first thing requested in an employment dispute.
- **A sunset check for the old version** — outdated policy documents left accessible on an intranet are a recurring source of confusion and, in disputes, evidence of inconsistent practice.

## Common Drafting Pitfalls

- **Writing aspirational statements into binding policy.** "We strive to accommodate all requests" reads as a promise a court may later hold the organization to. Separate culture statements from enforceable rules.
- **Copying a template policy without adapting it to actual practice.** A policy the organization doesn't actually follow is worse than no policy — it becomes evidence of a gap between stated and actual practice.
- **Letting policy and practice diverge silently.** If managers have informally stopped enforcing a rule, update the policy or re-establish enforcement — don't leave an unenforced rule on the books indefinitely.
- **Omitting a severability or policy-precedence clause** in the handbook, which can leave the whole document vulnerable if one provision is found unenforceable in a given jurisdiction.

## Multi-Jurisdiction Considerations

Organizations operating across states or countries should maintain a base policy plus jurisdiction-specific addenda rather than one policy that tries to satisfy every jurisdiction's strictest requirement by default — this can create obligations in locations where they aren't legally required and are hard to walk back later. Flag every policy with jurisdiction dependencies (leave, background checks, non-competes, pay transparency) for legal review before rollout to a new location.
