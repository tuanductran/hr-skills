# Game Development Hiring

## Overview

Game development hiring spans multiple distinct disciplines — engineering, design, art, and production — each requiring different evaluation approaches, often centered on portfolio review and shipped-work verification more than in typical software hiring.

## Main topics

### Discipline types

Gameplay engineers build core game mechanics and systems; technical artists bridge art and engineering (shaders, pipelines, optimization); game designers define mechanics, levels, and player experience; producers manage schedule, scope, and cross-discipline coordination.

### Portfolio and shipped-work review

For design and art roles especially, reviewing actual playable portfolio pieces or shipped title contributions provides far more signal than a resume or generic interview questions.

### Craft vs. collaboration

Game development is highly collaborative; rubrics should separately evaluate craft/creative quality and the candidate's ability to iterate and work well within a team, since these don't always correlate.

### Industry culture considerations

The games industry has a well-documented history of crunch culture; transparent job postings about actual work-life balance expectations help attract candidates for the right reasons and reduce early attrition.

## Practical guidance

- Prioritize reviewing real playable or shipped portfolio work over resume claims alone.
- Score craft and collaboration separately in interview rubrics.
- Ask candidates to discuss iteration and revision, not just present a polished final product.
- Be transparent in postings about crunch and overtime expectations to set realistic expectations.

## Conclusion

Effective game development hiring blends discipline-specific technical and creative evaluation with genuine portfolio review, while being honest about the culture and expectations candidates will actually experience.
