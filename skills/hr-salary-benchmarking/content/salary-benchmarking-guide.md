# Salary Benchmarking and Market Compensation Analysis

## Overview

Salary benchmarking is the process of comparing your compensation to what the market pays for equivalent roles, skills, and locations. It answers: "Are we paying competitively? Are we overpaying some roles and underpaying others? How much should we adjust compensation to stay competitive?"

Without benchmarking, compensation decisions are made in a vacuum — a hiring manager says "I think this role should pay $100K" without knowing whether that's 20th percentile or 80th percentile in the market. Benchmarking grounds compensation strategy in market reality.

## Why Benchmarking Matters

Benchmarking affects:

**Recruitment competitiveness:** If you are underpaying the market, candidates reject your offers for higher-paying competitors. You get lower-quality candidates, or you have to accept longer time-to-fill. If you are significantly overpaying, you attract external interest easily but create internal morale problems when people find out they are getting paid more than they need to be.

**Retention:** Employees know what the market pays (via Glassdoor, salary websites, conversations with peers). If they discover they are significantly underpaid, they leave. Competitive compensation is not the primary reason people stay, but non-competitive compensation will cause attrition.

**Pay equity:** Benchmarking reveals whether your compensation structure has consistent biases (e.g., certain demographic groups consistently paid less for the same role). It doesn't solve pay equity problems, but it surfaces them.

**Cost management:** Benchmarking allows you to optimize spend — don't underpay and create turnover, but don't overpay on roles where the market has abundant supply.

## Benchmarking Data Sources

### Survey data

**Mercer, Radford/Aon, Willis Towers Watson, PayScale:** Structured surveys where companies self-report compensation data anonymized by role, industry, location, company size. These are the gold standard for accuracy but are expensive (subscriptions $10K-$100K+/year).

**Industry associations:** Many industries have association surveys (Silicon Valley HR Association, Financial Women's Association, etc.). Lower cost than big vendors; more relevant to specific industries.

For Vietnam specifically:
- **Anphabe:** Conducts annual salary surveys for Vietnamese tech and HR markets; reasonable pricing
- **TopCV:** Job posting data and salary trends; free but less structured than formal surveys

### Job posting data

**Burning Glass (acquired by Lightcast), Indeed, LinkedIn:** Extract salary information from job postings. Scale advantage — covers hundreds of thousands of postings. Disadvantage: salary ranges in postings are often wider/less reliable than survey data.

Useful for: spotting rapid market shifts, regional variation, identifying which competitors are actively hiring.

### Custom market surveys

For roles or markets not well-covered by published surveys, organizations sometimes run custom surveys — directly asking peer companies for compensation data (usually under NDA).

Requires careful legal review to avoid antitrust issues (competitors colluding to set wages is illegal; honest sharing of compensation data for benchmarking is legal — the line is subtle).

## Benchmarking Methodology

### Defining the benchmark job

Before comparing salaries, define what role you are benchmarking. A "Software Engineer" is too broad — there are too many variations.

Better definition: "Backend Software Engineer, 3-5 years of experience, reporting to Engineering Manager, primary technologies Java/Spring, based in San Francisco."

This specificity matters. A junior backend engineer in Kansas City will make significantly less than a senior backend engineer in San Francisco.

### Finding matches

Use job titles, experience level, function, and location to find comparable roles in survey data. Often there is not a perfect match — you have to find roles that are close and adjust mentally for differences.

### Percentile positioning

Survey data is typically reported as percentiles: 25th percentile (bottom quartile), 50th (median), 75th (top quartile), sometimes 10th and 90th.

Your positioning choice reflects strategy:
- **25th percentile:** Cost-minimizing; recruit people willing to work below market; expect higher turnover
- **50th percentile (median):** Competitive; align with market
- **75th percentile:** Premium positioning; attract top talent; higher cost

Many organizations target 50th-75th percentile — median to above-median — because it's competitive without being unnecessarily expensive.

### Adjusting for differences

Surveys provide data for standard profiles. You adjust for:

**Geography:** Cost of living varies enormously. A role paying $120K in San Francisco might pay $90K in Austin or $70K in a smaller city. Use cost-of-living indices or location adjustments from survey vendors.

**Company size:** Large companies typically pay more than small ones for the same role. Surveys break out by company size.

**Industry:** Finance and tech pay more than nonprofits or government for comparable roles. Surveys break out by industry.

**Experience level:** Surveys break out by years of experience; use the closest match.

## Building a Competitive Compensation Strategy

Benchmarking is not just about picking a percentile and locking in that number. It's about building a strategy.

### Market-based pay bands

Most organizations build salary bands (minimum to maximum) for each level/role, set to market data:

Example: Senior Software Engineer in San Francisco
- Minimum: 50th percentile ($150K)
- Midpoint: 60th percentile ($170K)
- Maximum: 75th percentile ($190K)

These bands:
- Create consistency (everyone at this level in this location has a band)
- Provide room for growth (employee can get raises within the band)
- Set a ceiling (no one gets paid above maximum unless they are exceptional and you deliberately break the band)

### Adjustments for specialty/scarcity

Some skills or roles command premiums above the published benchmark:

- Roles in very tight labor markets (ML engineers, specialized healthcare roles): may need to pay 90th percentile to attract
- Emerging skills: often paid above market because supply has not caught up to demand
- Highly specialized domain expertise: command premiums

Conversely, some roles are less competitive:

- Roles where you have a brand advantage (everyone wants to work at your organization, so you can pay less)
- Legacy technologies where talent supply is abundant
- Geographic areas where your organization is a major employer

### Transparency and communication

An emerging trend: sharing salary bands with candidates and employees. Advantages:
- Removes salary negotiation mystery; candidates know the range
- Demonstrates fairness (no hidden compensation decisions)
- Reduces recruiting friction

Risks:
- If you are underpaying, transparency reveals it
- Internal equity problems surface immediately
- Requires discipline to actually stick to the bands

Organizations moving toward transparency (e.g., Gitlab, Buffer) report net benefit — attracts candidates seeking fairness, and forces alignment between stated and actual practices.

## Common Benchmarking Mistakes

**Using outdated survey data:** Compensation moves fast, especially in tech and healthcare. Survey data that is 12 months old is stale. Best practice: benchmark annually.

**Comparing yourself to the wrong peer group:** If you benchmark your manufacturing company against tech companies, you'll overpay and destroy your margin. Know your actual competitive set — the companies you're hiring from and losing people to.

**Setting compensation to attract a specific person:** "We'll pay above market to get this person" can create problems — internal equity issues, resentment from people already at that level. Better: assess whether your market position is off, and adjust across the board.

**Ignoring total compensation:** Base salary is only part of total compensation. Benefits, equity, flexibility, job security all affect competitiveness. Sometimes you can compensate for lower base with better benefits or other factors. But pretending benefits offset a 20% salary gap is a mistake.

**Benchmarking in isolation:** Compensation is connected to budget, business performance, and talent availability. A benchmarking exercise that results in "we should be paying 10% more" needs to connect to budget realities and prioritization.
