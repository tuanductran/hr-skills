# Time and Attendance

## Overview

Time and attendance management covers how organizations record hours worked, calculate pay-affecting time (overtime, breaks, leave), and enforce attendance expectations. It sits at the intersection of HR policy, payroll accuracy, and labor law compliance.

## Main topics

### Exempt vs. non-exempt tracking

Non-exempt employees generally must have all hours worked recorded precisely, since pay and overtime depend on it. Exempt employees are typically tracked for leave/attendance purposes only, not for overtime calculation, though local law varies.

### Overtime rules

Overtime thresholds (daily, weekly, or both) and multipliers differ by jurisdiction. Systems must correctly identify the applicable workweek, handle blended rates for employees with multiple pay rates, and route pre-approval where required by policy.

### Rounding and time-clock rules

Rounding punches to the nearest 5, 6, or 15 minutes is common but must be neutral over time (not systematically favoring the employer) to avoid wage-and-hour claims.

### Attendance policies

Attendance policies typically define what counts as an occurrence (late, absence, early departure), grace periods, escalation steps, and how points or occurrences expire over time (rolling window vs. calendar year).

### Leave and accrual tracking

Leave balances (vacation, sick, parental) accrue based on tenure, hours worked, or a fixed schedule, with rules for carryover caps, payout on termination, and negative balance handling.

## Practical guidance

- Configure systems to flag missed punches automatically rather than relying on manual review.
- Build in manager visibility into pending timesheet approvals to avoid payroll delays.
- Keep a clear audit trail for every manual edit: who made it, when, and why.
- Review attendance policy against local labor law annually, since thresholds and required leave types change.

## Conclusion

Reliable time and attendance management protects both the organization and employees: it ensures accurate pay, supports fair enforcement of attendance expectations, and reduces legal exposure from wage-and-hour errors.
