# Workplace Accessibility and Accommodation

## Overview

Workplace accessibility and accommodation is the practice of ensuring that employees with disabilities, chronic conditions, or other needs can perform their jobs effectively — through reasonable adjustments to the work environment, tools, or processes. It is both a legal obligation in most jurisdictions and an organizational equity imperative.

Most organizations manage accommodation reactively — waiting for requests and then determining what to provide. Leading organizations are increasingly moving toward proactive accessibility design: building inclusive workplaces from the start, so that fewer individual accommodations are needed.

## Legal Framework

### Vietnam context

Vietnam's Law on Persons with Disabilities (2010) and the Labor Code establish obligations for employers regarding disabled employees. Key provisions include:
- Anti-discrimination protections in hiring and employment
- Obligations to facilitate work for employees with disabilities
- Requirements for accessible physical workplaces (with specific obligations for organizations above defined headcount thresholds)

Employers should work with legal counsel to understand current obligations, as implementation guidance and enforcement evolve.

### International context (for multinational organizations)

Organizations with operations in multiple countries must navigate different accessibility frameworks:
- **US:** Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) — interactive process requirement, undue hardship standard
- **EU:** EU Disability Strategy, individual member state implementation
- **UK:** Equality Act 2010 — duty to make reasonable adjustments

The interactive process (US ADA) model is a useful framework even where not legally required: when an employee requests accommodation, the employer and employee engage in a good-faith dialogue to identify an effective accommodation.

## Handling Accommodation Requests

### Process design

A clear, documented accommodation process reduces legal risk, improves employee experience, and creates consistency:

1. **Request received:** Employee initiates request (written preferred but not required)
2. **Acknowledgment:** HR acknowledges receipt and explains the process and timeline
3. **Medical documentation:** Request medical documentation from a healthcare provider confirming the condition and functional limitations (not a diagnosis — only what the employee cannot do)
4. **Interactive discussion:** HR and employee discuss the employee's limitations, job requirements, and potential accommodations
5. **Decision:** HR determines whether the requested accommodation is reasonable, whether it creates undue hardship, and if so what alternative accommodations might be effective
6. **Implementation:** Accommodation is put in place with any necessary coordination (IT, facilities, manager)
7. **Follow-up:** Scheduled check-in to assess whether the accommodation is effective

### Documentation requirements

Document every step of the accommodation process. Documentation serves two purposes: ensuring the process was followed correctly, and creating a record in the event of a future legal challenge.

Retain: the initial request, medical documentation received, records of the interactive process discussion, the accommodation decision and reasoning, and any follow-up communications.

### Common accommodation types

**Physical workspace:** Ergonomic furniture, accessible workstation height, quiet workspace, proximity to accessible facilities.

**Schedule flexibility:** Adjusted start and end times, modified break schedules, intermittent leave for medical appointments, reduced hours arrangements.

**Technology:** Screen readers, speech-to-text software, enlarged display settings, captioning for video content.

**Remote work:** For employees whose conditions are better managed from home. Remote work as accommodation requires the same analysis as any other accommodation — is it effective, is it feasible, does it create undue hardship?

**Role modification:** Temporarily or permanently reassigning marginal (non-essential) functions of the role to other employees.

**Leave:** Additional leave beyond standard policy where needed for medical treatment or recovery.

## Proactive Accessibility Design

Waiting for accommodation requests is a minimum standard. Proactive accessibility design reduces the frequency and complexity of individual accommodations by designing inclusive workplaces from the start.

### Digital accessibility

HR systems, intranets, job application portals, and communication platforms should meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.1 AA) standards. This ensures employees with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive disabilities can use the tools they need without requiring individual accommodation for every system.

When procuring new HR technology, include accessibility compliance as a vendor evaluation criterion. Ask vendors for their VPAT (Voluntary Product Accessibility Template) and test with assistive technology before purchase.

### Physical workspace design

Universal design principles in office environments: adjustable-height desks available to all employees (not only those with documented needs), captioning on video screens in meeting rooms, accessible wayfinding, quiet zones for employees who need reduced sensory stimulation.

### Inclusive hiring practices

Job descriptions that include only genuine requirements (not credential inflation that excludes disabled candidates). Interview processes that offer alternative formats where reasonable. Assessment tools evaluated for accessibility and bias against disabled candidates. Consistent offer of accommodation at application stage.

## Building a Culture of Accessibility

Accommodation processes and physical accessibility are necessary but not sufficient. A culture where employees feel they can disclose a disability or request accommodation without stigma or career risk is equally important.

Signs of a culture problem: employees who avoid requesting accommodation because they fear being seen as less capable, managers who express frustration at accommodation requests, disclosure rates significantly below population disability prevalence rates (suggesting employees are not disclosing).

Leadership modeling matters: senior leaders who speak openly about their own disabilities or mental health conditions meaningfully reduce stigma for the broader workforce.
