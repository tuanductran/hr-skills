# Recruitment Marketing

## Overview

Recruitment marketing is the application of marketing discipline to talent attraction — using employer brand, targeted content, paid and organic channels, and conversion optimization to build candidate awareness and intent before a job is posted.

The difference between recruitment marketing and traditional job advertising is the same as the difference between brand marketing and classified ads. Job ads reach active candidates who are already looking. Recruitment marketing reaches the passive majority who are not actively looking but might be convinced.

## Employer Brand as the Foundation

Employer brand is what people believe it is like to work at your organization — before they have ever worked there. It is formed by what your employees say publicly, what candidates experience in your process, what journalists write about you, and what you communicate intentionally.

Most organizations have an employer brand. Few actively manage it.

### Defining your employer value proposition (EVP)

The EVP is the articulation of what you offer employees that competitors do not — or do not as well. It is the answer to: why should someone who has options choose to work here?

An EVP is not a slogan. It is a set of differentiated, credible claims about the employment experience. Differentiated means it is not true of all companies in your segment. Credible means current employees would recognize it as accurate.

Common EVP dimensions: work that matters, growth and learning, quality of colleagues, leadership and culture, compensation and benefits, flexibility and autonomy, mission and impact.

Your EVP should lead with the dimensions where you are genuinely differentiated and stop there. Claiming everything is a claim of nothing.

### EVP validation

Before publishing an EVP, test it:

- Do current employees recognize these claims as accurate?
- Do recent joiners say these factors influenced their decision to join?
- Do departed employees refute the claims? (Exit interview data)
- Do candidates who declined offers cite different factors than the EVP leads with?

Misalignment between EVP claims and employee experience erodes employer brand faster than having no EVP.

## Channel Strategy

Different channels reach different candidate segments. Match channel investment to where your target profiles actually spend time and what their job search behavior looks like.

### Organic channels (owned)

**Career site:** Your highest-intent traffic. Candidates on your career site are already interested. Poor career site UX, slow load times, or an impersonal experience wastes that intent. The career site should communicate the EVP, surface relevant content about work and culture, and have a functional job search and application flow.

**LinkedIn Company Page:** The second career site for most professional candidates. Employee posts and culture content get significant organic reach. Consistency of posting matters more than individual post performance.

**Employee advocacy:** When employees share their authentic work experiences publicly, it reaches audiences and carries credibility that branded content cannot match. Build a framework that makes it easy for willing employees to share, without scripting them.

**Glassdoor management:** You cannot control what current or former employees write on Glassdoor, but you can respond to reviews, flag inaccurate content, and ensure your company profile accurately represents current conditions. Candidates read Glassdoor. Ignoring it does not make it go away.

### Paid channels

**LinkedIn job ads and InMail:** Highest quality for professional roles; highest cost. Target by function, seniority, skills, and company. InMail for passive candidate outreach; sponsored jobs for active candidates.

**VietnamWorks, TopCV, CareerBuilder (Vietnam market):** Primary active-candidate job boards for Vietnam. VietnamWorks has the highest professional candidate volume; TopCV skews tech. Essential for roles where active candidate pools are adequate.

**Facebook and Instagram:** Effective for reaching candidates in consumer, retail, hospitality, and frontline roles. Less effective for senior professional and technical roles.

**Google Jobs:** Indexing your jobs through structured data so they appear in Google Search job results is low-cost and high-reach for active candidates.

**Programmatic job advertising:** Automated buying across multiple job boards optimized for cost-per-applicant or cost-per-qualified-applicant. Efficient for high-volume roles where manual channel management is impractical.

### Community channels

Tech communities (Viblo, Kipalog, Vietnam developer Slack groups), professional associations, alumni networks, and niche forums are trust-dense environments where authentic engagement outperforms paid advertising. Contributions require investment in genuine value — sharing knowledge, not just job postings.

## Campaign Measurement

Recruitment marketing investment should be measured, but many teams measure the wrong things.

### Metrics worth tracking

**Qualified applicant rate:** Applicants who meet the minimum criteria for a role ÷ total applicants. A high volume of unqualified applicants is not a success — it increases screening cost without improving outcomes.

**Source-to-hire:** Where do your actual hires come from? This is the metric that should drive channel investment decisions, not applicant volume or cost-per-click.

**Cost per hire by source:** Total channel investment ÷ hires from that channel. Compare across channels to identify where ROI is highest.

**Career site conversion rate:** Visitors to career site ÷ applications submitted. Low conversion despite adequate traffic indicates a content or UX problem.

**Employer brand perception:** Candidate survey data on why they applied, what they knew about the company before applying, and whether their impression of the employer changed through the process.

### Metrics that mislead

- Total applicant volume (high volume with low qualified rate wastes recruiter time)
- Cost per click (traffic is not candidates)
- Glassdoor rating as a primary KPI (gameable and not well correlated with hiring outcomes)
