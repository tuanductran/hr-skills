# Planning Recruitment Marketing Campaigns

## Overview

Recruitment marketing is distinct from one-to-one candidate outreach: it's about attracting candidates at scale through content, channels, and campaigns rather than individual sourcing conversations. The discipline borrows heavily from consumer marketing — funnel thinking, channel measurement, content calendars — but applied to a market where the "product" is a job opportunity and the "conversion" is a qualified application, which changes both what good creative looks like and how success should be measured.

## Campaign Planning

A recruitment marketing campaign should start from a defined hiring goal, not from a channel or content idea in isolation:

- **Define the specific hiring need the campaign serves** — role type, volume, timeline — since campaign design differs substantially between a single senior hire and a high-volume seasonal hiring push
- **Identify the target candidate profile** with enough specificity to guide both channel choice and creative tone — a campaign aimed at experienced senior engineers looks and sounds different from one aimed at early-career candidates
- **Set a measurable goal tied to qualified applications or hires**, not just impressions or clicks — vanity metrics like reach are easy to hit and easy to misinterpret as success

## Writing Job Ad Copy That Converts

Job ad copy for paid or social distribution differs from a standard job description — it needs to earn attention in a crowded feed before a candidate ever reaches the full posting:

- Lead with the most differentiating, specific detail about the role or opportunity, not a generic company description — specificity is what stops a scroll
- Write for the channel's actual format and audience expectations — copy that works on LinkedIn often reads as too formal for a platform like TikTok or Instagram, and vice versa
- Include a clear, low-friction call to action — every additional step between seeing the ad and starting an application measurably reduces conversion

## Content Calendar Design

A content calendar keeps recruitment marketing consistent rather than reactive, campaign-by-campaign:

- Balance always-on employer brand content (culture, employee stories, day-in-the-life content) with role-specific campaign content tied to active hiring needs — always-on content builds the audience that role-specific campaigns later convert
- Plan content around known hiring cycles (seasonal peaks, new office openings, major team expansions) rather than only reacting to open requisitions as they appear
- Repurpose high-performing content across channels rather than creating entirely new assets for each platform, which is rarely sustainable at scale

## Channel Strategy

| Channel type | Best suited for | Measurement focus |
|---|---|---|
| Paid social and job boards | High-volume roles, broad candidate pools | Cost per application, application-to-qualified rate |
| Organic social and employer brand content | Long-term pipeline building, employer reputation | Engagement trend, follower growth relevance to target audience |
| Programmatic job advertising | Scaling reach efficiently across many open roles simultaneously | Cost per qualified application at portfolio level |
| Niche or community-specific channels | Specialized or hard-to-reach candidate segments | Quality of applicants relative to volume, not volume alone |

Allocate budget based on cost-per-qualified-application by channel, reviewed regularly, rather than a fixed allocation decided once and left unchanged — channel performance shifts with market conditions and platform algorithm changes.

## Measuring Campaign ROI

- Track the full funnel from campaign exposure through application through hire, not just top-of-funnel metrics — a campaign that drives high application volume but low qualified-conversion is underperforming even if the headline numbers look strong
- Attribute hires to source channel consistently, using the same tracking methodology across campaigns so comparisons are meaningful over time
- Calculate cost per qualified hire, not just cost per application, as the primary ROI metric — application volume without quality context overstates a campaign's actual value

## Common Pitfalls

- **Measuring success by reach or impressions** rather than qualified application or hire outcomes, which rewards campaigns that look successful but don't actually solve the hiring need.
- **Running campaign content divorced from actual open roles and hiring timelines**, which creates candidate interest with nowhere productive to go.
- **Using identical creative and channel strategy across very different roles and candidate audiences**, which underperforms compared to tailoring both to the specific hiring goal.
