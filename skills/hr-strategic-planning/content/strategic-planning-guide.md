# HR Strategic Planning

## Overview

HR strategic planning is the process of defining what the HR function will focus on, invest in, and measure over a defined planning horizon — aligned to the business strategy and calibrated to what the organization actually needs from its people and organization.

Most HR functions produce an annual plan that is really a calendar of programs and initiatives, not a strategy. A genuine HR strategy makes explicit choices about priorities, defines what will not be done, and connects HR work to business outcomes.

## The Strategic Planning Process

### Input gathering

Before developing HR strategy, gather inputs from multiple sources:

**Business strategy:** What is the business trying to achieve in the next 1-3 years? What are the key strategic bets? What capabilities are required? Where are the organization's biggest vulnerabilities?

**Workforce data:** Current workforce composition, attrition trends, skills gaps, engagement data, diversity metrics, span and layer analysis.

**People risk assessment:** What are the top people-related risks to business execution? Succession gaps, capability shortfalls, culture issues, compliance exposures.

**External environment:** Labor market trends, regulatory changes, competitive dynamics in the talent market, technology changes affecting work.

**Stakeholder input:** What do business leaders believe HR's most important contribution would be? What problems do they most want HR help solving?

### Prioritization

From these inputs, HR leadership should identify the 3-5 areas where HR investment will have the most material impact on business outcomes. These become the strategic priorities.

For each priority, define:
- What business outcome does this priority serve?
- What is the current state and target state?
- What specifically will HR do to close the gap?
- How will success be measured and by when?
- What resources (people, budget, technology) are required?

### What to stop doing

Effective prioritization requires explicitly naming what will be deprioritized. HR functions that add new priorities without removing old ones accumulate work until they are not effective at anything.

Common candidates for deprioritization or automation:
- Low-value transactional work that can be moved to self-service or automated
- Programs that continue because of organizational inertia rather than demonstrated impact
- Reporting that no one acts on
- Processes that exist for compliance reasons but have never been tested

### OKRs for HR

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) provide a useful structure for translating HR strategy into measurable outcomes:

**Objective:** A qualitative, inspiring statement of what HR is trying to achieve. "Build the leadership capability the business needs to scale from 300 to 600 employees."

**Key Results:** 3-5 specific, measurable outcomes that would indicate the objective is being achieved. "75% of new managers complete management onboarding within 30 days of promotion." "Manager upward feedback scores improve from 3.6 to 4.0 by Q4." "Internal promotion rate for senior roles reaches 50%."

Key results should measure outcomes, not activities. "Launch manager training program" is an activity. "Manager effectiveness scores improve by 0.3 points" is an outcome.

## Workforce Planning Integration

Strategic HR planning and workforce planning are closely related and should be developed together.

Workforce planning answers: what people do we need, when, where, and with what skills to execute the business strategy? HR strategy answers: how will we build, source, and manage that workforce?

The connection is: workforce plan reveals capability gaps → HR strategy defines how to close them (build, buy, borrow) → HR plan allocates resources to close the highest-priority gaps.

Organizations that do workforce planning and HR strategy separately produce plans that are not connected to each other and therefore do not integrate with business planning.

## Communicating HR Strategy

An HR strategy that is not understood and supported by senior business leaders will not be resourced or acted on.

### Communicating to the CEO and leadership team

Frame the HR strategy in business language, not HR language:
- Lead with business context ("the business needs to scale from X to Y — here is what that means for people and organization")
- Connect every HR priority to a specific business outcome
- Quantify the cost of the problems being addressed where possible
- Be explicit about trade-offs and resource requirements
- Present the measurement framework — how will we know if this is working?

### Communicating to the HR team

The HR team needs to understand how their individual work connects to the strategic priorities. A strategy presentation is not sufficient — it needs to be translated into individual team members' goals and development priorities.

### Annual planning calendar

HR strategic planning should be integrated with the business planning calendar, not run separately:

- Business strategy confirmed → HR assesses people and organization implications
- Finance sets enterprise budget envelope → HR develops headcount and program budget
- Business unit plans finalized → HR refines priorities and aligns to business unit needs
- Annual HR strategy and plan finalized and presented to leadership team

HR strategies developed in isolation from the business planning cycle arrive too late to be resourced and are rarely aligned to what the business actually approved.
