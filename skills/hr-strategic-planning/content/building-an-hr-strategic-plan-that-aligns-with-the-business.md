# Building an HR Strategic Plan That Aligns with the Business

## Overview

Many HR strategic plans are built as a compilation of program plans — recruiting goals, L&D initiatives, engagement targets — assembled independently and stapled together under a shared cover page, rather than derived from a single, explicit view of what the business strategy actually requires from the workforce. The result reads like an HR activity calendar rather than a strategy, and struggles to demonstrate its connection to business outcomes when questioned by leadership.

## Starting from Business Strategy, Not HR Priorities

An HR strategic plan should be built backward from the business strategy, not forward from what HR functions already do well or have historically prioritized:

- Translate the business strategy into explicit workforce implications — what capabilities, headcount shape, and organizational design does this specific strategy require, rather than a generic "we need great talent" statement
- Identify where the current workforce and organizational state diverges from what the strategy requires — this gap, not a list of HR's own aspirations, should drive the plan's priorities
- Revisit this translation whenever business strategy shifts materially, rather than only at the annual planning cycle — an HR strategy built against an outdated business strategy loses relevance quickly

## Structuring the Plan Around Priorities, Not Functions

A common structural mistake is organizing the plan by HR function (recruiting section, L&D section, comp section) rather than by strategic priority, which obscures how different functions need to work together toward the same outcome:

- Organize around 3–5 strategic priorities derived from the business-strategy translation, each potentially spanning multiple HR functions
- For each priority, name the specific initiatives, owners, and success metrics — a priority without a defined owner and metric tends to lose momentum against more concretely tracked work
- Resist the temptation to include every worthwhile HR initiative in the plan — a strategic plan crowded with too many priorities functions as no priority at all, diluting both focus and resourcing

## Setting Meaningful Success Metrics

- Tie metrics to business outcomes where a credible causal link exists, not just HR activity volume — "number of training sessions delivered" measures activity, not impact; "capability gap closure in critical skill areas" measures something closer to actual strategic contribution
- Set both leading and lagging indicators for each priority — lagging indicators (like turnover or capability metrics) confirm impact, but leading indicators (like pipeline health or program participation) give earlier signal on whether the plan is on track
- Avoid metrics that can be hit through effort without reflecting genuine progress on the underlying priority, which is a common flaw in poorly designed HR scorecards

## Aligning with Business Planning Cycles

An HR strategic plan disconnected from the organization's broader planning rhythm struggles to secure resourcing and visibility:

- Time the HR strategic planning cycle to precede or align with the business's annual and budget planning cycles, so workforce and organizational implications are available as an input rather than arriving after resourcing decisions are already made
- Build in a mid-cycle review checkpoint, not just an annual planning moment — business strategy and conditions shift within a year more often than a purely annual cadence accounts for
- Present the HR plan using the same cadence and format the business uses for its own strategic reviews, which improves both comprehension and credibility with non-HR leadership

## Communicating the Plan Across the Organization

- Translate the plan into audience-specific versions — executive leadership needs the strategic narrative and top-line metrics; HRBPs and managers need the specific initiatives and their role in execution
- Revisit and communicate progress against the plan on a defined cadence throughout the year, not only at the next annual planning cycle — a plan that's only discussed once a year loses momentum and visibility
- Be explicit about trade-offs made in prioritization, including what the plan deliberately chose not to prioritize, since this transparency helps manage expectations from stakeholders whose preferred initiative didn't make the cut

## Common Pitfalls

- **Building the plan as a compilation of existing function-level plans** rather than deriving priorities from an explicit translation of business strategy.
- **Including too many priorities**, which dilutes focus and resourcing across all of them rather than concentrating effort where it matters most.
- **Measuring HR activity rather than business-relevant impact**, which makes it difficult to demonstrate the plan's actual contribution when questioned.
