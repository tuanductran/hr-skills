# Retirement Benefits Strategy and Plan Design

## Overview

> Related example: [Redesigning the 401(k) Plan and Communicating a Provider Change](../examples/redesigning-the-401k-plan-and-communicating-a-provider-change.md).

Retirement benefits are the most significant long-term financial obligation an organization takes on. They affect employee retention, recruitment competitiveness, organizational liability, and financial statements. Designing and managing retirement benefits is highly technical, regulated, and consequential.

A retirement plan is not just "we put money in a savings account for you." It is a complex system involving tax treatment, vesting schedules, investment options, required disclosures, fiduciary duties, and actuarial assumptions.

## Retirement Plan Fundamentals

### Types of plans (US context)

**Defined Benefit (DB) plans:** The organization guarantees a specific income stream in retirement based on a formula (typically: years of service × average salary × percentage). The organization bears the investment risk and the obligation to fund the plan.

Advantages: Predictable retirement income; strong retention (vesting schedules encourage long tenure).
Disadvantages: Expensive and volatile for the organization; complex to manage; most employers have moved away from them.

**Defined Contribution (DC) plans:** The organization contributes a defined amount (typically matched to employee contributions). The employee owns the account; investment risk is on the employee.

Advantages: Predictable cost to the organization; portable (employee owns the money); simple to understand.
Disadvantages: Retirement income depends on investment returns and employee discipline; if employee doesn't contribute, employer match goes unused.

**Hybrid plans:** Combination of DB and DC — common in the public sector.

Most US private sector organizations use DC plans (401(k) for for-profits, 403(b) for nonprofits, SIMPLE IRA for small businesses). DB plans are mostly found in government and unionized environments.

### Key plan design decisions

**Contribution matching:** If employee contributes X%, will the organization match? How much — 50 cents on the dollar? Dollar for dollar? Capped at what?

High matching (dollar for dollar up to 6% of salary) is more generous but expensive for the organization; it also improves employee participation. Low or no matching reduces costs but may leave employees with inadequate retirement savings.

**Vesting schedule:** How long does the employee need to be with the organization before they fully own employer contributions?

Immediate vesting: employee owns contributions immediately (rare, expensive).
Cliff vesting: employee gets nothing until they hit a threshold (typically 3 years), then gets everything (simple but harsh).
Gradual vesting: employee gets a percentage each year (20% per year over 5 years is common; smoother).

**Investment options:** What can employees invest their money in? How many options? How actively managed vs. passive index funds?

More options = more confusion; fewer options = employees feel limited. A common approach: target-date funds (the investment automatically shifts more conservative as retirement approaches) plus a few core options (low-cost index funds).

**Portability:** Can the employee take the account with them when they leave? (Yes, for DC plans — this is legal requirement in the US). Can they roll it into an IRA? Yes. Can they keep it in the company plan? Only if they're not the last employee left; most companies move small accounts out.

## Regulatory and Compliance Landscape

Retirement plans are among the most regulated employee benefits. In the US, the rules come from:

**ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act):** Establishes standards for plan administration, fiduciary duties, disclosure, and vesting. Violation can result in penalties on the organization and lawsuits from employees.

**IRS tax code (Section 401(a), 403(b), 408, 4975, etc.):** Determines what types of plans are qualified for tax-deferred growth, contribution limits, required distributions, and penalties for violations.

**State law:** Some states (California, Illinois) have additional requirements for private employer retirement plans or automatic IRA enrollment.

**DOL regulations:** The Department of Labor enforces ERISA and has rules on plan administration, fiduciary duty, disclosure.

For an organization, this means:
- Hiring a third-party administrator (TPA) to handle the technical compliance (contribution calculations, vesting verification, required disclosures, annual testing)
- Retaining a benefits attorney to advise on design and regulatory changes
- Regular audits to confirm plan is in compliance

## Designing Competitive Retirement Benefits

Retirement competitiveness depends on:

**Matching formula:** What percentage does the organization match? How much can the employee contribute? A 50% match up to 6% of salary (so if you contribute 6%, you get a 3% match) is reasonably competitive in many industries. In tech and finance, 100% matches are common.

**After-tax contributions:** Does the plan allow after-tax employee contributions (money contributed with already-taxed dollars)? Allows higher-income employees to save more than pre-tax limits allow. Not all plans offer this.

**Loan provisions:** Can employees borrow against their balance? This helps with liquidity but reduces retirement savings.

**Roth options:** Can employees contribute to a Roth 401(k) (after-tax contributions, tax-free growth)? Younger employees often prefer Roth; older employees prefer traditional pre-tax.

**Financial wellness tools:** Does the organization provide tools/coaching to help employees plan for retirement? Financial planning is a growing benefit, especially as DB plans disappear and more responsibility shifts to individuals.

## Communication and Enrollment

A great plan is only useful if employees understand it and participate. Common communication failures:

**Complexity:** Retirement plans are genuinely complex; over-simplifying in communications means employees make poor choices. The goal is enough clarity to make a reasonable decision without overwhelming jargon.

**Timing:** Enrolling in retirement benefits happens once (at hire) or annually (open enrollment). If communication only happens then, employees forget. Ongoing education is more effective.

**Behavioral economics:** Employees are bad at long-term financial planning (everyone knows they should save more; most don't). Default enrollment (automatically enroll at 3% contribution, let employee opt out) increases participation dramatically versus opt-in.

Successful programs:
- Default enrollment at a reasonable percentage (3-4% is common)
- Quarterly education emails or articles on a specific topic (how to choose investments, how much is enough, how inflation affects retirement)
- One-on-one financial coaching for employees who want it
- Encouraging participants to increase contributions at raises (automatic increase programs)

## Retirement Plan Administration Lifecycle

**Design and implementation:** Work with an attorney and consultant to design the plan, get it adopted by the company, implement with a TPA.

**Annual administration:** Process payroll deductions, calculate matches, run required compliance testing (especially for organizations with higher-paid employees who face contribution limits), provide required disclosures (Summary Plan Description, Summary of Material Modifications, annual statements).

**Enrollments and changes:** Handle new employee enrollments, open enrollment changes, life event changes.

**Regulatory updates:** Stay current on regulatory changes; many require plan amendments.

**Audit:** Annual audit by an external auditor to confirm the TPA is administering correctly.

**Termination (if plan ever closes):** This is complex; involves calculating final benefits, distributing assets, and final filings with the government.

For many organizations, the cost of a third-party administrator ($30K-$100K+ annually depending on size and complexity) is worth it versus managing internally. Retirement plan errors are expensive to fix and can result in penalties.

## Retirement Adequacy and Retirement Readiness

Organizations increasingly track whether their retirement plans are helping employees actually achieve adequate retirement savings.

**Replacement rate:** What percentage of pre-retirement income will the employee's retirement income (Social Security + savings) provide? Conventional wisdom: 70-80% is needed to maintain living standard. Many Americans are on track for 40-50%.

**Retirement readiness:** What percentage of your workforce is on track for an adequate retirement? This varies dramatically by age and tenure (older tenure employees are more likely to be on track).

Progressive organizations:
- Model employee retirement readiness by age cohort
- Increase matching or auto-enrollment percentages if readiness is inadequate
- Provide financial coaching to help employees catch up
- Track retirement readiness as an HR metric

This is partly out of genuine care for employee wellbeing, and partly recognition that employees in financial stress are less productive and more likely to leave.
