# Leading a Reorganization and Stabilization Plan for a Scaling Company

## Context

You are the HR Business Partner supporting a 230-person product company that has decided to move from a functional structure (separate Engineering, Design, and Product departments reporting up to their own VPs) to cross-functional product pods. The CEO announced the strategic direction at an all-hands two weeks ago without a detailed plan, and since then three team leads have privately raised concerns about unclear reporting lines, and one senior engineer has already mentioned looking externally. You have been asked to build the actual structure, the change plan, and a stabilization approach — starting essentially from the CEO's one-paragraph vision.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our CEO announced we're moving to cross-functional pods but there's no actual plan yet, and people are already getting anxious. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a strategic direction already announced without a detailed plan, and early anxiety signals among team leads — start with two priority areas:

**Structural clarity (the team leads' concern):**

- Translate the CEO's vision into 2–3 concrete structural options before presenting anything final
- Define decision rights early — who owns prioritization, hiring, and budget within each pod
- Identify which roles are most affected by the change in reporting lines

The team leads' anxiety is not really about pods as a concept — it is about the absence of clarity on what happens to their own role and authority. You need a concrete answer before the rumor mill fills the gap.

**Retention risk containment (the senior engineer's concern):**

- Have a direct, individual conversation with the engineer who is already looking externally
- Identify whether this is a structure-specific concern or a broader signal about change communication
- Move quickly — the longer ambiguity persists, the more it compounds

> **Avoid:** Do not present a single finalized structure to leadership without having modeled alternatives. A reorganization announced two weeks ago with anxiety already building does not have room for a second restart if the first structural proposal does not hold up to leadership scrutiny.

## Step 2 — Modeling Structural Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few structural options for moving from functional departments to cross-functional pods so I can compare trade-offs before proposing anything."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Full pod restructuring (all ICs move into pods):**

```txt
Structure: 6 cross-functional pods, each with embedded Engineering,
           Design, and Product members reporting to a Pod Lead
Span of control: Pod Leads manage 6-8 people each (within healthy range)
Trade-off: Maximum cross-functional alignment, but removes functional
           career ladders and manager relationships built over years
```

**Option B — Dual reporting (functional managers retained, pod leads coordinate delivery):**

```txt
Structure: ICs remain under functional managers (Eng, Design, Product)
           for career development; Pod Leads coordinate day-to-day
           delivery work without formal reporting authority
Span of control: Functional managers retain 8-10 reports; Pod Leads
           coordinate 6-8 people without HR authority
Trade-off: Preserves career paths and existing manager trust, but
           creates ambiguous authority over prioritization decisions
```

**Option C — Phased transition (pilot with 2 pods before full rollout):**

```txt
Structure: 2 pilot pods formed from volunteers and lower-risk teams;
           remaining org stays functional for 1 quarter
Span of control: Limited to pilot scope; full structure deferred
Trade-off: Lower immediate disruption and a chance to learn before
           full rollout, but delays the CEO's stated strategic direction
           and may read as indecisive after a public announcement
```

**Benchmark context:**

A healthy span of control generally falls between 6 and 10 direct reports per manager. Option A and the pilot pods in Option C both fall within this range; Option B's dual-reporting model is the most structurally fragile, since unclear decision rights between functional managers and Pod Leads is a well-documented source of organizational friction.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the CEO has already publicly announced the direction, Option C's full deferral risks appearing indecisive, but a modified version — full structural rollout with decision rights clearly assigned to Pod Leads, paired with retained 1:1 career development conversations from former functional managers — captures most of Option A's clarity while softening the career-path concern driving the team leads' anxiety.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Underlying Resistance

**Sample prompt:**

> "Three team leads have raised concerns privately and one senior engineer is already looking externally. I need to understand what's really driving this before I finalize the structure."

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming the cause, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Authority and decision rights concerns:**

- Are team leads worried about losing formal authority, or about ambiguity in who has authority going forward?
- Is the concern about the structure itself, or about how the decision was communicated without their input?

**2. Career path and identity concerns:**

- Do affected employees see a clear path for growth and recognition within the new pod structure?
- Is the move perceived as flattening or removing visible leadership roles people had worked toward?

**3. Timing and communication gaps:**

- How much detail has actually been shared since the all-hands announcement two weeks ago?
- Has the silence since the announcement itself become the primary source of anxiety, independent of the structure's actual merits?

**4. Individual flight-risk specifics:**

- For the senior engineer specifically, is the concern structural, or does it predate the reorg announcement and the timing is coincidental?
- Has anyone had a direct, individual conversation with this person yet, or has communication been limited to broad updates?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present resistance as a sign the reorg decision was wrong. Bring a clear picture of which concerns are structural and addressable through design choices (decision rights, career paths) versus which are communication gaps that can be closed quickly with direct conversations.

## Step 4 — Building the Change Management Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build a change management plan for this reorg using the ADKAR framework, since leadership announced the change but we have no actual plan yet."

**Skill response — ADKAR-based change plan:**

| ADKAR Stage | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Awareness | Achieved via all-hands announcement | None — already in place |
| Desire | Mixed — team leads have concerns, not yet bought in | Individual conversations addressing authority and career path concerns |
| Knowledge | Low — no structural detail has been shared yet | Publish the finalized pod structure with decision rights and team assignments |
| Ability | Not yet built | Provide Pod Lead training on prioritization, delegation, and cross-functional facilitation |
| Reinforcement | Not yet planned | Build a 90-day stabilization check-in cadence (see Step 7) |

**Stakeholder-specific communication sequence:**

1. Team leads and Pod Lead candidates — individual conversations first, before any broader announcement, given their existing anxiety
2. Affected ICs — small group sessions by current functional team, allowing space for questions
3. Broader organization — follow-up all-hands with the finalized structure, decision rights, and timeline

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequence matters: team leads should never learn structural details through a broad announcement after already raising concerns privately — that sequencing itself would compound the trust issue
- Build in explicit space for questions rather than treating communication as one-directional

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the original all-hands announcement as the "Awareness" stage being complete and moving straight to a final detailed rollout. The two-week gap with no follow-up has likely degraded trust in the process itself, which needs to be acknowledged directly in the next communication.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this structural recommendation and change plan to the CEO and leadership team. How do I frame it given they already announced the direction publicly?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership responds to a credible plan that builds on their stated direction rather than appearing to second-guess it. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Affirm the direction with a concrete plan:**

"Building on the direction announced two weeks ago, I've modeled three structural approaches for the pod transition and recommend a hybrid: full pod formation with clear Pod Lead decision rights, while preserving career development conversations through former functional managers to address the career-path concerns we're hearing."

**Part 2 — Name the current risk directly:**

"Three team leads have raised concerns about reporting clarity, and one senior engineer has begun looking externally. This isn't a signal the direction is wrong — it's a signal that the two-week gap since the announcement, without structural detail, has created space for anxiety. We need to close that gap quickly."

**Part 3 — Present the plan and ask for what you need:**

"I'm recommending individual conversations with the affected team leads this week, a finalized structure communication within 10 days, and a 90-day stabilization plan with defined check-ins. I need your support prioritizing the team lead conversations this week, since further delay increases retention risk."

> **Key principle:** Leadership responds well to a plan that shows momentum and ownership rather than a list of problems — frame the resistance as expected and addressable, with a clear sequence already in motion.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Change Communication

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft communication for the team lead conversations and the broader all-hands follow-up. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without it sounding generic?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the structural decision, the specific concerns raised so far (anonymized if needed), and the tone appropriate for your organization before drafting anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am an HR Business Partner leading the change communication for a
reorganization from functional departments to cross-functional pods
at a 230-person product company.

Context:
- CEO announced the direction at an all-hands two weeks ago with no detail since
- Team leads are concerned about loss of authority and unclear reporting lines
- We are forming pods with Pod Leads holding prioritization and delegation
  decision rights; former functional managers retain career development conversations

Please:
1. Draft talking points for individual conversations with team leads,
   acknowledging the anxiety directly and explaining the new decision rights
2. Draft a short, direct all-hands follow-up message announcing the
   finalized structure and timeline
3. Suggest 3-4 likely questions employees will ask and draft honest,
   direct answers to each
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with stabilization planning:**

```txt
Based on this plan, draft a 90-day stabilization check-in framework
with specific questions to ask Pod Leads and team members at the
30, 60, and 90-day marks to confirm the new structure is holding.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting clear, structured talking points quickly
- Anticipating likely employee questions and concerns
- Generating a reusable stabilization check-in framework

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Reading the room and adjusting tone in the actual individual conversations
- Judgment on which specific concerns are unique to your organization's history
- The trust that comes from a leader being personally present and responsive

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "The structure will be announced next week. I don't want this to quietly revert to the old functional reporting once the initial excitement fades. How do I build a stabilization plan?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Are Pod Leads actually exercising the prioritization and delegation authority assigned to them, or are decisions quietly flowing back to former functional managers?
- Are cross-functional pod members reporting clarity on who they go to for what type of decision?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Is delivery velocity stable or improving compared to the pre-reorg baseline, or is the new structure creating friction that needs adjustment?
- Are career development conversations with former functional managers actually happening, or has that commitment quietly lapsed?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would Pod Leads and pod members describe the new structure as the "real" way the organization works now, or as a layer on top of how things actually still get decided?
- What structural adjustments, if any, are needed based on what has been learned in the first quarter?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Pod Lead decision-rights check-in | HRBP | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Pod member clarity pulse check | HRBP + Pod Leads | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Delivery velocity review | Engineering/Product leadership | Monthly |
| Career development conversation audit | HRBP | 60 days |

> **Common finding at fast-growing companies:** Reorganizations frequently look successful immediately after launch because the announcement itself creates temporary clarity and energy. The real test is whether the new decision rights are still being used, unprompted, 60–90 days later — without a deliberate check-in cadence, informal reversion to old patterns is the most common failure mode.

## Full Organizational Development Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (strategic announcement, structural friction, M&A, growth)
                    ↓
Model multiple structural options and compare trade-offs
                    ↓
Diagnose the resistance and concerns driving early friction
                    ↓
Build a change management plan alongside the structure decision
                    ↓
Present the recommendation to leadership with risk and momentum framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft tailored change communication for each stakeholder group
                    ↓
Design a 90-day stabilization plan with defined checkpoints
                    ↓
Report on adoption and stability to leadership at each checkpoint
```

### Common Organizational Development Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Announcing a structural direction before any detailed plan exists | Pair any public announcement with a committed timeline for detailed follow-up |
| Finalizing one structural option without modeling alternatives | Model 2–3 structural options and compare trade-offs before recommending one |
| Treating resistance as a sign the decision was wrong | Diagnose resistance as structural, communication-based, or individual before reacting |
| Building a change plan only after the structure is finalized | Build change management alongside the structural design, not after it |
| Assuming adoption is complete once the announcement is sent | Track adoption through defined checkpoints over the following 90 days |
| Treating culture and structure as separate, unrelated problems | Diagnose culture and structural friction together, since they are often connected |
| Skipping a stabilization plan after implementation | Build a 90-day stabilization cadence with named owners before declaring the reorg complete |
