# Diagnosing an Engagement Crisis and Redesigning the Employee Experience for a Scaling Company

## Context

You are the Employee Experience Manager at a 280-person fintech company that has grown from 120 to 280 people in two years. Last month, the annual engagement survey returned a company-wide score of 58 — down from 71 the previous year. The sharpest declines are in belonging (down 18 points), manager effectiveness (down 14 points), and confidence in leadership direction (down 12 points). Since the results were shared, three senior engineers have resigned, the Head of Product has flagged "cultural drift" to the CEO in a leadership meeting, and two team leads have privately told HR that people on their teams feel like they are "working for a different company than the one they joined." The CEO has asked for a plan within two weeks. You have been asked to build the diagnosis, the recovery strategy, and an experience redesign roadmap — starting essentially from a survey score and a list of concerns with no prior EX infrastructure.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our engagement score dropped 13 points, belonging is down 18, three senior engineers just resigned, and the CEO wants a plan in two weeks. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a significant, multi-dimensional engagement decline with active retention consequences and no prior EX infrastructure — start with two priority areas before designing any programs:

**Retention risk containment (the senior engineer departures and flight risk):**

- Identify whether the three departures are connected by a common manager, team, or experience pattern, or whether they represent independent signals across the organization
- Run stay conversations with high-tenure and identified high-potential employees immediately — not a formal survey, but direct conversations with a defined question set, to understand whether the engagement decline reflects dissatisfaction that is still reversible
- Move quickly — engagement declines of this magnitude in high-growth companies have a compounding effect; the people most likely to leave next are the ones who have options and who are watching how leadership responds to the survey results

**Root cause diagnosis before program design (the belonging and manager effectiveness concern):**

- A 13-point overall drop with the sharpest declines in belonging and manager effectiveness is not a programs problem — it is a structural and cultural drift problem that programs alone will not fix
- The "different company" framing from team leads is a meaningful signal: rapid growth has almost certainly introduced a large number of managers and employees who did not experience the earlier culture, and the cultural continuity that long-tenure employees expected has been diluted
- Diagnose what "belonging" actually broke down into — was it team-level connection, identity alignment with the company's direction, confidence in career growth, or visibility from leadership? Each of these requires a different intervention

> **Avoid:** Do not open with a list of programs — a new recognition platform, a culture committee, a revised onboarding journey — before the root cause diagnosis is complete. Launching programs in response to an engagement crisis without understanding what drove the crisis produces visible activity without addressing the actual experience breakdown, and often generates a second, sharper decline when the next survey confirms nothing changed.

## Step 2 — Modeling Strategic Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few strategic options for responding to this engagement crisis so I can compare trade-offs before presenting anything to the CEO."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Company-level culture and experience relaunch (CEO-sponsored, visible, high-investment):**

```txt
Approach: CEO-sponsored culture refresh with a named initiative,
          company-wide communications, a culture task force, and
          a set of new or redesigned experience programs (onboarding,
          recognition, leadership visibility rituals)
Timeline: 6-8 weeks to visible launch; 6-month program arc
Trade-off: High visibility and CEO sponsorship signal organizational
           seriousness, but a company-level program alone does not
           address the team-level manager effectiveness gap driving
           the largest score declines; risks being perceived as
           "initiative theater" if the day-to-day team experience
           does not visibly change
```

**Option B — Manager-led team experience recovery (team-level, manager-enabled, HR-supported):**

```txt
Approach: Build manager capability for team-level experience design
          first; give managers a defined toolkit (team rituals,
          recognition practices, belonging conversation guides) and
          accountability for acting on their team's specific results
Timeline: 4-6 weeks for manager enablement; team-level actions
          visible within 6-8 weeks
Trade-off: Addresses the highest-impact lever (manager effectiveness)
           directly, but slower to produce company-level visible
           signals; risk is that the CEO's "plan in two weeks"
           expectation is not met by a manager enablement program
           alone
```

**Option C — Phased response (immediate listening and retention actions first, structural redesign second):**

```txt
Approach: Stay conversations and retention risk triage within
          2 weeks; root cause diagnosis and manager enablement
          kit in parallel; company-level experience redesign
          sequenced at 6-8 weeks once diagnosis is complete
Timeline: 2 weeks for listening and triage; 6-8 weeks for
          full redesign launch
Trade-off: Gives the CEO something credible to announce within
           two weeks (listening actions underway, retention
           triage in progress) while protecting the quality of
           the longer program design; risk is the perception
           that the "plan" is still in progress when leadership
           wants to see action
```

**Benchmark context:**

Research on engagement recovery consistently shows that manager-enabled team-level action produces more sustained engagement improvement than company-level programs alone, and that the quality of a manager's response to engagement results — whether they have a direct conversation with their team, acknowledge the concerns, and act on at least one thing — is the single highest predictor of score improvement in the following cycle.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the CEO's two-week timeline and the active retention risk, a modified Option C is the strongest path: stay conversations and retention triage announced publicly within two weeks, manager enablement kit built in parallel, and the full experience redesign launched at the 6-8 week mark with CEO sponsorship. This gives the CEO a credible, visible response to announce while protecting the quality of the structural work.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Root Cause of the Engagement Decline

**Sample prompt:**

> "The scores tell me what declined but not why. Before I design anything, I need to understand what specifically broke down in belonging, manager effectiveness, and leadership confidence. How do I diagnose this?"

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before designing any intervention, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Cultural drift and identity continuity:**

- When the team leads say people feel like they are working for "a different company," what specifically changed? Is it the pace, the decision-making style, the type of people being hired, or the values being modeled in practice?
- Are long-tenure employees and recent hires experiencing the culture differently, and if so, at what approximate hire date does the perception shift?

**2. Manager effectiveness and capability gaps:**

- The 14-point manager effectiveness decline in a company that doubled headcount in two years almost certainly reflects a manager population that expanded faster than manager development did
- Which teams show the sharpest declines in manager scores? Are these concentrated in specific business units or growth-phase hires?
- What specific behaviors are employees describing when manager effectiveness is low — clarity, availability, feedback, recognition, or advocacy?

**3. Belonging and connection breakdown:**

- Is belonging declining because people do not feel connected to their immediate team, to the company's direction, or to their own career path within the organization?
- For a hybrid or distributed workforce, has belonging decline tracked the shift to hybrid — or does it pre-date it?

**4. Leadership confidence and communication gaps:**

- The 12-point leadership confidence decline is most often driven by perceived inconsistency between what leadership says and what employees observe in decisions and priorities
- Has there been a period of significant strategic change, reorganization, or shifting priorities in the last 12 months that may have created a credibility gap?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present the engagement decline as a culture failure or a management failure. Bring a clear, specific diagnosis of which components of the experience broke down, which are addressable through manager enablement and quick experience fixes, and which require structural or leadership behavior changes that need CEO and executive team ownership — not just an HR program.

## Step 4 — Building the Experience Recovery Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build an experience recovery plan that covers the retention triage, the manager enablement, and the longer-term experience redesign — since I have to present to the CEO in two weeks."

**Skill response — phased recovery plan:**

| Phase | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| Retention triage | Not started | Stay conversations with identified flight risks and high-tenure employees within 10 days |
| Root cause diagnosis | Partial — survey data only | Qualitative listening sessions by business unit and tenure cohort to triangulate survey signals |
| Manager enablement | Not in place | Manager results briefing + team conversation guide + one-priority action commitment per manager |
| Quick experience fixes | Not identified | Identify 2-3 high-visibility, low-cost experience improvements that can be communicated as immediate actions |
| Company-level experience redesign | Not designed | Journey mapping, onboarding and recognition redesign, belonging initiative — sequenced at 6-8 weeks |
| Measurement cadence | Not in place | Pulse survey and behavioral signal tracking established before full redesign launches |

**Stakeholder-specific sequencing:**

1. Senior leadership and CEO — present the diagnosis and phased plan within two weeks; name what HR owns and what requires executive behavior change
2. People managers — individual team results briefed before any company-wide communication; manager action commitments secured before employee-facing announcements
3. Identified flight risks — individual stay conversations completed before any company announcement, so these employees feel seen individually rather than addressed through a broadcast
4. Broader employee population — company-wide communication on what was heard, what is being done, and when the next listening touchpoint will be

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequencing is critical for trust: employees who participated in the survey are watching whether leadership responds credibly or defensively; the first communication after the results should acknowledge the specific findings honestly rather than managing the narrative
- Manager results briefings must happen before employee-facing communications — managers who learn their team's scores at the same time as their team members cannot have a credible team conversation

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the two-week CEO deadline as the primary design constraint. A program launched in two weeks to meet a presentation deadline, built without the root cause diagnosis, will produce visible activity and a second engagement decline. The credible two-week deliverable is the listening plan, the retention triage in progress, and the recovery roadmap — not a finished program.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this recovery plan to the CEO in two weeks. How do I frame it given the expectation that I'll have 'a plan' — not a diagnosis and a roadmap?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership at a company in an engagement crisis responds to a plan that shows ownership, honesty, and a credible sequence — not a list of programs or a defense of the survey methodology. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Name what the scores are actually telling us:**

"The 13-point decline and the pattern across belonging, manager effectiveness, and leadership confidence is consistent with what we see in companies that have doubled in headcount in two years without building the culture infrastructure to support that growth. The team leads' framing — 'working for a different company' — is accurate: we have grown into a different company, and the experience has not kept pace with the size. This is addressable, but it requires structural work alongside programs."

**Part 2 — Name what HR owns and what leadership must own:**

"There is a portion of this recovery that HR and the experience team can drive — manager enablement, onboarding redesign, recognition, listening architecture. There is a portion that requires this leadership team's visible behavior change — decision transparency, leadership visibility with frontline employees, consistency between stated values and observed decisions. I want to name that distinction directly, because a recovery plan that only addresses the HR-owned portion will produce a partial result."

**Part 3 — Present the phased plan and ask for what you need:**

"In the next two weeks, stay conversations are underway and manager results briefings will be complete. In the next six to eight weeks, the manager enablement kit launches, the quick experience improvements are in place, and the journey redesign is in progress. I need three things from you: CEO-sponsored visible listening actions in the next two weeks, executive team commitment to the specific leadership behavior changes I'll share after the diagnosis is complete, and protected HR capacity to run this recovery without it being treated as a side project."

> **Key principle:** Leadership in an engagement crisis responds better to a plan that names what they need to do personally than to one that presents EX as an HR problem with an HR solution — the highest-impact engagement lever is always what the leadership team models, not what HR programs.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Experience Content and Recovery Communications

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft the stay conversation guide, the manager team results briefing, and the all-company engagement response communication. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without it sounding generic?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the specific score declines, the root cause hypotheses from the diagnostic, the tone appropriate for your company's culture, and the specific audience for each piece of content before generating anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```txt
I am the Employee Experience Manager designing an engagement recovery
plan for a 280-person fintech company. Our annual engagement score
dropped from 71 to 58, with the largest declines in belonging (-18),
manager effectiveness (-14), and leadership confidence (-12). The
company doubled headcount in two years with no prior EX infrastructure.

Context:
- Cultural drift is the primary diagnosis — rapid growth without
  culture-building infrastructure
- Manager effectiveness gap is concentrated in recently expanded
  manager population
- We are running stay conversations and manager results briefings
  before any public communication

Please:
1. Draft a stay conversation guide with 6-8 questions that surface
   flight risk and belonging breakdown without making the conversation
   feel like a retention interview
2. Draft a manager team results briefing script for managers to use
   when sharing their team's results — honest, not defensive, focused
   on listening and one-priority action commitment
3. Draft an all-company communication acknowledging the engagement
   results honestly, naming what was heard, and describing what will
   happen next — without overpromising or managing the narrative
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with experience design and measurement:**

```txt
Based on this recovery plan, draft a 90-day employee experience
recovery measurement framework with specific behavioral and survey
signals to track at the 30, 60, and 90-day marks to confirm the
recovery is producing real experience change, not just visible
activity metrics.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting listening and conversation guides with specific, psychologically safe question sets
- Generating manager briefing scripts that are honest and structured for a difficult conversation
- Producing employee communications that are direct and specific rather than corporate-neutral
- Building measurement frameworks with behavioral signal indicators alongside survey metrics

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Judgment on which specific organizational dynamics and leadership relationships shape how a message lands
- Reading the room in stay conversations and manager briefings where tone matters as much as content
- The visible, personal leadership presence that is the highest-impact signal in any engagement recovery

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "The stay conversations are done, the manager briefings are complete, and the company-wide communication goes out next week. How do I make sure this does not fade into 'the year we had a bad engagement survey and launched some things'?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Are managers having team-level conversations about the results and committing to at least one visible action, or are the briefings happening without follow-through?
- Are identified flight risks showing behavioral stabilization signals (re-engagement in projects, declining absence patterns), or is attrition continuing at the same rate?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Are the quick experience improvements that were committed to in the company communication visibly in place, or have they slipped?
- Are employees referencing the engagement response in their day-to-day experience, or has it faded from the conversation within six weeks?
- Is the belonging score trend measurable in pulse data, or are we still relying on the annual survey as the only signal?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would employees describe the company's response to the engagement decline as credible and visible, or as "they said they'd do things and we haven't seen it"?
- Has manager effectiveness improved at the team level based on pulse data and qualitative manager conversation signals?
- Is the experience redesign work (onboarding, recognition, belonging rituals) in place and producing early adoption signals?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Flight risk retention status check | HRBP | Bi-weekly for first 60 days |
| Manager action commitment follow-up | EX Manager + HRBPs | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Pulse survey (belonging and manager effectiveness items) | EX Manager | Monthly |
| Leadership behavior visibility check-in | EX Manager + Head of People | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Experience initiative adoption tracking | EX Manager | Monthly |

> **Common finding at companies recovering from an engagement crisis:** The 60-day mark is the most critical and most commonly underinvested checkpoint. Early recovery energy — from the company communication, the manager briefings, and the visible quick wins — typically produces a genuine short-term experience improvement. The test at 60 days is whether that improvement is being sustained by structural and behavioral change, or whether it was driven entirely by the attention the crisis itself generated. Without a deliberate 60-day check-in, the second group masquerades as the first until the next annual survey confirms nothing held.

## Full Employee Experience Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (engagement decline, attrition spike, culture drift signal, leadership request)
                    ↓
Retain flight risks and run stay conversations before designing any programs
                    ↓
Diagnose the root cause across belonging, manager effectiveness, and leadership trust dimensions
                    ↓
Model multiple strategic options and compare trade-offs
                    ↓
Build a phased recovery plan sequenced by urgency — retention triage, manager enablement, experience redesign
                    ↓
Present to leadership with honest diagnosis, a clear distinction between HR-owned and leadership-owned actions
                    ↓
Use AI to draft listening guides, manager briefing scripts, and employee communications
                    ↓
Design a 90-day stabilization plan with pulse listening and manager action tracking
                    ↓
Report on engagement signal, retention status, and initiative adoption at each checkpoint
```

### Common Employee Experience Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Launching programs in response to an engagement crisis before diagnosing root cause | Run a qualitative diagnosis using stay conversations, exit themes, and manager feedback before designing any program |
| Presenting the engagement survey results to leadership as the plan | The survey is the input; the diagnosis, recovery strategy, and measurement cadence are the plan |
| Treating engagement as an HR problem with an HR solution | Name what leadership must own — decision transparency, visible listening, values-to-behavior consistency — as part of the recovery plan, not an optional add-on |
| Briefing managers and employees on results at the same time | Always brief managers first; managers who learn their team's results alongside their team cannot have a credible follow-up conversation |
| Measuring recovery by program launch count rather than experience signal | Define pulse survey items and behavioral signal indicators before launch; track them at 30, 60, and 90 days |
| Treating belonging and manager effectiveness as the same problem | Diagnose and address them with different interventions; manager enablement and belonging design require different approaches |
| Skipping the 60-day checkpoint because early results look positive | The 60-day mark is where early crisis energy fades and structural change either holds or quietly reverts — it is the most important checkpoint and the most commonly skipped |
