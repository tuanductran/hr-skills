# Responding to an Employer Reputation Crisis and Rebuilding the EVP for a Scaling Company

## Context

You are the Employer Brand Manager at a 180-person fintech scaleup. Three months ago the company conducted a 12% reduction in force. Since then, the company's Glassdoor rating has dropped from 4.2 to 3.1, several detailed negative reviews describing "chaotic communication" and "broken promises" have been posted, and the recruiting team is reporting a 30% increase in candidate drop-off between offer and acceptance. The CEO wants a "brand refresh" announced at the next town hall, but there is no EVP work, no candidate experience audit, and no reputation monitoring in place yet. You have been asked to build the actual strategy, the content plan, and a stabilization approach — starting essentially from the CEO's one-line request.

## Step 1 — Choosing Where to Start

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our CEO wants a 'brand refresh' announced soon, but our Glassdoor rating just tanked after layoffs and candidates are dropping off after offers. Where do I start?"

**Expected skill response:**

Given your situation — a reputation drop with specific, credible negative reviews and a measurable candidate-experience problem — start with two priority areas before touching any content or messaging:

**Reputation diagnosis (the Glassdoor concern):**

- Read the negative reviews in full and categorize the recurring themes (communication, trust, process) rather than reacting to the rating number alone
- Identify whether the reviews reflect the layoff itself, or a broader pattern of EVP-reality gaps that predate it
- Check whether current employees would corroborate or contradict the reviews — a brand refresh built on messaging alone, while the underlying complaints are still true, will fail publicly

**Funnel risk containment (the offer drop-off concern):**

- Pull the data on where in the funnel candidates are dropping — is it post-offer specifically, or has it been building across the whole pipeline?
- Talk directly to a sample of recent declined candidates if possible; their stated reasons are more reliable than assumptions
- Move quickly — a reputation problem compounds every week candidates can see it before applying

> **Avoid:** Do not let "brand refresh" become a content and design exercise before the underlying reputation and candidate-experience issues are understood. A visually polished careers page launched on top of unresolved trust issues will read as tone-deaf and generate its own negative commentary.

## Step 2 — Modeling Strategic Options

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me model a few strategic options for responding to this reputation drop so I can compare trade-offs before proposing anything to the CEO."

**Skill walkthrough:**

**Option A — Full EVP relaunch (new messaging, new careers site, new content campaign):**

```text
Approach: Commission new EVP research, rebuild careers site, launch
          employee-story content campaign across LinkedIn and social
Timeline: 10-12 weeks to credible launch
Trade-off: Strong long-term positioning, but launching new messaging
           on top of unresolved trust issues risks being read as
           "spin" rather than change, especially given recent reviews
```

**Option B — Reputation repair first, EVP relaunch deferred (respond directly to review themes before any new messaging):**

```text
Approach: Public, specific response to recurring review themes;
          internal fixes to the communication and process issues named;
          EVP relaunch paused until repair work shows measurable signal
Timeline: 4-6 weeks for repair phase, EVP work resumes after
Trade-off: Slower visible "refresh," but addresses the credibility
           gap directly; risk is the CEO's town-hall timeline expectation
```

**Option C — Phased approach (targeted candidate experience fixes immediately, EVP research in parallel, full relaunch sequenced after):**

```text
Approach: Fix the highest-impact candidate experience gaps (offer-stage
          communication, hiring manager training) immediately; run EVP
          research in parallel using current and exited employees;
          sequence full relaunch only once repair signals are visible
Timeline: 2-3 weeks for immediate fixes, EVP research in parallel,
          relaunch at 10-12 weeks
Trade-off: Addresses both urgency (funnel drop-off) and credibility
           (reputation) without overpromising a fast "refresh," but
           requires the CEO to accept a longer, less dramatic timeline
```

**Benchmark context:**

Reputation recovery research generally shows that public responses addressing specific, named themes outperform generic statements, and that new employer brand messaging launched without addressing the underlying complaints tends to generate a second wave of negative commentary referencing the inconsistency directly.

> **Recommendation framing:** Given the CEO wants visible momentum but the reviews are specific and credible, Option A's full relaunch on the town-hall timeline risks public backlash, but a modified version of Option C — immediate candidate-experience fixes within 2-3 weeks paired with a direct, specific response to review themes, and the full EVP relaunch sequenced at 10-12 weeks once repair signals are visible — gives the CEO real progress to announce at town hall without overpromising a finished brand refresh.

## Step 3 — Diagnosing the Underlying Reputation Gap

**Sample prompt:**

> "Reviews mention 'chaotic communication' and 'broken promises' specifically. I need to understand what's really driving this before I propose any messaging."

**Skill response — diagnostic framework:**

Before assuming the cause, gather signals across four dimensions:

**1. Layoff-specific concerns:**

- Do the reviews describe the layoff decision itself, or the way it was communicated and executed?
- Is "broken promises" referring to specific commitments made before the layoff (stability assurances, growth plans) that did not hold?

**2. EVP-reality gap concerns:**

- Were the values or promises referenced in the reviews ever accurate, or did they reflect aspirational messaging that was already disconnected from the employee experience?
- Do current employee engagement signals corroborate or contradict the departed employees' account?

**3. Candidate-facing timing and communication gaps:**

- How much has actually been communicated to candidates currently in the pipeline since the layoff and the reviews became visible?
- Has silence toward active candidates become its own signal, independent of the reviews themselves?

**4. Funnel-specific drop-off drivers:**

- For the post-offer drop-off specifically, are declining candidates citing the reviews directly, or citing process issues (slow communication, unclear next steps) that are separate but compounding the reputation problem?
- Has anyone had a direct conversation with a sample of declined candidates, or has the team been working from funnel data alone?

> **What to bring to leadership:** Do not present the reputation drop as proof the company culture is fundamentally broken, and do not present it as something that will pass on its own. Bring a clear picture of which concerns are structural and addressable (communication practices, candidate experience gaps) versus which are credibility gaps that require visible follow-through, not new messaging, to close.

## Step 4 — Building the Reputation Repair and EVP Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "Help me build a plan that addresses the reputation drop and gets us toward a credible EVP relaunch, since leadership wants visible progress but we have no actual plan yet."

**Skill response — phased repair-to-relaunch plan:**

| Phase | Current Status | Action Needed |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Awareness of the problem | Achieved — leadership and recruiting both see the impact | None — already in place |
| Acknowledgment | Not yet done publicly | Specific, named response to recurring review themes, not a generic statement |
| Repair | Not yet started | Fix highest-impact candidate experience gaps (offer-stage communication, hiring manager prep) within 2-3 weeks |
| Evidence | Not yet built | Track whether repair actions produce measurable change (offer-acceptance rate, review sentiment) before any relaunch messaging |
| Relaunch | Premature at this stage | Sequence full EVP and content relaunch only once repair evidence is visible |

**Stakeholder-specific communication sequence:**

1. Active candidates in the pipeline — direct, individual outreach acknowledging the situation before any public response, given they are the most immediate flight risk
2. Hiring managers and recruiters — brief them on the repair plan and what they should and should not say if candidates raise the reviews directly
3. Broader public response — a specific, accountable response addressing the named review themes, published before any new EVP messaging

**Implementation notes:**

- Sequence matters: candidates currently in the pipeline should never learn about the repair plan through a public post before hearing directly from their recruiter — that sequencing would compound the "broken promises" theme already in the reviews
- Build in a visible feedback loop so departed and current employees see that specific concerns were heard, not just generalized "we hear you" messaging

> **Common mistake to avoid:** Treating the CEO's "brand refresh" request as permission to move straight to new messaging. The credibility gap described in the reviews needs to be closed through demonstrated change first, which needs to be reflected directly in what is communicated next, not skipped past.

## Step 5 — Presenting the Plan to Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to bring this reputation repair plan and EVP timeline to the CEO before town hall. How do I frame it given they want a 'brand refresh' announced soon?"

**Skill response — leadership communication framework:**

Leadership responds to a credible plan that gives them something real to announce, rather than a list of reasons the timeline needs to slip. Structure the conversation in three parts.

**Part 1 — Affirm the goal with a concrete plan:**

"I want us to get to a strong brand relaunch, and I've modeled three paths to get there. I'm recommending we lead with visible candidate experience fixes in the next 2-3 weeks and a direct, specific response to what the reviews are actually describing, then sequence the full EVP and content relaunch once we have evidence the repair work is landing."

**Part 2 — Name the current risk directly:**

"Our Glassdoor rating dropped to 3.1 with specific, credible reviews, and offer acceptance has dropped 30%. Launching new brand messaging on top of those unresolved concerns risks a second wave of negative commentary calling out the inconsistency directly. The fastest credible path to a strong relaunch is closing that gap first."

**Part 3 — Present the plan and ask for what you need:**

"I'm recommending we announce the candidate experience fixes and our specific response to the review themes at town hall, with a committed timeline for the full EVP relaunch at week 10-12. I need your support on hiring manager time for offer-stage process training this week, since that is the highest-impact fix we can make immediately."

> **Key principle:** Leadership responds well to a plan that gives them real, defensible progress to announce rather than a delayed timeline alone — frame the repair phase as the fastest credible route to the relaunch they actually want.

## Step 6 — Using AI to Draft Reputation Response and EVP Content

**Sample prompt:**

> "I need to draft the public response to the reviews and the candidate outreach messaging. How do I use Claude or ChatGPT to help without it sounding like generic PR language?"

**Skill response — AI-assisted drafting workflow:**

**Step 1 — Prepare your context:**

Summarize the specific themes in the negative reviews (anonymized where needed), the repair actions already committed to, and the tone appropriate for your company before drafting anything.

**Step 2 — Use this prompt structure:**

```text
I am the Employer Brand Manager responding to a reputation drop
following a layoff at a 180-person fintech scaleup. Our Glassdoor
rating dropped from 4.2 to 3.1, and reviews specifically describe
"chaotic communication" and "broken promises" during the layoff.

Context:
- We are committing to specific candidate experience fixes:
  faster offer-stage communication and hiring manager prep
- We are not yet ready to relaunch our full EVP messaging
- We want to acknowledge the review themes directly, not respond
  with generic corporate language

Please:
1. Draft a public response addressing the specific themes raised,
   acknowledging what happened and naming the concrete changes underway
2. Draft individual outreach messaging for candidates currently in
   our pipeline, acknowledging the situation honestly
3. Suggest 3-4 likely follow-up questions candidates or reviewers
   might raise, and draft honest, direct answers to each
```

**Step 3 — Follow up with stabilization planning:**

```text
Based on this plan, draft a 90-day reputation and candidate experience
stabilization framework with specific metrics and check-in questions
to track at the 30, 60, and 90-day marks to confirm the repair work
is producing real change before we proceed to the EVP relaunch.
```

**What AI does well here:**
- Drafting clear, specific response language quickly
- Anticipating likely follow-up questions from candidates and reviewers
- Generating a reusable stabilization tracking framework

**What AI cannot replace:**
- Judgment on which review details are too sensitive or specific to reference publicly
- Reading whether a draft response sounds accountable versus defensive in your company's actual voice
- The trust that comes from leadership and hiring managers being personally consistent in follow-through

## Step 7 — Designing the 90-Day Stabilization Plan

**Sample prompt:**

> "We're about to publish the response and start the candidate experience fixes. I don't want this to quietly fade once the town hall excitement passes. How do I build a stabilization plan?"

**Skill response — stabilization design:**

Build the stabilization plan around three checkpoints, each with a specific diagnostic question and an owner.

**30-day checkpoint:**

- Are hiring managers actually following the new offer-stage communication process, or has it quietly reverted to the old timeline?
- Is there any early movement in offer-acceptance rate or candidate drop-off compared to the pre-fix baseline?

**60-day checkpoint:**

- Has the Glassdoor rating or sentiment trend shown any measurable movement, or are new reviews continuing to reference the same unresolved themes?
- Are recruiters reporting that candidates are still raising the reviews unprompted, or has that started to decline?

**90-day checkpoint:**

- Would current employees and recent hires describe the repair commitments as real changes, or as a one-time response that did not hold?
- Is there enough credible evidence to proceed with the full EVP relaunch, or does the repair phase need to continue?

**Standing stabilization cadence:**

| Process Step | Owner | Frequency |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Offer-stage process adherence check | Employer Brand Manager + TA Lead | 30, 60, 90 days |
| Glassdoor and social sentiment review | Employer Brand Manager | Monthly |
| Candidate funnel drop-off review | TA Lead | Monthly |
| EVP relaunch readiness assessment | Employer Brand Manager | 90 days |

> **Common finding at companies recovering from a reputation drop:** Repair efforts frequently look successful immediately after a public response, because the response itself generates short-term goodwill. The real test is whether candidate-facing behavior and review sentiment have genuinely shifted 60-90 days later — without a deliberate tracking cadence, quiet reversion to the old process is the most common failure mode, and it sets up a second, harder-to-recover reputation hit.

## Full Employer Branding Workflow Summary

```text
Identify trigger (reputation drop, EVP misalignment, leadership request, scaling need)
                    ↓
Diagnose the reputation gap and funnel impact before drafting any messaging
                    ↓
Model multiple strategic options and compare trade-offs
                    ↓
Build a repair-to-relaunch plan sequenced with the EVP work, not ahead of it
                    ↓
Present the recommendation to leadership with risk and credible-progress framing
                    ↓
Use AI to draft tailored response and candidate communication for each stakeholder group
                    ↓
Design a 90-day stabilization plan with defined checkpoints
                    ↓
Report on repair signal and relaunch readiness to leadership at each checkpoint
```

### Common Employer Branding Mistakes

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Launching a "brand refresh" before diagnosing what is actually driving the reputation drop | Read and categorize the actual themes in reviews and candidate feedback before drafting any messaging |
| Finalizing one strategic response without modeling alternatives | Model 2-3 strategic options and compare trade-offs before recommending one |
| Treating negative reviews as proof the culture is irredeemable, or as something that will pass on its own | Diagnose concerns as structural, communication-based, or credibility-based before reacting |
| Building new EVP messaging before fixing the underlying experience gaps | Sequence repair work ahead of relaunch messaging, not after it |
| Assuming the reputation issue is resolved once a public response is published | Track repair signal through defined checkpoints over the following 90 days |
| Treating candidate experience and public reputation as separate, unrelated problems | Diagnose funnel drop-off and reputation sentiment together, since they are often connected |
| Skipping a stabilization plan after the initial response | Build a 90-day stabilization cadence with named owners before declaring the relaunch ready |
