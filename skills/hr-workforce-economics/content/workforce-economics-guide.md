# Workforce Economics

## Overview

Workforce economics is the discipline of quantifying the financial dimensions of workforce decisions — fully loaded labor cost, the cost of attrition and vacancies, the ROI of hiring versus developing versus automating, and the business case for people investments. It is the foundation for HR being treated as a strategic function rather than a cost center.

Most HR leaders can speak fluently about people strategy. Fewer can translate that strategy into the financial language that business leaders and CFOs use to make resource allocation decisions. Workforce economics is that translation layer.

## Fully Loaded Labor Cost

Salary is only a portion of what an employee costs. Fully loaded labor cost — the true cost to the organization — includes:

### Direct compensation

- Base salary
- Variable pay (target bonus, commission, equity vesting)
- Overtime and shift differentials

### Employer-paid benefits and taxes

- Employer social insurance contributions (in Vietnam: BHXH, BHYT, BHTN — totaling approximately 17-21.5% of salary up to the applicable ceiling)
- Health insurance premium (employer share)
- Retirement / pension contributions
- Other mandatory benefits by jurisdiction

### Overhead and facilities

- Allocated real estate cost per employee (office space, utilities, facilities management)
- IT equipment and software licenses
- Shared services overhead allocation

### Learning and development

- Training and certification costs
- Conference and learning budget
- Onboarding and ramp cost for new hires

A common rule of thumb for fully loaded labor cost is 1.25-1.4× base salary for salaried employees in Vietnam, and higher (1.5-1.8×) for roles with significant benefits, equity, and overhead. Build the actual multiplier for your organization rather than using the rule of thumb for financial decisions.

## The Cost of Attrition

Voluntary attrition is one of the most significant and underestimated workforce costs. It is also one of the most actionable — which makes building an accurate attrition cost model one of the highest-ROI activities in workforce economics.

### Attrition cost components

**Separation costs:** Exit processing, accrued leave payout, severance where applicable.

**Vacancy costs:** Lost productivity during the open period. Estimated as (daily fully loaded cost of the role) × (number of days to fill). Also include any premium paid to contractors or overtime for colleagues covering the vacancy.

**Recruiting costs:** Recruiter time, job advertising, agency fees if applicable, interview time (fully loaded cost of interviewers × hours spent), background check costs.

**Onboarding and ramp costs:** New hire's salary during ramp period at reduced productivity. A common estimate is that a new employee in a knowledge worker role reaches full productivity at 3-6 months, and the productivity gap during ramp represents a real cost.

**Training and development costs:** Role-specific training delivered to the new hire.

**Institutional knowledge loss:** Difficult to quantify precisely, but meaningful for specialized or tenured roles. The most practical proxy is increased error rate, longer cycle times, or reduced output quality during the ramp period.

### Typical fully loaded attrition cost ranges

- **Entry-level / junior roles:** 30-50% of annual salary
- **Mid-level professional roles:** 75-150% of annual salary
- **Senior / specialized roles:** 150-250% of annual salary
- **Executive roles:** 200%+ of annual salary, plus transition risk

These ranges vary significantly based on market tightness, role specialization, and organizational context.

## Build vs. Buy vs. Borrow

When a workforce gap exists, organizations have three options: build the capability internally (develop existing employees), buy it externally (hire), or borrow it (contract or outsource). The right answer depends on economics, timing, and strategic importance.

### Factors favoring build (internal development)

- The capability is a long-term, strategic differentiator
- Internal talent can be developed to the required level within the available time frame
- Retention of developed talent is high (development investment is not immediately lost to attrition)
- The development cost is lower than the external labor market premium for the skill

### Factors favoring buy (external hire)

- The capability is needed immediately and cannot wait for development
- The skill is specialized and rare internally
- External talent can be onboarded to productivity faster than internal development
- The talent market has supply available at the compensation the organization is willing to pay

### Factors favoring borrow (contract / outsource)

- The need is temporary, project-specific, or variable
- The capability is not a strategic differentiator worth owning permanently
- Speed is critical and neither hire nor develop can close the gap in time
- The risk of a long-term commitment is higher than the cost premium of contracting

## Building the People Investment Business Case

Every significant HR investment — a new training program, a retention initiative, a benefits enhancement — benefits from a business case that speaks the language of the finance team.

### Structure of a people investment business case

**Problem quantification:** What does the current state cost? Express in financial terms: attrition cost × attrition rate for a retention program; vacancy cost × average time-to-fill for a recruiting investment; productivity gap cost × affected headcount for a capability development program.

**Investment cost:** Total cost of the proposed intervention, including HR implementation time, vendor cost, technology, and ongoing maintenance.

**Expected outcome:** What change in the problem metric is expected? Be conservative and cite evidence where available.

**ROI calculation:** (Expected financial benefit − investment cost) ÷ investment cost. Include the time horizon over which the benefit is expected to accrue.

**Sensitivity analysis:** What does the ROI look like if key assumptions are wrong? If the retention program reduces attrition by 5% instead of 10%, is it still worth doing?

**Risks and mitigations:** What could prevent the expected outcome, and how will those risks be managed?

A well-constructed business case uses the organization's own data where possible (actual attrition costs, actual time-to-fill, actual vacancy costs) rather than industry benchmarks. The more specific the numbers, the more credible the case.
