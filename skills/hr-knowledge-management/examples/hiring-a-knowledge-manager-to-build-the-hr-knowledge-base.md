# Hiring a Knowledge Manager to Build the HR Knowledge Base

## Context

You are an HR Manager at a 250-person company that has grown fast without ever investing in documentation. Policies live in scattered Google Docs, SOPs exist only in people's heads, and HR fields the same repetitive questions over Slack every week. Leadership has approved a new **Knowledge Manager** role to build a real HR knowledge base from scratch. You have no background in content or systems work and need end-to-end support throughout the hiring process.

## Step 1 — Translating the Need from Leadership

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our Head of People says we need a 'Knowledge Manager' to fix our documentation problem and reduce HR's ticket load. I don't really know what this role does day to day. What do we actually need?"

**Expected skill response:**

- **The core problem** = critical process knowledge currently lives only in individual people's heads or scattered documents, which means HR answers the same questions repeatedly and risks losing knowledge entirely when someone leaves
- **What a Knowledge Manager actually does** = interviews subject matter experts to extract undocumented processes, organizes everything into a structured, searchable knowledge base, and sets up governance so it stays accurate over time — not just writing documents, but designing a system
- **Reducing ticket load** = if employees can self-serve answers to common questions (leave policy, benefits enrollment, expense process), HR spends less time on repetitive requests and more time on higher-value work — this is measured through "ticket deflection," not just how much content exists
- **This is a systems and process role, not just a writing role** = strong candidates need interviewing skills, information architecture thinking, and the discipline to set up review cycles, not just clean prose
- This is a **Knowledge Manager** role focused on architecture and governance, distinct from an **HR Documentation Specialist**, who typically executes within a structure someone else has already designed

## Step 2 — Writing the Job Description

**Sample prompt:**

> "Write a JD for a Knowledge Manager at a 250-person company with no existing HR knowledge base. Must be able to interview subject matter experts, build a knowledge base from scratch, and reduce HR ticket volume."

**Sample JD generated:**

```txt
Knowledge Manager — HR Knowledge Base & Self-Service

About us:
[Company] has grown to 250 people without ever building real HR
documentation. Policies live in scattered docs, SOPs live in people's
heads, and HR fields the same questions on repeat. We're hiring our
first Knowledge Manager to fix that.

What you will do:
- Audit existing HR documentation for gaps, duplication, and outdated
  content
- Interview HR team members and subject matter experts to capture
  undocumented processes and institutional knowledge
- Design and build a structured, searchable HR knowledge base from
  the ground up
- Establish content ownership, review cycles, and version control so
  the knowledge base stays accurate over time
- Analyze search behavior and support ticket trends to prioritize
  what to document next
- Partner with HR systems teams to connect knowledge content to
  self-service tools and ticketing

Requirements:
- 3+ years of experience in knowledge management, technical writing,
  or HR documentation, ideally including building a knowledge base
  from scratch
- Strong interviewing skills — able to extract knowledge from people
  who don't think of their own expertise as "documentable"
- Excellent, clear written communication
- Experience with a knowledge base or wiki platform (Confluence,
  Notion, Guru, or similar)
- Comfortable using basic usage or search data to prioritize work

Nice to have:
- Experience reducing support ticket volume through self-service content
- Background partnering with HR systems or IT on chatbot/search tooling
- Experience in a fast-growing company without existing documentation

Compensation: Competitive + benefits

Not a fit if:
- Your experience is limited to writing individual documents within
  an existing, well-structured system
- You're looking for a role focused only on compliance document drafting
- You don't enjoy the "detective work" of extracting knowledge from
  people who've never written it down
```

**Skill note:**

> ⚠️ Do not combine Knowledge Manager + full-time HRBP + Learning & Development Lead into a single role unless the company is genuinely too small to separate them. A JD demanding deep ownership of documentation, employee relations, and training design simultaneously will be ignored by strong knowledge management specialists, who tend to be systems-and-content focused rather than generalist HR practitioners.

## Step 3 — Building the Interview Question Set

**Sample prompt:**

> "Create an interview question set for a Knowledge Manager covering both practical skills and behavioral signals. I need to know what a strong answer sounds like without a content or systems background."

**Generated question set:**

### Practical Questions (for the hiring panel)

| # | Question | What it tests | What a strong answer looks like |
|---|---------|---------------|----------------------------------|
| 1 | "Walk me through how you would build a knowledge base from scratch for a 250-person company with no existing documentation." | Structural and prioritization thinking | Discusses starting with an audit of what already exists, prioritizing by ticket volume or search frequency, and building a category/tagging structure before writing content — not just "start writing everything" |
| 2 | "Describe a time you had to get information out of someone who didn't think their knowledge was worth documenting." | Interviewing and knowledge-extraction skill | Describes specific techniques (asking them to walk through a real recent example, shadowing a task) rather than just "I asked them to write it down" |
| 3 | "How do you decide what content to prioritize first when you can't document everything at once?" | Prioritization and data-driven thinking | Mentions using ticket volume, search gaps, or onboarding pain points to prioritize, not just personal judgment |
| 4 | "How do you keep a knowledge base from going stale six months after launch?" | Governance thinking | Discusses assigning content owners, setting review cadences, and building in a process to catch outdated content, not just "keep it updated" |
| 5 | "How would you measure whether the knowledge base is actually working?" | Outcome orientation | Mentions ticket deflection, search success rate, or reduced repetitive questions to HR — not just "people will use it if it's good" |

### Behavioral Questions (HR can ask directly)

| # | Question | What it tests |
|---|---------|--------------|
| 1 | "Tell me about a documentation or knowledge project that didn't get adopted the way you hoped. What did you learn?" | Ownership and learning from a failed rollout |
| 2 | "How do you handle pushback from a busy team lead who doesn't have time to help you document their process?" | Stakeholder management and persistence |
| 3 | "Describe how you've made a complex or technical process understandable to a non-expert reader." | Writing clarity and audience awareness |
| 4 | "How do you decide what NOT to document, or when existing content should be retired?" | Editorial judgment and content discipline |

## Step 4 — Evaluating a Candidate's Writing Sample or Past Knowledge Base

**Sample prompt:**

> "A candidate shared a writing sample and a link to a knowledge base they built at a previous company. How do I assess whether this shows real knowledge management depth or just decent writing?"

**Evaluation checklist generated by skill:**

### ✅ Strong signals

- [ ] Content is organized with a clear, consistent structure — not just a long list of loosely related articles
- [ ] Articles are scannable (headers, short paragraphs, clear steps), not dense walls of text
- [ ] There's visible evidence of a tagging or categorization system, not just folders named after departments
- [ ] Candidate can explain the reasoning behind their structure, not just describe what it contains
- [ ] Sample includes evidence of governance — version notes, "last reviewed" dates, or ownership tags
- [ ] Candidate can describe how they prioritized what to document first
- [ ] Writing translates a complex process into something a first-time reader could actually follow

### ⚠️ Worth asking about

- Sample is well-written but candidate can't explain how it fits into a broader structure or strategy
- No evidence of ownership, review dates, or update history
- Candidate describes the project as "done" with no plan for ongoing maintenance
- Structure exists but categories feel arbitrary or inconsistent when probed

### ❌ Concerning signals

- Content is a single long document with no real organization or navigation
- Candidate cannot explain how they decided what to include or exclude
- No awareness that documentation needs to be revisited and maintained over time
- Cannot describe any instance of extracting knowledge from a reluctant or busy person

## Step 6 — Understanding a Candidate's Answer Without a Content Background

**Sample prompt:**

> "A candidate said: 'Before writing anything, I pulled six months of HR ticket data, grouped the repeated questions into themes, and built the knowledge base's top-level categories around those themes instead of around our org chart.' Is this a strong answer?"

**Skill explanation:**

- **Pulling ticket data first** = instead of guessing what to document, the candidate used real evidence of what employees actually struggle with — this shows a data-driven, prioritization-first approach rather than starting from assumption
- **Grouping repeated questions into themes** = they looked for patterns across many tickets rather than treating each question as a one-off — this is the "search gap" or "ticket deflection" thinking that makes a knowledge base actually useful
- **Building categories around themes, not the org chart** = this is a meaningful, non-obvious design choice — organizing content the way employees think about their problems ("how do I request leave") rather than the way HR is internally structured ("Total Rewards," "Employee Relations") makes the knowledge base far easier to navigate for the people using it
- **Assessment: Strong signal** — this answer demonstrates genuine information architecture thinking and a user-first mindset, not just an intent to write clearly. It is significantly above the level of someone who would simply transcribe existing policy documents into a new tool.

## Step 7 — Distinguishing Knowledge Management Role Types

**Sample prompt:**

> "Our Head of People mentioned we might need either a 'Knowledge Manager' or an 'HR Documentation Specialist.' What is the difference and how does it change who I look for?"

**Skill explanation:**

| Dimension | HR Documentation Specialist | Knowledge Manager |
|-----------|------------------------------|----------------------|
| Primary focus | Writing and updating individual policies, SOPs, and FAQs | Owning the overall knowledge base architecture and governance |
| Day-to-day | Drafting and editing content within an existing structure | Designing structure, setting governance, prioritizing based on data |
| Output | Clear, accurate individual documents | A coherent, maintained knowledge system across the organization |
| Screening signal | Writing clarity, attention to detail, consistency | Information architecture thinking, interviewing skill, governance design |
| JD keywords | Writing, editing, SOPs, FAQs, style guide | Knowledge base design, governance, ticket deflection, information architecture |
| When to hire | You already have a structure and need it filled in and maintained | You have no structure yet, or your existing one needs to be redesigned |

> If your company already has a reasonably structured knowledge base and mainly needs more content written and kept current, hire an **HR Documentation Specialist**. If you're starting from scratch, or your documentation is scattered and unstructured, hire a **Knowledge Manager** who can design the system first. Hiring a Documentation Specialist for a Knowledge Manager-scope problem is a common reason these projects stall — there's no one deciding how the pieces should fit together.

## Full Hiring Workflow Summary

```txt
Define knowledge management role type clearly (Specialist vs Manager)
                    ↓
Write a focused JD with realistic scope and ticket-reduction goals
                    ↓
CV screening: look for real information architecture experience, not just writing samples
                    ↓
Phone screen: behavioral questions + one prioritization scenario
                    ↓
Practical interview (structure design, extraction skill, governance thinking)
                    ↓
Writing sample or mini exercise: draft one SOP from a messy verbal process
                    ↓
HR debrief using scorecard
                    ↓
Offer / No Offer decision
```

### Common HR Mistakes When Hiring Knowledge Management Talent

| Mistake | How to avoid it |
|---------|----------------|
| Treating strong individual writing samples as proof of knowledge base design skill | Always ask candidates to explain the structure behind a sample, not just the writing itself |
| Confusing HR Documentation Specialist with Knowledge Manager scope | Use the role comparison table — these require different seniority and skill sets |
| Expecting one hire to also own full-time HRBP or L&D responsibilities | Each of these is a distinct focus area — scope the role primarily around knowledge architecture and governance |
| Launching a knowledge base without assigning content owners | Require candidates to explain their governance plan before hiring, not after go-live |
| Not testing interviewing and extraction skill | A Knowledge Manager who can't get information out of a busy subject matter expert will struggle with the hardest part of the job |
| Measuring success only by content volume | Ask how the candidate would measure real impact — ticket deflection and search success, not article count |
| Assuming AI-assisted search will fix a disorganized knowledge base | Strong content structure and governance still matter as much, or more, once AI search tools are layered on top |
