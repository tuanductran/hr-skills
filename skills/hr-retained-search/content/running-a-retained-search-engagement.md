# Running a Retained Search Engagement

## Overview

Retained search differs structurally from contingency recruiting in ways that shape how the engagement should be run: the client commits to a fee and milestone structure upfront, in exchange for dedicated, prioritized effort and typically a confidential, more senior search process. Running a retained engagement well means honoring both sides of that trade — genuine dedicated effort and milestone-based transparency in exchange for the client's upfront commitment.

## Structuring the Client Agreement

A well-structured retained search agreement sets expectations clearly enough to prevent the most common sources of friction later in the engagement:

- **Fee structure and payment milestones** — typically staged across engagement start, slate presentation, and placement — should be explicit, along with what happens if the search is paused or the role requirements change materially mid-search
- **Exclusivity terms** — retained search typically implies the client won't run the same search through other channels simultaneously; this should be stated explicitly rather than assumed
- **Scope boundaries** — what happens if the initial search doesn't produce a placement within the expected timeline, including any replacement guarantee terms, should be defined upfront rather than negotiated after the fact

## Milestone-Based Delivery

Retained engagements are typically structured around defined milestones that create accountability and visibility for the client without requiring daily check-ins:

1. **Search strategy and brief sign-off** — confirming target profile, positioning, and timeline before full sourcing begins
2. **Progress update and market feedback** — an early checkpoint reporting on market response to the search, including any signal that the brief needs recalibration
3. **Slate presentation** — a curated set of qualified candidates presented together, rather than trickled one at a time, which allows the client to evaluate comparatively
4. **Reference and close support** — continued engagement through offer negotiation and close, not just candidate delivery

Missing or skipping the early progress-update milestone is a common cause of client dissatisfaction even when the eventual slate is strong — clients who go long stretches without visibility tend to lose confidence in the process regardless of outcome quality.

## Managing Confidentiality

Many retained searches, particularly for senior or sensitive roles, require discretion that shapes how sourcing is conducted:

- Confirm confidentiality requirements explicitly at the outset — whether the client's identity can be disclosed to candidates before a certain stage, and what level of detail can be shared in early outreach
- Brief candidates on confidentiality expectations early in the process as well, since a confidential search can be compromised in either direction
- Maintain careful internal documentation of who has been approached and what's been disclosed, since confidentiality breaches in senior searches carry real reputational risk for both the search firm or internal TA function and the client

## Building the Candidate Slate

- Present a genuinely comparative slate — typically three to five candidates who each represent a credible, differentiated option — rather than a single strongly preferred candidate with token alternatives included only to satisfy the slate format
- Include a structured comparison alongside the slate (relative strengths, potential concerns, fit considerations) so the client can evaluate efficiently rather than needing to reconstruct the comparison themselves from separate candidate summaries
- Be transparent about market realities reflected in the slate — if the search surfaced fewer strong candidates than expected, that context belongs in the slate presentation, not glossed over

## Managing Timeline and Expectations

- Set realistic milestone timelines at the outset based on genuine market conditions for the target profile, rather than an optimistic timeline offered to win the engagement that the search then struggles to meet
- Communicate proactively when a milestone is at risk, rather than waiting until the missed deadline to raise it — clients generally respond better to early, honest updates than to silence followed by a delay
- Revisit the search brief collaboratively with the client if early market feedback suggests the original criteria are unrealistic, rather than continuing to search against criteria the process has already signaled won't be met

## Common Pitfalls

- **Skipping the early progress-update milestone**, leaving the client without visibility during the search's early, most uncertain phase.
- **Presenting a slate skewed toward one clearly preferred candidate** rather than a genuinely comparative set, undermining the client's ability to evaluate independently.
- **Overpromising timeline at the outset** to win the engagement, creating a credibility problem when the realistic market timeline reasserts itself later.
