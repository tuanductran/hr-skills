# HR Shared Services

## Overview

HR shared services centralizes transactional and administrative HR work — such as payroll processing, benefits administration, and records management — into a single team serving multiple business units, replacing duplicated local HR administration.

## Main topics

### Scope of services

Typical shared services scope includes payroll administration, benefits enrollment support, employee records management, onboarding/offboarding paperwork, and Tier 1 HR inquiries. Strategic HR work (workforce planning, employee relations, organizational design) usually stays with local HRBPs.

### Governance model

A steering committee representing major business units oversees the shared services center, sets priorities, and resolves cross-unit conflicts. Clear SLAs and escalation paths prevent business units from feeling under-served.

### Cost allocation

Shared services costs are typically allocated back to business units via a chargeback model based on headcount, transaction volume, or a flat fee, requiring transparent, auditable cost tracking.

### Centralization candidates

Processes with high volume and low variation across business units (e.g., payroll processing) centralize well. Highly localized or judgment-intensive work (e.g., employee relations investigations) is harder to centralize effectively.

### Multi-country considerations

Cross-border shared services must account for language, time zone coverage, local labor law variation, and data residency requirements, often requiring regional hubs rather than one global center.

## Practical guidance

- Sequence centralization by process maturity and standardization potential, not all at once.
- Build a transparent cost allocation model business units can audit and understand.
- Retain a clear escalation path to specialists for anything outside standard scope.
- Invest heavily in change management communication before go-live, not just after.

## Conclusion

A well-designed HR shared services model reduces administrative cost and improves consistency, but its success depends more on governance, change management, and process readiness than on technology alone.
