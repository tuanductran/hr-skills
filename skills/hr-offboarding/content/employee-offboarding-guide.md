# Employee Offboarding

## Overview

Offboarding is the process of transitioning an employee out of the organization — voluntarily or involuntarily. It is consistently underinvested relative to onboarding, despite being the touchpoint that most directly shapes what departing employees say about the organization to their networks, on Glassdoor, and to future candidates.

## Voluntary Offboarding

### Process essentials

A well-designed voluntary offboarding process covers:

**Resignation acknowledgment and transition planning:** Confirm the notice period, agree on what will be communicated to the team and when, and establish a transition plan. The transition plan should focus on knowledge transfer, not retention (by the time someone resigns, retention efforts have a very low success rate and often create awkwardness).

**Knowledge transfer:** For roles with significant institutional knowledge, structured knowledge transfer is important. Document key processes, contacts, context, and in-flight work. The knowledge transfer plan should be agreed with the manager, not left to the departing employee to define alone.

**Exit interview:** A structured conversation designed to surface honest feedback about the employee's experience and the reasons for departure. Best conducted by someone other than the direct manager. See the hr-reference-checking parallel: exit interviews work better when the interviewee trusts that feedback will not affect references.

**Administrative offboarding:** Return of equipment, revocation of system access, final payroll processing, benefits cessation, COBRA or continuation notices where applicable, documentation of employment for visa or loan purposes.

**Alumni relationship:** For employees leaving on good terms, a deliberate alumni relationship — staying connected, rehire consideration, referral cultivation — has long-term value. Offboarding that treats departure as the end of the relationship leaves value on the table.

### The resignation conversation

When an employee resigns, the manager's first response sets the tone for the rest of the offboarding experience. Best practice:

- Acknowledge the resignation professionally and without visible hurt or anger
- Ask about their plans (with genuine interest, not to interrogate)
- Confirm logistics (last day, transition period, team communication)
- Do not make a counteroffer in the moment without consulting HR — counteroffers have low long-term retention rates and may create equity issues

### When not to make a counteroffer

Counteroffers should not be the automatic response to every resignation. Before making a counter, ask:
- Is this person resigning because of compensation (addressable) or something else (not addressable with money)?
- If we close the compensation gap, is this a person we are committed to retaining long-term, or will we be in the same situation in 6 months?
- What is the impact on internal equity if we increase this person's pay to retain them?

## Involuntary Offboarding

Involuntary separation — layoffs, performance-based terminations — requires more planning and legal care than voluntary offboarding.

### For performance-based terminations

- Documentation of the performance issue and prior communication must be in order before the termination conversation
- HR and legal should review the decision before action is taken
- The termination conversation should be conducted by the direct manager with HR present
- The conversation should be clear, brief, and not leave room for ambiguity about whether the decision is final
- Logistics (final pay, access revocation, equipment return) should be ready before the conversation

### For layoffs

- Legal review is mandatory — layoff processes, required notice periods, and severance obligations vary by jurisdiction and headcount
- In Vietnam: the Labor Code specifies advance notice requirements (45 days for indefinite-term contracts), severance pay obligations, and process requirements for collective redundancies
- The notification conversation and severance package should be delivered simultaneously
- Affected employees should be notified before surviving employees are informed of the change
- After-action communication to remaining employees should happen the same day
