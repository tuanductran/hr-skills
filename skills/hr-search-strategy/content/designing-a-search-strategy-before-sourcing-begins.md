# Designing a Search Strategy Before Sourcing Begins

## Overview

Sourcing that starts before the search is strategically planned tends to produce a scattered, inconsistent candidate pool and a much longer time-to-fill than a search that invests upfront in defining target companies, channels, and positioning. The search brief is the artifact that forces this planning to happen deliberately, rather than being figured out reactively as sourcing proceeds.

## Building the Search Brief

A complete search brief, agreed with the hiring manager before sourcing begins, should cover:

- **The precise target profile** — role scope, required capability, and genuine must-have versus nice-to-have criteria, distinguished as clearly as in job description drafting
- **Target company sources**, informed by market mapping, ranked by expected fit and addressability
- **Positioning** — the specific narrative that will make this opportunity compelling to the target candidate profile, tailored to what actually motivates people in that market segment rather than a generic pitch
- **Timeline and channel plan** — which channels will be prioritized in what sequence, and the expected cadence for pipeline reviews with the hiring manager

## Channel Prioritization

Different searches warrant different channel mixes, and the strategy should name this explicitly rather than defaulting to whatever channel was used last time:

| Search type | Primary channel emphasis |
|---|---|
| High-volume, moderate seniority | Inbound optimization, job board and programmatic advertising |
| Specialized or scarce skill | Targeted outbound sourcing against market-mapped companies |
| Senior or executive | Retained search relationships, confidential outbound, referral networks |
| Urgent, time-sensitive | Agency partnership alongside internal sourcing to parallelize effort |

Naming the channel priority upfront, and the rationale behind it, prevents the common pattern of a search drifting toward whichever channel is most familiar to the individual recruiter rather than what's actually best suited to this specific search.

## Positioning the Opportunity

Positioning is the search strategy element most often skipped, yet it directly shapes both response rate and candidate quality:

- Identify what genuinely differentiates this opportunity for the target candidate profile — compensation, scope, mission, technical challenge, career trajectory — rather than a generic list of company benefits that applies to any role
- Test positioning early against a small number of initial outreach attempts and adjust based on actual response quality, rather than committing to one narrative for the full search without validation
- Align positioning with what the hiring manager can credibly deliver — overselling scope or growth trajectory during search to secure interest creates the same expectation-mismatch risk covered in offer and onboarding stages

## Setting Realistic Timelines

- Base timeline expectations on the actual addressable market size (from market mapping) and search type, rather than a standard company-wide time-to-fill target applied uniformly regardless of role scarcity
- Build in checkpoints for reassessing strategy if initial sourcing isn't producing the expected pipeline quality within the first stretch of the search, rather than continuing an underperforming approach for the full planned duration before adjusting
- Communicate realistic timeline expectations to the hiring manager upfront, especially for scarce-skill searches, since misaligned expectations at the outset are a common source of hiring manager frustration later

## Reviewing and Adjusting Strategy Mid-Search

A search strategy set at the outset shouldn't be treated as fixed for the full duration:

- Review pipeline quality and channel performance at defined checkpoints, not only when the search stalls visibly
- Be willing to revise the target profile itself if early sourcing consistently surfaces a pattern suggesting the original criteria were miscalibrated, rather than continuing to source against criteria the market can't support
- Document what's learned during the search — which channels and positioning actually worked — for reuse in future similar searches, rather than starting the strategic planning from scratch each time

## Common Pitfalls

- **Starting sourcing before the brief is agreed**, leading to a scattered pipeline that doesn't match the hiring manager's actual expectations.
- **Using the same channel mix and positioning for every search** regardless of role type, missing the efficiency gained from a deliberately tailored strategy.
- **Treating the initial strategy as fixed** rather than reviewing and adjusting it against actual early results.
