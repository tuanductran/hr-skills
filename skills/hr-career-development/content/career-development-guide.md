# Career Development

## Overview

Career development is how employees grow their skills, expand their scope, and advance their careers — and how the organization enables and supports that growth. It is one of the top drivers of employee retention and engagement, and one of the areas most often cited by employees who leave as "something I couldn't get here."

The gap between what organizations say about career development ("we invest in our people") and what employees experience ("there is no path forward here") is one of the most persistent in HR. Closing it requires concrete frameworks, deliberate manager enablement, and organizational accountability.

## Career Frameworks

A career framework is the structured definition of roles, levels, and advancement criteria that makes career paths visible and navigable for employees.

### Why career frameworks matter

Without a career framework:
- Employees cannot understand what they would need to demonstrate for advancement
- Promotion decisions are perceived (and often are) inconsistent and political
- Compensation is managed without a coherent structure
- Development conversations lack a shared vocabulary

With a clear framework:
- Employees know what "the next level" looks like and can work toward it deliberately
- Managers have consistent criteria for assessing readiness for advancement
- Promotion decisions are defensible and perceived as fair
- Compensation ranges are anchored to defined levels

### Career framework components

**Career tracks:** Most professional roles have at least two tracks — management (leading people and teams) and individual contributor (deepening expertise). Not all employees want to manage people; a framework that requires management for advancement loses strong technical contributors.

**Levels:** A defined set of levels with scope-based criteria. Four to six levels is typically sufficient. More than six creates grade inflation and level-splitting debates. Fewer than four compresses meaningful differences in scope and impact.

**Level criteria:** For each level, what does the work look like, what is the expected impact, and what are the behavioral indicators? Criteria should be specific enough that a manager and an employee looking at the same performance evidence would agree on level.

**Title map:** Which titles correspond to which levels. Titles carry market meaning; align them to external benchmarks where possible.

### Building the framework

Framework design should involve managers who evaluate performance at each level, employees who have been at various levels, and HR/compensation to ensure alignment with market data. Frameworks designed solely by HR leadership without practitioner input typically miss the nuances of what distinguishes levels in practice.

## Development Planning

An individual development plan (IDP) translates career framework aspirations into a 6-12 month action plan. Well-executed IDPs create accountability for growth; poorly executed IDPs are forms employees fill out and file.

### Elements of a useful IDP

**Career goal:** Where does the employee want to be in 1-3 years? This should be specific enough to plan toward, not "I want to grow."

**Strengths to leverage:** What is the employee particularly good at? Development plans that ignore existing strengths are less effective than ones that build from them.

**Development priorities:** The 1-3 areas of skill or experience the employee is developing. Not a comprehensive list of every gap — prioritized focus areas.

**Development activities:** Specific actions for each development priority. The 70-20-10 framework is a useful structure: 70% of development comes from on-the-job experiences, 20% from relationships (mentoring, coaching, feedback), and 10% from formal learning (training, coursework). Development plans that rely entirely on the 10% (training) underperform those that deliberately design the 70% (stretch assignments, project leadership, cross-functional work).

**Timeline and milestones:** When will specific development activities happen? Development plans without timelines are intentions, not plans.

**Manager commitments:** What will the manager do to support the plan? Development is not the employee's responsibility alone.

## Career Conversations

The development conversation between an employee and their manager is the highest-leverage development activity in most organizations. It is also frequently avoided — because managers are uncomfortable discussing aspirations they cannot guarantee, or because the conversation has never been normalized as an expected part of management.

### Making career conversations a norm

Career conversations should be an explicit agenda item in regular 1:1 meetings — not only at annual performance reviews. The expectation should be set at the organizational level: managers are expected to have meaningful career conversations with each direct report at least quarterly.

### Conversation structure

A career conversation is not a performance conversation. It is focused on the future, not the recent past.

**Opening question:** "Where do you want your career to go? What kinds of work energize you most?" Let the employee lead — this is about their goals, not the manager's assessment.

**Strengths discussion:** "What do you think you're particularly good at? What have you gotten positive feedback on that feels accurate?"

**Development priorities:** "What do you want to develop in the next year? What experiences or skills feel like the next important step for you?"

**Connection to opportunity:** "Given what you're working toward, here's what I see coming up that might be relevant..." Managers who connect employees' development goals to actual opportunities are the most effective development partners.

**Commitments:** "What will you work on, and what can I do to support that?" Concrete next steps from both sides.
