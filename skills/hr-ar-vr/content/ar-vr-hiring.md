# AR/VR Hiring

## Overview

AR/VR (and broader spatial computing) hiring spans engineering, 3D/UX design, and performance optimization disciplines unique to headset and spatial hardware constraints, in a field that evolves quickly as platforms and tooling mature.

## Main topics

### Role types

AR/VR engineers build interactive spatial experiences and handle platform SDK integration; spatial UX designers focus on interaction design, comfort, and accessibility unique to headset-based interfaces; 3D artists create optimized assets for real-time rendering under tight performance budgets.

### Performance constraints

Headset hardware imposes much tighter rendering performance budgets than typical screens, since frame-rate drops can directly cause user discomfort; strong candidates demonstrate real experience optimizing under these constraints.

### Comfort and accessibility design

Motion sickness, disorientation, and physical strain are spatial-computing-specific UX concerns; strong spatial UX designers explicitly account for these in their design decisions, which is a distinguishing, often underweighted evaluation criterion.

### Portfolio review

Because AR/VR work is inherently experiential, reviewing actual shipped or demo-able experiences (rather than static screenshots) gives far more signal on a candidate's real capability.

## Practical guidance

- Prioritize hands-on or recorded demo review over static portfolio images.
- Screen explicitly for performance-optimization experience given tight hardware constraints.
- Ask about comfort/accessibility design decisions as a distinct evaluation criterion.
- Keep platform requirements in job postings current given how quickly AR/VR tooling evolves.

## Conclusion

Effective AR/VR hiring blends genuine hands-on portfolio review, explicit evaluation of performance-optimization and comfort-design skills, and up-to-date platform requirements suited to this fast-evolving field.
